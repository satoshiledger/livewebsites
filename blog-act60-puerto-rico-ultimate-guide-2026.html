The Ultimate Guide to Act 60 Puerto Rico: Everything You Need to Know in 2026 La Guía Definitiva de la Ley 60 de Puerto Rico: Todo Lo Que Necesita Saber en 2026

Share this article: Compartir este articulo:

Puerto Rico's Act 60 has become one of the most powerful tax incentive programs available to U.S. citizens and investors worldwide. In 2026, with recent legislative reforms and the program's extension to 2055, understanding Act 60 is more critical than ever for high-net-worth individuals, business owners, and investors seeking legitimate tax optimization strategies.

This comprehensive guide breaks down everything you need to know about Act 60-2019 (Puerto Rico Incentives Code), the 2025 reforms that take effect January 1, 2026, the federal tax treatment under IRC Section 933, and the exact compliance requirements to legally reduce your tax burden while living in the Caribbean.

Critical 2026 Deadline

Applications submitted on or before December 31, 2025 will be grandfathered under the current 0% tax rate on passive income. Starting January 1, 2026, new applicants will face a 4% tax on capital gains, interest, and dividends. The application submission date determines your rate—not your move date or decree approval date.

What Is Act 60-2019?

Act 60-2019, officially titled the "Puerto Rico Incentives Code," was signed into law on July 1, 2019. It consolidated multiple existing tax incentive programs—most notably Act 20 (Export Services) and Act 22 (Individual Investors)—into a single comprehensive framework designed to attract businesses and investors to Puerto Rico.

The law is divided into six subtitles, with Subtitle B containing the primary tax incentives organized by chapter. The most popular provisions include:

Chapter 2

Individual Resident Investors

Formerly Act 22. Provides tax exemptions on dividends, interest, and capital gains for bona fide Puerto Rico residents.

Chapter 3

Export Services & Commerce

Formerly Act 20. Offers 4% corporate tax rate for eligible export service businesses with operations in Puerto Rico.

According to Section 2031.01 of Act 60, the law creates "a simple, streamlined, and efficient process" to promote Puerto Rico's economic competitiveness through tax incentives that generate measurable return on investment for the Puerto Rico Treasury.

The 2025 Act 60 Reform: What Changed on January 1, 2026

On June 25, 2025, the Puerto Rico Senate passed legislation extending and revising Act 60. Governor Jenniffer González-Colón signed the bill into law in 2025. These reforms represent the most significant changes to Puerto Rico's tax incentive framework since Act 60's original enactment.

Key Changes Effective January 1, 2026

1. New 4% Tax on Passive Income for New Applicants

Starting January 1, 2026, individuals who apply for an Act 60 Individual Resident Investor decree will be subject to a 4% fixed income tax rate on:

  • Capital gains (long-term and short-term)
  • Interest income
  • Dividend income

This replaces the previous 0% rate for these income categories. However, existing decree holders are fully grandfathered—their 0% rate remains intact for the life of their decree.

2. Program Extended Through December 31, 2055

The Act 60 Individual Investor program was originally set to expire on December 31, 2035. The 2025 reform extends the program validity through December 31, 2055—providing an additional 20 years of certainty for investors and families planning generational wealth strategies in Puerto Rico.

3. New Six-Year Prior Residency Requirement

New applicants must certify that they have not been bona fide residents of Puerto Rico for the six years immediately preceding their decree application. This provision targets the incentive toward genuinely new arrivals and prevents former residents from re-entering the program after brief departures.

Grandfathering Deadline: December 31, 2025

The date that matters is the application submission date—not when you move to Puerto Rico or when your decree is approved. Applications submitted through the Department of Economic Development and Commerce (DDEC) Single Business Portal on or before December 31, 2025 will lock in the 0% rate.

This means if you submit your application on December 30, 2025 but don't move until March 2026, you still qualify for the 0% rate for the duration of your decree (typically valid until December 31, 2035 or until 2055 under renewal provisions).

IRC Section 933: The Federal Foundation of Act 60

The power of Act 60 stems from IRC Section 933 of the U.S. Internal Revenue Code, which states:

"In the case of an individual who is a bona fide resident of Puerto Rico during the entire taxable year, income derived from sources within Puerto Rico (except amounts received for services performed as an employee of the United States or any agency thereof)... shall not be included in gross income and shall be exempt from taxation under this subtitle."

— 26 U.S. Code § 933(1)

This means that Puerto Rico-source income earned by bona fide Puerto Rico residents is excluded from U.S. federal gross income. Combined with Act 60's Puerto Rico tax exemptions, qualifying income can effectively be taxed at 0% (pre-2026 decrees) or 4% (post-2026 decrees).

How Income Sourcing Works Under IRC § 937

Puerto Rico-Source Income

  • Wages for services performed in Puerto Rico
  • Capital gains on property sold while PR resident (acquired after residency)
  • Interest from Puerto Rico banks
  • Dividends from PR corporations (with >80% PR-source income)

U.S.-Source Income (Still Taxable)

  • Wages for services performed in the U.S.
  • Interest from U.S. banks or bonds
  • Dividends from U.S. corporations
  • Rental income from U.S. properties

Critical Planning Point

Bona fide Puerto Rico residents must still file U.S. federal tax returns (Form 1040) reporting all U.S.-source and foreign-source income. Puerto Rico-source income is excluded via Form 8898. Self-employment tax and FICA taxes still apply to wages and self-employment income regardless of sourcing.

Bona Fide Residency: The Three-Part Test

To qualify for Act 60 benefits and IRC Section 933 exclusion, you must become a bona fide resident of Puerto Rico under IRC Section 937. This is a strict three-part test enforced by both the IRS and the Puerto Rico Department of the Treasury (Hacienda).

Test #1: Presence Test

You must be physically present in Puerto Rico for at least 183 days during the taxable year.

Alternative safe harbor: You meet the presence test if:

  • You are present in Puerto Rico for at least 183 days, OR
  • You are present in Puerto Rico for at least 549 days during the current and prior 2 tax years (including at least 60 days in each year), OR
  • You are present in the U.S. for no more than 90 days during the tax year, you have no more than $3,000 of U.S.-source income, and you have no significant connection to the U.S.

Keep meticulous records: boarding passes, flight itineraries, hotel receipts, and credit card statements showing dates in Puerto Rico.

Test #2: Tax Home Test

Your "tax home" (principal place of business or employment) must be in Puerto Rico during the taxable year. You cannot have a tax home outside Puerto Rico.

For investors and business owners, this means your primary income-generating activities must be managed from Puerto Rico. Remote work for a U.S. employer while living in Puerto Rico can jeopardize this test if the work is performed in the U.S.

Test #3: Closer Connection Test

You must not have a closer connection to the United States or a foreign country than to Puerto Rico. The IRS examines:

  • Location of your permanent home and family
  • Location of personal belongings and vehicles
  • Jurisdiction where you vote and hold a driver's license
  • Social, religious, and community ties
  • Location of professional advisors (doctors, lawyers, accountants)
  • Location of your banking and financial accounts

IRS Enforcement Campaign

In 2021, the IRS announced a formal enforcement campaign targeting Act 60 participants who fail to meet bona fide residency requirements. In December 2025, the U.S. Government Accountability Office (GAO) recommended that the IRS establish procedures to regularly obtain data on all taxpayers claiming Puerto Rico's resident investor incentive.

The IRS has made clear: improper claims will result in back taxes, penalties, and interest. Document everything.

Act 60 Compliance Requirements

Obtaining a tax exemption decree is just the beginning. Act 60 decree holders face ongoing annual compliance obligations. Failure to meet these requirements can result in decree revocation and loss of all tax benefits.

For Individual Resident Investors (Chapter 2)

Annual Requirements

  • Charitable Donation: $10,000 minimum annually to Puerto Rico nonprofits. At least $5,000 must go to organizations focused on alleviating child poverty (certified by DDEC).
  • Annual Report: Submit decree compliance report to DDEC by November 15 each year, certifying all requirements have been met.
  • Property Purchase: Purchase residential property in Puerto Rico within two years of receiving the decree for use as primary residence.

Tax Filing Obligations

  • U.S. Federal Return: File Form 1040 annually reporting all U.S.-source and foreign-source income. Exclude Puerto Rico-source income via Form 8898.
  • Puerto Rico Return: File Puerto Rico tax return (Form 482) reporting all worldwide income. Claim exemptions under your Act 60 decree.
  • FBAR & FATCA: Report foreign financial accounts if balances exceed thresholds ($10,000 for FBAR; $50,000-$600,000 for FATCA).

Compliance Best Practices

  • Maintain a compliance calendar with key dates (November 15 annual report, April 15 tax returns, December 31 charitable donations)
  • Keep detailed travel logs with boarding passes, hotel receipts, and credit card statements for every trip
  • Engage a Puerto Rico CPA familiar with Act 60 compliance to prepare your annual reports and tax returns
  • Work with a Puerto Rico attorney to review contracts and ensure proper income sourcing
  • Document your closer connection to Puerto Rico with photos, utility bills, membership cards, and community involvement records

All Act 60 decree applications are submitted through the Puerto Rico Department of Economic Development and Commerce (DDEC) via the Single Business Portal (SBP) online system.

  • Completed application form

Processing times vary from 3-6 months. DDEC may request additional documentation or clarifications.

Once approved, you receive a formal decree document detailing your specific tax rates and obligations. This decree is a binding contract with the Government of Puerto Rico, guaranteed for the term of your decree regardless of future law changes.

Five Common Act 60 Mistakes That Trigger IRS Audits

Mistake #1: Failing the 183-Day Test

Many decree holders underestimate how difficult it is to spend 183 days per year in Puerto Rico. The IRS will request detailed proof. Missing the presence test by even one day can disqualify you for the entire tax year.

Mistake #2: Improper Income Sourcing

Claiming U.S.-source income as Puerto Rico-source is tax fraud. If you provide consulting services to a U.S. client while physically in New York, that income is U.S.-source—even if you're a Puerto Rico resident. Services are sourced where performed, not where you reside.

Mistake #3: Pre-Move Appreciation

Gains on assets acquired before becoming a Puerto Rico resident are subject to special 10-year lookback rules under Treas. Reg. § 1.937-2(f). Pre-move appreciation remains U.S.-taxable even if you sell as a Puerto Rico resident. Only post-residency appreciation qualifies for Act 60 benefits.

Mistake #4: Maintaining Closer U.S. Ties

Keeping your family, primary home, country club membership, and financial advisors in Florida while claiming Puerto Rico residency is a red flag. The IRS examines where your true center of life is located. Half-hearted relocations fail the closer connection test.

Mistake #5: Missing Compliance Deadlines

Failing to file your annual DDEC compliance report by November 15, missing the $10,000 charitable donation, or not purchasing property within two years can result in decree suspension or revocation. The Puerto Rico government is increasing enforcement and coordination with the IRS.

Is Act 60 Right for You?

Act 60 is not a "loophole" or a simple tax avoidance scheme. It's a legitimate economic development program requiring genuine relocation and full compliance with complex U.S. and Puerto Rico tax laws.

Act 60 Makes Sense If You:

Good Fit

  • Have substantial capital gains, dividends, or interest income (>$200K annually)
  • Are genuinely willing to relocate and live in Puerto Rico full-time
  • Can spend 183+ days per year on the island
  • Have mobile income (investments, business ownership, consulting)
  • Can meet all annual compliance requirements
  • Are comfortable with the tropical island lifestyle

Poor Fit

  • Primarily earn W-2 wages from a U.S. employer (limited benefits)
  • Want to maintain primary residence in the U.S.
  • Have significant U.S.-source income that can't be restructured
  • Aren't willing to spend 6+ months per year in Puerto Rico
  • Don't have professional advisors to ensure compliance
  • View this as a "paper" relocation only

The Bottom Line on 2026

Even with the new 4% rate for post-2026 applicants, Act 60 remains one of the most attractive tax incentive programs available to U.S. citizens. A 4% tax on investment income is dramatically lower than the 20-37% federal rates plus state taxes most investors face on the mainland.

But if you're considering Act 60, the window to lock in the 0% rate closes on December 31, 2025. For high-net-worth investors with substantial passive income, that deadline could represent millions of dollars in lifetime tax savings.
