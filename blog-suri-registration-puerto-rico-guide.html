SURI Registration in Puerto Rico: How to Register Your Business for Taxes Step by Step Registro SURI en Puerto Rico: Cómo Registrar Su Negocio para Impuestos Paso a Paso

Share this article:Compartir este articulo:

If you're starting a business in Puerto Rico — whether you're a new Act 60 decree holder, a mainland entrepreneur relocating, or a local resident launching a venture — one of the first things you'll need to do is register with SURI, Puerto Rico's tax administration platform. Without it, you cannot legally operate, collect sales tax, or file your returns.

SURI stands for Sistema Unificado de Rentas Internas (Unified Internal Revenue System). Launched in 2016 by Puerto Rico's Department of Treasury (Hacienda), it is the mandatory digital platform for virtually all tax interactions between businesses and the government. Through SURI, you file returns, make payments, obtain certificates, and manage your tax accounts — entirely online (LegalClarity, Dec. 2025; Piloto 151).

Penalty Warning

Every business operating in Puerto Rico must register with Hacienda at least 30 days before starting operations. The penalty for failure to register is $10,000. An additional $20,000 penalty may be imposed for failure to register with the Fiscal Terminals Program. These are not idle threats — Hacienda enforces them (Grant Thornton; PWC Puerto Rico).

What Is SURI and Why Does Every PR Business Need It?

SURI is Hacienda's centralized online portal for all tax-related activity. Before SURI, businesses had to navigate multiple disconnected systems. Today, SURI consolidates everything into a single platform accessible at suri.hacienda.pr.gov. The portal can be used in both English and Spanish — switch the language using the link in the upper-right corner (Piloto 151).

Through SURI, your business can file income tax returns, submit monthly Sales and Use Tax (IVU/SUT) returns, file informative returns (Forms 480.6A, 480.6B, 480.7 series), make tax payments, request extensions, obtain your Merchant's Registration Certificate, manage payroll withholding accounts, declare goods imported into Puerto Rico, request Tax Good Standing Certificates (Certificado de Deuda), and receive official notices from Hacienda (LegalClarity, Dec. 2025; PRelocate).

For Act 60 Decree Holders

SURI is also where Act 60 holders manage their charitable donation requirements and file declarations related to exempt income. Your DDEC compliance and your Hacienda compliance are separate — SURI handles the Hacienda side. You still need to file your DDEC Exempt Annual Report separately through the DDEC Portal of Incentives (incentivos.ddec.pr.gov).

Before You Register: Prerequisites You'll Need

Before you can create your SURI account and apply for your Merchant's Registration Certificate, you need several things in place. Missing any of these will delay your registration.

1. Business Entity Formed with the Department of State

Your LLC or corporation must be registered with the PR Department of State before you can register for taxes. For LLCs, file the Certificate of Formation ($250 filing fee). This can be done online and processed in as little as 1 business day ($100 expedited) or 2 hours ($500 rush). See our guide: How to Start a Business in Puerto Rico.

2. Federal Employer Identification Number (EIN)

Apply for your EIN through the IRS website (Form SS-4) or the online application at irs.gov. This is free and typically issued immediately when applying online during IRS business hours. Your EIN is required for all SURI registrations (MyShyft; Multiplier).

3. Physical Business Address in Puerto Rico

You need a real physical address for your business in PR — a lease agreement, property deed, or similar documentation proving your presence. Virtual mailboxes alone typically do not satisfy this requirement for merchant registration. For Act 60 Chapter 3, you need a bona fide office (MyShyft; Northwest Registered Agent).

4. Valid Identification for Owners/Officers

Government-issued ID for all owners, officers, or authorized representatives. You'll also need the Social Security Number or Individual Taxpayer Identification Number of each owner (MyShyft).

5. NAICS Code

Know your North American Industry Classification System (NAICS) code — the standardized code for your business activity. This will be required on the merchant registration application (Form AS 2914.1). You can look up your code at census.gov/naics (Hacienda Form AS 2914.1).

SURI Registration: Step-by-Step Process

1

Create Your SURI User Account

Go to suri.hacienda.pr.gov. In the Registration section, click "Create SURI Logon." You have two options: "Filing Returns for the First Time" (if you've never registered with Hacienda) or "I Have Filed a Return Previously" (if you have an existing history). Select the appropriate option (PRelocate; PR Business Law Firm).

Create a username and password. Provide your contact details. The system will send a verification code via email or SMS to authenticate your identity. Your first login will establish you as the Principal Administrator of the account — this person has unlimited access and can delegate authority to others later (McConnell Valdés; LegalClarity, Dec. 2025).

2

Choose Your Taxpayer Account Type

Select the kind of taxpayer account you need: Individual Taxpayer, Business Account, or Administrator of an Estate. For most businesses, select Business Account. Enter your entity information, including your EIN, legal business name (exactly as registered with the Department of State), entity type (LLC, corporation, etc.), and business address (PRelocate; Piloto 151).

3

Apply for Your Merchant's Registration Certificate

Once your SURI account is active, apply for the Merchant's Registration Certificate (Certificado de Registro de Comerciante). This is the single most important registration — it's your authorization to do business in Puerto Rico and confirms your obligation to collect and remit the Sales and Use Tax (IVU) (PWC Puerto Rico; Saavedra CPA).

The application process requires you to provide your business location details (you need a separate certificate for each physical location), your NAICS code, the nature of your principal business activity, whether you sell tangible personal property, and your expected volume of business. Each location gets a unique registry number (Hacienda Form AS 2914.1; Saavedra CPA).

Processing time: Typically 7–10 business days, but can vary based on application volume and completeness. Once issued, your Merchant's Registration Certificate must be prominently displayed at your business location at all times. The certificate is renewed every 2 years (MyShyft; Saavedra CPA).

4

Set Up Your Tax Accounts in SURI

Once registered, link or create the tax accounts relevant to your business. Common tax accounts include:

Sales and Use Tax (IVU/SUT): Created automatically with your merchant registration. Used for monthly SUT returns.

Salary Withholdings (Section 1062.01): Required if you have employees. Used to withhold and remit PR income tax from employee wages.

Non-Resident Withholdings (Section 1062.08/1062.11): Required if you make payments to non-residents of PR that are subject to withholding.

Income Tax: For filing your annual PR income tax return (Form 482 for individuals, Form 480.20 for corporations).

Import Declarations: If you import tangible goods into PR, you must declare them via SURI (McConnell Valdés; Piloto 151).

5

Configure Access Levels

SURI uses a hierarchical access system. The Principal Administrator (PA) has full access and can authorize others. You can add a Secondary Administrator (who gets whatever access the PA delegates), or standard users with limited permissions. If you work with a CPA or tax representative, they must be registered in SURI and authorized by you to access your accounts (McConnell Valdés; Hacienda Publication 25-01).

Understanding the IVU: Puerto Rico's Sales and Use Tax

Once registered, your primary ongoing obligation through SURI will be collecting and remitting Puerto Rico's Sales and Use Tax — known locally as the IVU (Impuesto sobre Ventas y Uso). Here's what you need to know:

IVU Component Rate Recipient
State portion (tangible property & most services) 10.5% Commonwealth of Puerto Rico
Municipal portion 1% Municipality where sale occurred
Combined general rate 11.5% Split between state and municipality
B2B services and designated professional services 4% Separate rate under PR Code

The IVU applies to the sale of tangible personal property, taxable services (including SaaS, maintenance, repair, and certain professional services), admissions, and digital products. Key exemptions include prescription drugs, most food and food ingredients (except prepared food), items purchased for resale (with an exemption certificate), and services rendered by merchants with annual volume under $50,000 for taxable services (DAVO; Sabalier Law; Kintsugi; PWC Puerto Rico).

Invoice Requirements

Every receipt or invoice must show the IVU as a separate line item, with the state portion (10.5%) and municipal portion (1%) displayed separately. This segregation is mandatory. Merchants using the fiscal terminal program must issue receipts through the registered device (PWC Puerto Rico; Grant Thornton).

Monthly IVU Filing: What, When, and How

Once you begin collecting IVU, you must file monthly returns through SURI. Here's the filing cadence:

State SUT Return

Due by the 20th day of the following month (e.g., January sales are reported and paid by February 20th). Filed through SURI. All returns must be submitted electronically — Hacienda does not accept paper SUT returns (PWC Puerto Rico; CPA Puerto Rico).

Municipal SUT Return

The 1% municipal portion is filed through COFIM (the municipal filing system) or through SURI, depending on your municipality. Check with your specific municipality's requirements (MyShyft).

Import Declarations

If you import tangible goods into PR, you must file a monthly use tax on imports return by the 20th day of the following month. Goods are subject to use tax at the port, and the tax must be satisfied before the goods are released — unless you are a bonded merchant or eligible reseller (PWC Puerto Rico; Piloto 151).

Early Payment Discount

If you submit your IVU payment by the 15th of the month (5 days early), you can receive a credit of 1.95% on the first $6,250 of IVU due, and 1% on any amount above that, up to a maximum discount of $500 per month. This adds up — if you qualify every month, that's up to $6,000 per year in savings (DAVO).

Beyond SURI: Other Tax Registrations Your Business Needs

SURI handles your relationship with Hacienda, but Puerto Rico businesses have additional registration requirements with other agencies:

Patente Municipal (Municipal License Tax)

Every business operating in Puerto Rico must register with the municipality where it operates within 30 days of starting operations. The Patente Municipal is a gross receipts tax ranging from 0.2% to 0.5% of gross revenue for non-financial businesses, paid semi-annually (July 15 and January 15), with the annual declaration due around April 15. To register, bring your Certificate of Incorporation, lease agreement, and Use Permit to the Municipal Director of Finance (Ferraiuoli; Northwest Registered Agent; Rivero Consulting).

CRIM Registration (Property Taxes)

Register with the Municipal Revenue Collection Center (CRIM) for property tax purposes. This applies to both real property and personal business property (equipment, furniture, inventory). Tax rates range from 5.80% to 9.83% depending on the municipality. You must file an annual personal property tax declaration, even if you're renting your business location (PWC Puerto Rico; MyShyft).

Department of Labor Registration

If you hire employees, register with the PR Department of Labor and Human Resources for unemployment insurance and disability insurance. You'll also need to comply with workers' compensation requirements (Multiplier).

Single Use Permit (Permiso Único)

Before applying for your Patente Municipal, you may need a Single Use Permit from the Puerto Rico Permits & Endorsements Management Office (OGPE). This combines your Use Permit, Fire Prevention Certificate, Sanitary License, and Categorical Exclusion into one document. Apply online through the Single Business Portal (Northwest Registered Agent).

Critical Note: PR LLC Tax Classification

Important Difference from Mainland

Puerto Rico LLCs default to corporate tax treatment under both the PR Internal Revenue Code and the Federal Internal Revenue Code. This is different from the mainland, where single-member LLCs default to disregarded entity status and multi-member LLCs default to partnership treatment. If you want your PR LLC to be treated as a pass-through entity (partnership or disregarded entity), you must affirmatively elect that status by filing IRS Form 8832 (Entity Classification Election) for federal purposes and a separate election under the PR Code for PR purposes (Ferraiuoli; 14 LPRA 3952).

This election has major implications for your Act 60 structure. Chapter 3 (Export Services) decree income typically flows through a corporate entity taxed at 4%. Chapter 2 (Individual Investor) benefits apply to the individual's personal investment income. Choosing the wrong entity classification can undermine your entire tax strategy. Work with a PR CPA and tax attorney to make this election correctly before you begin operations.

SURI Compliance Penalties: What Happens If You Don't Register or File

Puerto Rico takes merchant registration and IVU compliance seriously. Here are the penalties you face for non-compliance:

Failure to register as merchant$10,000
Failure to register with Fiscal Terminals Program$20,000
Providing false registration information$5,000
Selling/transferring merchant registration certificate$5,000
Displaying falsified certificate$5,000 per violation
Failure to display certificate$1,000

Beyond these financial penalties, Hacienda has the authority to place liens on business assets, seize property, and hold business owners personally liable for uncollected IVU. In extreme cases, criminal charges may apply. Maintaining timely registration and monthly filing through SURI is not optional — it's a core obligation of doing business in Puerto Rico (Grant Thornton; DAVO; Kintsugi).

SURI Tips and Common Pitfalls

Enter your name exactly as it appears in the SURI system

When registering or filing, use your legal name exactly as it appears in previous filings or in the SURI database. Mismatches will cause errors and delays (PRelocate; Piloto 151).

Use a reliable internet connection

SURI sessions can time out, and interrupted submissions may need to be restarted. Complete your registration in one sitting with a stable connection (PR Business Law Firm).

File even if you have no sales

Even if you had zero taxable sales in a given month, you must still file a monthly SUT return reporting those zero sales. Failing to file a return — even a zero return — triggers the late filing penalty (CPA Puerto Rico; Grant Thornton).

Keep records for 10 years

Hacienda requires you to retain copies of your filings data — or be able to reconstruct the data — for at least 10 years after the due date of the return. Maintain organized digital records (Hacienda Publication 25-01).

Use Hacienda's free training resources

Hacienda offers free training sessions and resources for navigating SURI. You can also visit one of Hacienda's 360 Service Centers in person for assistance. The SURI platform includes help documentation and video tutorials (MyShyft; McConnell Valdés).

Don't forget use tax on online purchases

If your business purchases goods online from vendors outside PR who don't collect IVU, you are responsible for self-assessing and remitting the use tax on those items. This catches many new businesses off guard. Equipment, supplies, and inventory purchased from non-PR vendors are subject to use tax (Sabalier Law).
