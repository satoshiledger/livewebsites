Bitcoin Tax in Puerto Rico 2026: The Complete Guide for Holders, Traders, and Miners Impuestos de Bitcoin en Puerto Rico 2026: Guía Completa para Holders, Traders y Mineros

Share this article: Compartir este articulo:

Bitcoin hit new all-time highs in 2025, and with those gains came a wave of tax liability for investors across the U.S. mainland. At the top federal bracket, long-term capital gains on Bitcoin are taxed at 20% — plus the 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax — before state-level taxes are even considered. For high-volume traders and long-term holders sitting on significant unrealized appreciation, the tax bill can be staggering.

Puerto Rico offers a legal alternative. Under Act 60's Chapter 2 (Individual Resident Investors), bona fide Puerto Rico residents who obtained their decree before 2026 can pay 0% tax on Bitcoin capital gains accrued after establishing residency. Post-2025 applicants pay just 4%. Under Chapter 3 (Export Services), Bitcoin-related businesses can operate at a 4% corporate tax rate. But the rules are precise, the IRS is watching closely, and mistakes can be expensive.

This guide covers every angle of Bitcoin taxation in Puerto Rico for 2026: how capital gains work for holders, how trading income is treated, how mining and staking are taxed, what happens with Bitcoin ETFs, how the 5% toll tax applies to pre-move gains, and what you need to do to stay compliant with both Hacienda and the IRS.

Critical Distinction

Only Bitcoin acquired after you establish bona fide Puerto Rico residency qualifies for Act 60's favorable capital gains treatment. Bitcoin purchased before your move remains subject to U.S. federal capital gains tax on any appreciation that accrued while you were a mainland resident (IRS Notice 2014-21; Act 60-2019, Sec. 2021.01; IRC Section 865).

How the IRS Classifies Bitcoin: The Foundation Everything Builds On

Before we can discuss Puerto Rico's treatment of Bitcoin, we need to understand the federal baseline. In 2014, the IRS issued Notice 2014-21, which established that virtual currency — including Bitcoin — is treated as property for U.S. federal income tax purposes. This means that general tax principles applicable to property transactions apply to every Bitcoin transaction (IRS Notice 2014-21, Q&A-1). Bitcoin is not treated as currency for tax purposes, and it does not generate foreign currency gain or loss (IRS Notice 2014-21, Q&A-2).

The practical implications are significant. Every disposition of Bitcoin — whether you sell it for USD, trade it for another cryptocurrency, use it to pay for goods or services, or transfer it in any taxable transaction — is a taxable event that requires you to calculate gain or loss based on the difference between the fair market value at disposition and your cost basis. This is true whether you hold Bitcoin directly, through a self-custody wallet, on an exchange, or through an ETF wrapper. The IRS further clarified in Revenue Ruling 2023-14 that staking rewards are taxable as ordinary income when the taxpayer gains dominion and control over the rewards (Rev. Rul. 2023-14).

Key IRS Guidance on Bitcoin

Notice 2014-21: Bitcoin is property. Capital gains rules apply to all dispositions.

Revenue Ruling 2019-24: Hard forks and airdrops are taxable events when you gain dominion and control.

Revenue Ruling 2023-14: Staking rewards are gross income in the year you gain dominion and control.

2024 Final Regulations: New broker reporting requirements for digital asset transactions effective from January 1, 2025 (Form 1099-DA).

Bitcoin Capital Gains Under Act 60 Chapter 2

Act 60 Chapter 2 (Section 2021.01) provides that bona fide Puerto Rico residents with an approved Individual Resident Investor decree can exempt Puerto Rico-sourced capital gains from Puerto Rico income tax. Because Bitcoin is classified as property under IRS Notice 2014-21, capital gains on Bitcoin are sourced to the taxpayer's residence at the time of the sale under IRC Section 865. For a bona fide Puerto Rico resident, that means post-move Bitcoin gains are PR-sourced — and therefore eligible for Act 60 treatment (Holland & Knight, "Puerto Rico's Act 60, Income Sourcing and IRS Scrutiny," Nov. 2025).

Bitcoin Capital Gains Rates in Puerto Rico (2026)

Pre-2026 decree holders: 0% on PR-sourced Bitcoin capital gains (post-move appreciation) — grandfathered under Act 60-2019, Sec. 2021.01(a).

Post-2025 decree applicants: 4% flat rate on PR-sourced Bitcoin capital gains (HB 505, effective January 1, 2026).

Pre-move appreciation: Subject to the 5% toll tax election or standard U.S. federal capital gains treatment (Act 60-2019, Sec. 2021.01(e)).

Non-decree holders (standard PR residents): Puerto Rico's progressive income tax rates of up to 33%, unless electing the voluntary 4% rate on PR-sourced passive income introduced alongside HB 505.

The critical nuance: the favorable rate only applies to appreciation that accrued after you became a bona fide Puerto Rico resident. Bitcoin you purchased for $10,000 on the mainland that was worth $50,000 on the day you established PR residency — that $40,000 of gain is pre-move appreciation, and it does not qualify for the 0% or 4% rate. Only the gain above $50,000 (the post-residency appreciation) receives Act 60 treatment.

This is why establishing your cost basis as of the date you become a bona fide resident is among the most important tax actions you'll take. You should document the fair market value of every Bitcoin position you hold as of your move date, using verifiable exchange data, and maintain those records permanently. The IRS specifically looks for taxpayers who erroneously treat pre-move Bitcoin gains as Puerto Rico-sourced income (IRS, "Introduction to Puerto Rico Acts 20 and 22," LB&I Publication).

The 5% Toll Tax on Pre-Move Bitcoin Gains

Act 60 Section 2021.01(e) provides a special election for decree holders with unrealized gains that accrued before establishing Puerto Rico residency. Rather than paying standard U.S. federal capital gains rates on the pre-move portion when you eventually sell, you can elect to pay a flat 5% Puerto Rico toll tax on the unrealized appreciation as of your move date (Act 60-2019, Sec. 2021.01(e)).

Toll Tax Example

You purchased 10 BTC at $5,000 each ($50,000 total basis) while living in Texas.

On the date you establish bona fide PR residency, BTC is worth $60,000 each ($600,000 total FMV).

Pre-move appreciation: $600,000 – $50,000 = $550,000.

5% toll tax on pre-move gain: $550,000 × 5% = $27,500.

If you eventually sell those 10 BTC for $100,000 each ($1,000,000 total), the post-move gain of $400,000 ($1M – $600K move-date FMV) is eligible for the 0% or 4% rate under your Act 60 decree.

The toll tax election must be made proactively and is particularly attractive for Bitcoin holders with very large unrealized gains from years of holding. The alternative — selling without the election — means the pre-move gain is treated as U.S.-sourced income subject to federal capital gains tax at rates up to 23.8% (20% + 3.8% NIIT). The 5% toll tax represents a significant potential savings.

However, the toll tax election has important timing considerations. Under the 10-year lookback rule in IRC Section 937(b) and Treasury Regulation 1.937-2, gains from the sale of certain property owned before becoming a Puerto Rico resident may not be considered PR-sourced if sold within 10 years of establishing residency. Holders who wait to sell until after the 10-year mark and who elected the toll tax may achieve maximum tax efficiency, but this requires careful planning with qualified tax counsel (Holland & Knight, "Puerto Rico's Act 60, Income Sourcing and IRS Scrutiny," Nov. 2025).

Bitcoin Trading: Day Traders, Swing Traders, and Active Investors

For active Bitcoin traders who are bona fide PR residents with an Act 60 decree, every trade of Bitcoin acquired post-move is eligible for the Act 60 capital gains treatment — including short-term trades. This is a significant distinction from the mainland, where short-term capital gains are taxed at ordinary income rates up to 37%.

However, traders need to maintain meticulous lot-level records. Each Bitcoin position should be tagged as either pre-move or post-move, with the cost basis established accordingly. When you sell, you need to be able to prove which lot you disposed of and whether the gain is pre-move (U.S.-taxable) or post-move (Act 60-eligible). Using FIFO, LIFO, or specific identification methods — consistent with Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP) — is essential. The IRS expects this level of documentation, and failing to maintain it is a common audit trigger.

Token-to-token swaps are also taxable events under federal law (IRS CCA 202124008 confirmed that IRC Section 1031 like-kind exchange treatment does not apply to exchanges between different cryptocurrencies). For PR residents, if both tokens in the swap were acquired post-move, the gain is PR-sourced and Act 60-eligible. If either token was acquired pre-move, the analysis becomes more complex and requires careful sourcing.

Trader Warning

If your Bitcoin trading activity rises to the level of a trade or business, the income may be classified as ordinary business income rather than capital gains. In that case, it would be subject to Puerto Rico's standard income tax rates (up to 33%) rather than the Act 60 capital gains rate — unless you structure the activity through a PR corporation with a Chapter 3 export services decree. Consult a Puerto Rico CPA to determine the appropriate classification for your activity level.

Bitcoin Mining in Puerto Rico: Tax Treatment for Miners

Bitcoin mining income is treated differently from capital gains. Under IRS Notice 2014-21, when a taxpayer successfully mines Bitcoin, the fair market value of the mined coins must be included in gross income on the date the taxpayer receives them. For solo miners and mining pools, this is ordinary income — not capital gains. It may also be subject to self-employment tax if the mining constitutes a trade or business (IRS Notice 2014-21, Q&A-8, Q&A-9).

For Puerto Rico-based mining operations, the tax treatment depends on how the activity is structured. Individual miners who mine Bitcoin as a personal investment activity may argue that post-move mining rewards fall under Act 60 Chapter 2's passive income exemption, but this is an area of ongoing interpretive uncertainty. The IRS has not issued specific guidance on whether mining income qualifies as capital gain or ordinary income for Act 60 purposes.

Mining Tax Structures in Puerto Rico

Individual miner (small-scale, personal): Mined Bitcoin may be treated as passive income eligible for the 0%/4% rate under Chapter 2, but this position is aggressive and should be discussed with tax counsel. The safer approach is to treat it as ordinary income.

Mining through a PR corporation with Chapter 3 decree: If mining operations are structured as an export service business with a bona fide office in PR, mining income may qualify for the 4% corporate tax rate, plus 100% exemption on dividend distributions from exempt income (Act 60-2019, Ch. 3; eligible activities include blockchain-related services).

Mining without an Act 60 decree: Subject to Puerto Rico's standard corporate tax rates (up to 37.5%) or individual rates (up to 33%), depending on the entity structure.

Miners who operate through a Puerto Rico corporation with a Chapter 3 decree must pay themselves a reasonable salary, which is subject to ordinary Puerto Rico income tax rates. Only the net profit above salary qualifies for the 4% rate. The IRS and Puerto Rico's OITE both scrutinize "reasonable salary" determinations, and setting it artificially low to shift more income into the 4% bucket is a known audit trigger.

Staking Rewards: How the IRS Taxes Proof-of-Stake Income

Revenue Ruling 2023-14 settled the question of whether staking rewards are taxable: they are. The IRS ruled that when a cash-method taxpayer stakes cryptocurrency and receives additional units as validation rewards, the fair market value of those rewards must be included in gross income in the taxable year the taxpayer gains dominion and control over them. The same treatment applies whether the taxpayer stakes directly or through a centralized exchange (Rev. Rul. 2023-14).

For Puerto Rico residents, the analysis splits along the same lines as mining. Small-scale personal staking by a bona fide resident may fall under the Chapter 2 passive income exemption, making the rewards potentially eligible for the 0%/4% rate. However, if staking constitutes a trade or business, the income is ordinary business income. Large-scale staking operations, particularly those run through validators, are more safely structured through a Puerto Rico corporation with a Chapter 3 export services decree, where they would be taxed at the 4% corporate rate.

Once staking rewards are received and their fair market value is included in income (at whatever rate applies), those newly received tokens establish a fresh cost basis. Any subsequent appreciation on those tokens is a capital gain, which is eligible for Act 60 Chapter 2 treatment if the tokens were received after the holder established PR residency.

Bitcoin ETFs (IBIT, FBTC) and Act 60: What PR Residents Need to Know

The SEC-approved spot Bitcoin ETFs — including BlackRock's iShares Bitcoin Trust (IBIT) and Fidelity's Wise Origin Bitcoin Fund (FBTC) — are structured as exchange-traded grantor trusts. From a tax perspective, gains from selling shares of a spot Bitcoin ETF are treated as capital gains, the same as selling Bitcoin directly. The holding period determines whether the gain is short-term or long-term for mainland taxpayers (etf.com, "How Are Bitcoin ETFs Taxed," 2025; Green Trader Tax, "Digital Asset Trading Explained," 2025).

For bona fide Puerto Rico residents with an Act 60 Chapter 2 decree, gains from spot Bitcoin ETFs purchased after establishing residency should be eligible for the same favorable treatment as direct Bitcoin holdings. The gain is a capital gain on property, sourced to the taxpayer's residence under IRC Section 865, making it PR-sourced income for Act 60 purposes.

Bitcoin ETF Tax Treatment Summary for PR Residents

Spot Bitcoin ETFs (IBIT, FBTC): Capital gains treatment. Post-move shares eligible for 0%/4% Act 60 rate. Same pre-move vs. post-move rules as direct Bitcoin.

Futures-based Bitcoin ETFs (BITO): Subject to Section 1256 mark-to-market rules at the fund level, with 60/40 long-term/short-term treatment passed through to investors. Interaction with Act 60 is less clear — consult a CPA.

Pre-move ETF shares: Appreciation before PR residency is U.S.-sourced. The 5% toll tax election under Section 2021.01(e) may apply, but ETF shares are securities, and the sourcing rules for securities may differ from direct crypto holdings. This is an area requiring professional guidance.

An important consideration: unlike holding Bitcoin directly, Bitcoin ETF shares are securities. The IRS applies wash sale rules to securities (though not yet to direct crypto), which means selling ETF shares at a loss and repurchasing within 30 days could disallow the loss. Additionally, the 10-year lookback rule under Treasury Regulation 1.937-2 applies to securities, meaning that gains from pre-move ETF shares sold within 10 years of establishing PR residency may not be treatable as PR-sourced income — a factor that doesn't apply the same way to direct Bitcoin holdings (Holland & Knight, Nov. 2025).

Filing Requirements: What Bitcoin Holders in Puerto Rico Must File

Bona fide Puerto Rico residents have dual filing obligations. You don't escape filing entirely — you shift what you report and where.

Puerto Rico Tax Return (Form 482)

Due April 15, 2026 (extension to October 15 available). Report your worldwide income, then deduct Act 60-exempt gains on Schedule B3. Attach your decree documentation. PR-sourced Bitcoin gains eligible for the 0%/4% rate are reported here and exempted.

U.S. Federal Tax Return (Form 1040)

Still required. Report any U.S.-sourced income and pre-move capital gains. PR-sourced income excluded under IRC Section 933. You must answer the digital assets question on Form 1040. Do not report Act 60-exempt PR-sourced income on this return.

IRS Form 8898

Required in the year you establish (or end) bona fide Puerto Rico residency. This form notifies the IRS of your residency change and is critical documentation for your Act 60 file.

DDEC Annual Report

Required annually by your decree terms. Failure to file can result in fines of up to $1,000 per violation and potential decree suspension. Beginning in 2026, the enhanced compliance portal requires certified CPA letters, proof of residency, income source breakdowns, and — for crypto holders — wallet addresses and transaction histories.

2026 Enhanced Crypto Reporting

Beginning in 2026, the Act 60 compliance portal requires crypto holders to declare wallet addresses, transaction histories, and asset holdings to verify that capital gains claimed as exempt were genuinely earned post-residency. This aligns with the broader IRS push for digital asset transparency through Form 1099-DA broker reporting requirements that took effect in January 2025.

IRS Enforcement: Why Bitcoin Holders Face Extra Scrutiny

The IRS has specifically identified Bitcoin and cryptocurrency holders under Act 60 as an enforcement priority. The IRS Large Business & International Division noted that some individuals benefitting from Act 22/Act 60 may be improperly excluding income from U.S. tax, and that some may be erroneously treating capital gains on property acquired while a U.S. resident as Puerto Rico-sourced income (IRS, "Introduction to Puerto Rico Acts 20 and 22," LB&I Publication).

Holland & Knight has observed that the lack of specific IRS guidance on cryptocurrency taxation under Act 60 has created interpretive ambiguity. Some taxpayers have adopted aggressive positions — such as arguing that gains on pre-move crypto can be sourced entirely to Puerto Rico — that the IRS has challenged through Chief Counsel Memoranda and enforcement actions (Holland & Knight, Nov. 2025; Pillsbury Law, "Uncertainty Clouds Puerto Rico's Act 60 Tax Breaks").

Bitcoin-Specific Audit Defense Checklist

✓ Establish and document fair market value of all Bitcoin positions as of your bona fide residency date, using verifiable exchange data.

✓ Tag every lot as pre-move or post-move in your tracking software.

✓ Maintain complete transaction histories for all wallets and exchanges (CSVs, TXIDs, USD spot rates).

✓ Keep travel logs with boarding passes, flight records, and passport stamps to prove the 183-day presence test.

✓ Document your PR tax home: office lease, coworking membership, business registration.

✓ Prove closer connection: PR driver's license, voter registration, bank accounts, utility bills, community involvement.

✓ File Form 8898 in the year of your move.

✓ Meet the $10,000 annual charitable donation requirement (at least half to child poverty organizations).

✓ File all returns: Form 482 (PR), Form 1040 (US), DDEC annual report, and any applicable FinCEN/FBAR filings if you hold crypto on foreign exchanges.

The Bottom Line for Bitcoin Holders in Puerto Rico

Puerto Rico remains one of the most attractive jurisdictions in the world for Bitcoin investors who are willing to make a genuine relocation. Even with the 2026 HB 505 reform introducing a 4% rate for new applicants, the combined savings compared to mainland federal and state capital gains taxes are substantial — particularly for holders with large unrealized gains they plan to realize over the coming decades.

But the window for error is narrowing. The IRS is actively targeting Act 60 decree holders with crypto positions. Puerto Rico's own compliance portal now demands wallet addresses and transaction histories. The distinction between pre-move and post-move gains is being scrutinized lot by lot. Aggressive sourcing positions that were once in a gray area are being challenged through enforcement memoranda and court proceedings.

The Bitcoin holders who will benefit most from Puerto Rico's tax incentives are those who treat compliance as a first-class priority: establishing ironclad residency documentation, maintaining forensic-quality transaction records, working with a PR-licensed CPA who understands both federal and territorial crypto taxation, and never assuming that any single Bitcoin transaction is too small to matter.
