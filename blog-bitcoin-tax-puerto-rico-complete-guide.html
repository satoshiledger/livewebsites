Bitcoin Tax in Puerto Rico 2026: The Complete Guide for Holders, Traders, and Miners Impuestos de Bitcoin en Puerto Rico 2026: Guía Completa para Holders, Traders y Mineros

Bitcoin holders, traders, and miners face complex tax obligations in 2026. The IRS classifies Bitcoin as property—not currency—meaning virtually every transaction triggers capital gains or income tax. For U.S. residents on the mainland, this can mean paying up to 37% (2026 rates) on short-term gains or up to 20% (plus 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax) on long-term gains.

But there's a legal alternative: Puerto Rico's Act 60. Under Chapter 2 of Act 60, bona fide Puerto Rico residents can achieve 0% tax on Bitcoin capital gains accrued after establishing residency—if structured correctly. This article explains exactly how Bitcoin is taxed under U.S. federal law, how Puerto Rico's tax incentives apply, and the critical compliance rules you must follow in 2026.

Critical 2026 Update: The IRS now requires brokers to issue Form 1099-DA for digital asset transactions. Starting January 1, 2026, cost basis reporting becomes mandatory for digital assets acquired on or after that date. Accurate record-keeping is no longer optional—it's legally required.

Federal Bitcoin Tax Treatment: IRS Notice 2014-21

The foundation of Bitcoin taxation is IRS Notice 2014-21 (2014-16 I.R.B. 938). This notice established that virtual currency is treated as property for federal tax purposes, not as foreign currency. This classification has significant implications for every Bitcoin transaction you make.

What "Property Treatment" Means

Because the IRS classifies Bitcoin as a capital asset—not currency—every disposal event triggers a gain or loss calculation, just as it would for stock or real estate. This means:

Capital Asset Classification

Bitcoin held for investment is a capital asset. Sales trigger capital gains or losses just like stocks or real estate under IRC §§ 1221–1222.

Holding Period Matters

Bitcoin held ≤ 1 year = short-term gains (10%–37%, 2026 rates). Bitcoin held > 1 year = long-term gains (0%–20%, 2026 rates).

Every Transaction Is Taxable

Selling Bitcoin for USD, trading Bitcoin for another crypto, or spending Bitcoin all trigger taxable events.

Basis Tracking Required

You must track acquisition date, purchase price (cost basis), and fair market value at disposal for every Bitcoin unit.

Mining Income vs. Staking Rewards: Two Separate IRS Rules

Bitcoin mining rewards are taxed as ordinary income at fair market value when received, per IRS Notice 2014-21 (2014-16 I.R.B. 938). This has been the controlling guidance for proof-of-work mining since 2014.

For proof-of-stake assets (not Bitcoin's consensus mechanism, but relevant if you hold other digital assets), IRS Revenue Ruling 2023-14 (2023-33 I.R.B. 484, published August 14, 2023) confirmed that staking rewards are likewise taxable as ordinary income in the year the taxpayer gains dominion and control over them—measured at fair market value on that date.

Example: On March 15, 2026, your Bitcoin mining operation generates 0.1 BTC when Bitcoin trades at $95,000. You must report $9,500 as ordinary income on your 2026 tax return, even if you don't sell the Bitcoin. Your cost basis in that 0.1 BTC is now $9,500.

2026 Bitcoin Tax Rates: Mainland U.S.

For U.S. taxpayers residing on the mainland, Bitcoin is subject to either capital gains tax (for sales and trades) or ordinary income tax (for mining, staking, and airdrops). Here are the 2026 rates:

Short-Term Capital Gains (Held ≤ 1 Year)

Short-term capital gains are taxed at ordinary income rates, which range from 10% to 37% (2026 rates) depending on taxable income. An additional 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT) may apply if 2026 modified adjusted gross income exceeds $200,000 (single) or $250,000 (married filing jointly).

Long-Term Capital Gains (Held > 1 Year)

Long-term capital gains benefit from preferential rates of 0%, 15%, or 20% (2026 rates), depending on taxable income. The 3.8% NIIT may also apply to high earners.

Real-World Tax Comparison

Scenario: $500,000 Bitcoin Long-Term Capital Gain

Mainland U.S. (High Earner):

• Federal long-term capital gains tax: $500,000 × 20% = $100,000

• Net Investment Income Tax: $500,000 × 3.8% = $19,000

• California state tax (example): $500,000 × 13.3% = $66,500

Total Tax: $185,500

Puerto Rico (Act 60 Bona Fide Resident, Post-Residency Gain):

Total Tax: $0

Mining and Staking Income

Bitcoin received from mining is taxed as ordinary income at fair market value when received, per IRS Notice 2014-21. Miners operating as a business can deduct expenses such as electricity, equipment depreciation, and facility costs. Self-employed miners must also pay self-employment tax (15.3%) on net earnings.

Puerto Rico Act 60: 0% Bitcoin Capital Gains Tax

Chapter 2 of Puerto Rico's Act 60 (formerly Act 22) provides 100% exemption from Puerto Rico income taxes on capital gains, interest, and dividends for qualifying individual investors. Combined with IRC Section 933, which excludes Puerto Rico-source income from U.S. federal taxation, bona fide Puerto Rico residents can achieve 0% tax on Bitcoin capital gains accrued after establishing residency.

How It Works: The Two-Jurisdiction Split

The key to understanding Act 60's Bitcoin benefits is the distinction between pre-residency gains and post-residency gains:

Pre-Residency Gains

Bitcoin appreciation that occurred before you became a bona fide Puerto Rico resident is U.S.-source income and remains fully subject to U.S. federal capital gains tax under the 10-year lookback rule in Treas. Reg. § 1.937-2.

The 5% Toll Tax Election (Puerto Rico Only):

Under Act 60, § 2021.01(e), you may elect to pay a 5% Puerto Rico tax on pre-residency appreciation as of your move date. This is a Puerto Rico-layer election—it does not reduce or replace your U.S. federal tax obligation. In practice, pre-residency gains face: (1) full U.S. federal capital gains tax, plus (2) the 5% PR toll tax if you make that election.

Electing the toll tax gives Puerto Rico its acknowledgment of the basis step-up, which then protects post-move appreciation under the 0% exemption.

Post-Residency Gains

Bitcoin appreciation that occurs after you become a bona fide Puerto Rico resident is:

• 100% exempt from Puerto Rico income tax under Act 60, § 2021.01(a)

• Exempt from U.S. federal tax under IRC § 933 (Puerto Rico-source income exclusion)

Effective Tax Rate: 0%

Example: You buy 10 BTC for $30,000 per coin ($300,000 total) in 2024. In 2025, you move to Puerto Rico and become a bona fide resident. The fair market value on your move date is $70,000 per coin. In 2028, you sell all 10 BTC for $120,000 per coin ($1.2 million total).

Tax Analysis:

  • • Pre-residency gain: ($70,000 − $30,000) × 10 = $400,000 — subject to full U.S. federal capital gains tax (plus optional 5% PR toll tax if elected)
  • • Post-residency gain: ($120,000 − $70,000) × 10 = $500,000 — 0% tax under Act 60 + IRC § 933
  • • Total tax on $500,000 of post-residency gain: $0

IRC Section 933: The Federal Exclusion

IRC Section 933 provides that bona fide residents of Puerto Rico are not subject to U.S. federal income tax on Puerto Rico-source income. Under IRC Section 865(a)(2), capital gains from the sale of personal property (including Bitcoin) are sourced to the taxpayer's tax residence at the time of sale. Therefore, if you are a bona fide Puerto Rico resident when you sell Bitcoin acquired after establishing residency, the gain is Puerto Rico-source income and excluded from U.S. federal taxation.

10-Year Lookback Rule: Under Treas. Reg. § 1.937-2, if you owned Bitcoin before moving to Puerto Rico, the pre-residency appreciation is treated as U.S.-source income—not Puerto Rico-source income—and remains fully subject to U.S. federal capital gains tax regardless of Act 60. This rule applies for the ten-year period following the establishment of Puerto Rico residency. Timing your move relative to unrealized gains is one of the most critical planning decisions in any Act 60 strategy.

Bona Fide Residency: The Three Tests You Must Pass

To qualify for Act 60's 0% tax on Bitcoin gains, you must be a bona fide resident of Puerto Rico for the entire taxable year. This is determined by the IRS under IRC § 937 and Treasury Regulation § 1.937-1, not by Puerto Rico. You must satisfy three tests:

1 Presence Test

You must be present in Puerto Rico for at least 183 days during the taxable year, OR you must meet one of the alternative tests:

  • Present in the U.S. for no more than 90 days during the taxable year
  • Have no earned income from U.S. sources exceeding $3,000 and be present in Puerto Rico more days than in the U.S.
  • No "significant connection" to the U.S.

2 Tax Home Test

Your principal place of business or employment (your "tax home") must be in Puerto Rico. If you conduct business remotely from Puerto Rico, you must be able to prove Puerto Rico is your base of operations.

3 Closer Connection Test

You must not have a "closer connection" to the U.S. or a foreign country than to Puerto Rico. This is a facts-and-circumstances test that considers your permanent home, family location, bank accounts, driver's license, voter registration, and social ties.

Act 60 Decree Requirements

In addition to bona fide residency, you must obtain an Act 60 tax decree and satisfy these requirements:

  • • Application fee: $5,000 (one-time, paid with application)
  • • Annual fee: $5,000 (due with annual report)
  • • Annual donation: $10,000 to Puerto Rico nonprofits ($5,000 must go to organizations combating child poverty), per Act 60, § 2021.01(b)(4)
  • • Property purchase: Within two years of receiving the decree, purchase residential property in Puerto Rico for use as your principal residence
  • • Eligibility requirement: Must not have been a Puerto Rico resident at any time from January 1, 2006, to January 17, 2012

Note: Fee amounts are set administratively by DDEC and are subject to change. Verify current amounts at incentivos.ddec.pr.gov before filing.

IRS Enforcement Is Real — And Criminal: Federal prosecutors have made clear that Act 60 fraud carries serious consequences. In United States v. Gajwani (Case No. 1:25-cr-20117, S.D. Fla.), Miami investor Suresh Gajwani pleaded guilty on June 13, 2025 to making a false statement to the IRS under 26 U.S.C. § 7206(1). Gajwani had legitimately established bona fide Puerto Rico residency as of January 1, 2020, but attempted to shelter approximately $30 million in pre-residency built-in gains by backdating a corporate S-election to January 1, 2019 via a fraudulent Form 2553 filing. He was sentenced on August 20, 2025 to one year of probation and agreed to pay approximately $15.3 million in restitution. The case demonstrates that criminal exposure under Act 60 is not limited to false residency claims—it extends to any fraudulent document submitted to the IRS in connection with an Act 60 strategy. Document everything. Do not backdate anything.

Bitcoin Trading vs. Long-Term Holding: Tax Strategy

Under Act 60, there is a critical distinction between trading activity and investment activity. Puerto Rico tax law, like U.S. tax law, distinguishes between "dealers" (who trade as a business) and "investors" (who hold for long-term appreciation).

Long-Term Holders (Investors)

If you buy Bitcoin and hold it for long-term appreciation (not frequent trading), your gains qualify for Act 60's 0% capital gains exemption. This is the optimal structure for Bitcoin holders who believe in long-term price appreciation.

Active Traders

If you engage in frequent Bitcoin trading as a business, you may be considered a "dealer" rather than an investor. Under Section 1010.01(a)(40)(D) of the Puerto Rico Internal Revenue Code of 2011, the exclusion for trading in securities or commodities for one's own account does not apply to dealers in those assets. As a dealer, your Bitcoin trading income would be subject to Puerto Rico's graduated ordinary income tax rates (up to 33%) rather than the 0% capital gains exemption under Act 60.

Risk for Day Traders: The IRS and Puerto Rico Hacienda may recharacterize frequent Bitcoin trading as "dealer" activity, subjecting your profits to ordinary income tax. If you trade Bitcoin actively, consult a Puerto Rico CPA to determine whether you should operate under Act 60 Chapter 3 (Export Services) at a 4% corporate tax rate instead of Chapter 2.

Recommended Structure for Bitcoin Holders

For individuals who hold Bitcoin for long-term appreciation and make occasional sales (not frequent trading), the optimal structure is:

  • 1. Obtain Act 60 Chapter 2 (Individual Investor) tax decree
  • 2. Become a bona fide Puerto Rico resident before acquiring new Bitcoin
  • 3. Purchase Bitcoin after establishing residency
  • 4. Hold Bitcoin for long-term appreciation (avoid frequent trading)
  • 5. When you sell, all post-residency appreciation is 0% tax (Puerto Rico and federal)

Bitcoin Mining in Puerto Rico: Tax Treatment

Bitcoin mining income is treated differently from capital gains. Under IRS Notice 2014-21 (2014-16 I.R.B. 938), Bitcoin received from mining is ordinary income at fair market value when received, not capital gain. Mining rewards are taxable in the year received regardless of whether you sell the Bitcoin.

Mining as a Business: Act 60 Chapter 3

If you operate a Bitcoin mining business in Puerto Rico, you should structure the operation under Act 60 Chapter 3 (Export Services), not Chapter 2. Chapter 3 provides:

  • • 4% corporate income tax on eligible export service income
  • • 0% tax on dividends distributed to Puerto Rico resident shareholders
  • • Full deductibility of business expenses (electricity, equipment, facility costs)

Example: You establish a Puerto Rico corporation under Act 60 Chapter 3 to operate Bitcoin mining. In 2026, the corporation mines 5 BTC worth $475,000 (5 × $95,000). The corporation has $200,000 in deductible expenses (electricity, equipment depreciation, facility rent). Net income: $275,000. Corporate tax at 4%: $11,000. When the corporation distributes the after-tax profit to you as a dividend, you pay 0% tax (Act 60 Chapter 3 benefit). Total effective tax rate: 4%.

Hobby Mining vs. Business Mining

If you mine Bitcoin as a hobby (not as a business), the income is still taxable, but you cannot deduct expenses beyond the income. For serious miners, it's essential to operate as a business entity to maximize deductions.

2026 Record-Keeping Requirements: Form 1099-DA

Starting in 2025, brokers are required to issue Form 1099-DA for digital asset transactions, reporting gross proceeds to the IRS. However, cost basis reporting is a separate, later requirement: beginning January 1, 2026, brokers must also report cost basis for digital assets acquired on or after that date. Assets acquired before 2026 are not covered by the broker basis-reporting mandate—meaning you remain solely responsible for tracking basis on older positions.

2025 Transactions

Brokers report gross proceeds only. Cost basis is not reported by brokers. You are responsible for your own basis records.

2026 and Beyond

Brokers report gross proceeds and cost basis for digital assets acquired on or after January 1, 2026. Prior positions still require self-documentation.

What You Must Track

Under IRS regulations, you must maintain records showing:

  • • Date and time each unit of Bitcoin was acquired
  • • Purchase price (cost basis) and fair market value at acquisition
  • • Date and time each unit was sold, exchanged, or disposed of
  • • Fair market value at disposal and amount received
  • • Identification of specific units sold (if using specific identification method)

Transfer Basis Gap: If you transfer Bitcoin between exchanges or wallets, the receiving platform will not automatically have your cost basis. You are responsible for tracking basis across all platforms. Failure to maintain accurate records can result in overpayment of taxes (if you can't prove a higher basis) or IRS scrutiny (if reported proceeds don't reconcile with your return).

Recommended Tools

For Bitcoin holders with multiple transactions, we recommend using professional crypto tax software (such as CoinLedger, Koinly, or TokenTax) to track cost basis and generate tax reports. For Act 60 residents, you must also maintain records proving bona fide residency (flight records, utility bills, lease agreements, bank statements).

5 Critical Mistakes Bitcoin Holders Make in Puerto Rico

1 Moving to PR After Bitcoin Appreciates

If you buy Bitcoin for $30,000, it appreciates to $100,000, and then you move to Puerto Rico, the $70,000 of pre-residency gain is still subject to U.S. federal capital gains tax—Act 60 cannot exempt it. Timing matters. Move before significant appreciation occurs, or plan carefully around the toll tax election.

2 Failing to Satisfy Bona Fide Residency

Spending 183 days in Puerto Rico is not enough if you maintain a tax home or closer connection to the mainland. The IRS will disallow your Act 60 benefits if you fail any of the three tests under IRC § 937 and Treas. Reg. § 1.937-1.

3 Misclassifying Trading as Investment

If you trade Bitcoin frequently, Puerto Rico Hacienda may recharacterize your activity as "dealer" trading under PR IRC § 1010.01(a)(40)(D), subjecting you to ordinary income tax (up to 33%) instead of 0% capital gains.

4 Poor Record-Keeping

With Form 1099-DA now mandatory for gross proceeds, the IRS has transaction-level visibility into your Bitcoin activity. If you can't prove your cost basis or residency dates, you'll overpay taxes or face penalties. Remember: broker basis reporting only covers assets acquired after January 1, 2026—older positions are still your responsibility.

5 Not Filing Required Forms

Act 60 residents must file Form 8898 (Statement for Individuals Who Begin or End Bona Fide Residence in a U.S. Possession) with their federal tax return in the year of the move. Failure to file can result in penalties and loss of IRC § 933 exclusion.
