10 Mistakes People Make When Applying for Act 60 (And How to Avoid Them) 10 Errores Que Cometen al Solicitar la Ley 60 (Y Cómo Evitarlos)

Share this article:Compartir este articulo:

In 2025, the DDEC's Office of Business Incentives audited 1,798 Act 60 decrees. The result: 305 deficiency notices issued, 19 decrees annulled, 4 revoked for serious noncompliance, and 887 voluntarily surrendered. Starting January 15, 2026, the DDEC began automatically issuing fines for late annual report filings, with penalties starting at $1,000 that can escalate to full revocation (Tax Notes, Oct. 2025; News Is My Business, Oct. 2025).

Simultaneously, the IRS has entered what Procopio described as "a new and far more aggressive phase of enforcement" targeting Act 60 decree holders. The December 2025 GAO report criticized the IRS for years of inaction and recommended systematic data collection on all Act 60 participants. The IRS agreed with every recommendation (Procopio, Dec. 2025; GAO Dec. 2025).

The New Reality

The days of lax enforcement are over. Both the DDEC and the IRS are now actively policing Act 60 compliance. Whether you're applying for a decree, already hold one, or are planning your move, understanding these 10 mistakes — and how to avoid them — is the difference between saving millions in taxes and facing audit, penalties, or criminal prosecution.

1

Failing the Bona Fide Residency Test

This is the single most common and most consequential mistake. To qualify for Act 60 benefits, you must be a bona fide resident of Puerto Rico under IRC §937, which requires passing three separate tests: the presence test (183+ days in PR per tax year), the tax home test (principal place of business in PR), and the closer connection test (primary ties — home, family, banking, social — in PR rather than the mainland). All three must be met simultaneously (IRC §937; IRS Pub. 570; Treas. Reg. §1.937-1).

The GAO report found that average federal taxable income declined by 39% after taxpayers relocated to Puerto Rico, and average federal income tax paid declined by 46% — numbers that attract IRS scrutiny. The IRS now examines travel records, airline manifests, credit card geolocation data, and cell phone tower records to verify whether decree holders actually spent 183+ days on the island (Procopio, Dec. 2025; Windham Brannon, Feb. 2026).

How to Avoid It

Keep a detailed daily log of your physical location. Save boarding passes, hotel receipts, toll records, and any evidence of your presence in PR. Get a PR driver's license, register to vote in PR, change your bank and investment accounts to PR-based institutions, and ensure your family and social ties visibly center on Puerto Rico. Tax Law Ventures specifically warns that without clear travel documentation, residency claims can be dismissed outright (Tax Law Ventures, Jul. 2025).

2

Treating Pre-Move Gains as PR-Sourced

This mistake has generated criminal prosecution. Only income that accrues after you establish bona fide PR residency is eligible for Act 60 treatment. Capital gains on assets you owned before moving to Puerto Rico remain subject to U.S. federal tax — you cannot simply move to PR, sell pre-existing assets, and claim the gains are PR-sourced.

The Gajwani case is the cautionary tale: Miami investor Suresh Gajwani pleaded guilty to making false statements to avoid $7 million in capital gains tax and was sentenced to one year of probation. He attempted to claim pre-move appreciation as PR-sourced. The Senate Finance Committee also launched a probe into Pantera Capital founder Dan Morehead, alleging he used Act 60 to avoid hundreds of millions in U.S. capital gains tax on cryptocurrency gains that accrued before he established PR residency (Tax Notes, Oct. 2025; Holland & Knight, Nov. 2025).

Holland & Knight's November 2025 analysis warned that the IRS will aggressively scrutinize whether gains claimed as PR-sourced actually accrued after residency was established, and that IRS Chief Counsel Memorandum 202538025 supplemented by AM 2024-005 reinforced that taxpayers cannot re-characterize U.S.-sourced gains as PR-sourced through pass-through entities (Holland & Knight, Nov. 2025).

How to Avoid It

Establish the fair market value of all assets as of your residency date and document it thoroughly. For crypto, record wallet balances and FMV on the day you pass the bona fide residency test. Pre-move gains are either subject to the 5% toll tax election (Act 60-2019, §2021.01(e)) or must be apportioned between pre-move and post-move periods under the 10-year lookback rule in Treas. Reg. §1.937-2(f). Never treat 100% of gains on pre-move assets as PR-sourced.

3

Missing the Annual Report and Filing Deadlines

The DDEC specifically identified failure to file the Annual Report of Business Exempt (known as IANE in Spanish) as one of the most common compliance breaches in its 2025 audits. This report is due November 15 each year (or the 15th day of the 11th month after the close of the fiscal year) and must be filed through the DDEC's Portal of Incentives, accompanied by a $5,005 compliance fee and all supporting documentation (DDEC, Nov. 2025; Tax Notes, Oct. 2025; Bookkeeping Services, Jul. 2025).

Starting January 15, 2026, the DDEC began automatically issuing deficiency notifications and imposing fines for late or missing annual reports. The penalties start at $1,000 per noncompliance and can escalate to decree revocation for persistent violations. The compliance audit committee — composed of the DDEC undersecretary, legal director, and members of the secretary's office — can impose fines up to $10,000 or recommend revocation (Tax Notes, Oct. 2025).

How to Avoid It

Set a recurring calendar reminder for September 1 to begin preparing your annual report. This is separate from your PR income tax return (Form 482 or 480.20) and your annual LLC report to the Department of State. You need all three. Engage a PR-based CPA who is familiar with the DDEC portal to ensure the filing is complete and on time. The report requires proof of residency, tax payment evidence, donation receipts, and for export services companies, CPA-audited financial statements.

4

Neglecting the $10,000 Charitable Donation Requirement

Chapter 2 (Individual Resident Investor) decree holders must make an annual charitable donation of at least $10,000 to eligible Puerto Rico-based nonprofits. At least $5,000 must go to an organization on the CECFL list — these are approved charities that work to alleviate child poverty in Puerto Rico. The remaining $5,000 can go to any charity certified under Section 1101.01(a)(2)(A)(iii) of the PR Internal Revenue Code, which is equivalent to 501(c)(3) status (Act 60-2019, §2021.01(b)(4); WoodsLux, Oct. 2025; PR Advantage; McConnell Valdés).

The DDEC's 2025 audit specifically flagged "noncompliance with required donations" as one of the most common breaches. Donations must be completed by December 31 of each tax year, and official receipts must be filed with the DDEC annual report. Crucially, the nonprofit cannot be controlled by the decree holder — donating to your own foundation doesn't count (News Is My Business, Oct. 2025; Act 60-2019).

How to Avoid It

Complete your donations by early November — don't wait until December 31 when nonprofits may have administrative delays processing receipts. WoodsLux recommends this buffer to avoid year-end complications. Verify the charity is on the current DDEC-approved CECFL list before donating the $5,000 children's portion. Keep official donation receipts in a dedicated compliance folder for your CPA. The DDEC has advised that the full $10,000 can go to a CECFL entity if desired (PR Advantage).

5

Not Purchasing Real Property Within Two Years

Chapter 2 decree holders must purchase real property in Puerto Rico for use as their principal residence within two years of obtaining the decree. The property must be owned by the individual (or jointly with a spouse) — not by an LLC, trust, or other entity. The DDEC identified "lack of proof of real estate purchases in Puerto Rico" as one of the most common breaches during its 2025 audit cycle (Act 60-2019, §2021.01(b); News Is My Business, Oct. 2025; McConnell Valdés).

Many applicants arrive in Puerto Rico, rent an apartment, and assume they have plenty of time. Two years passes quickly, and the PR real estate market in desirable areas (Condado, Dorado Beach, Isla Verde) has limited inventory for luxury purchases. Missing this deadline can result in decree revocation.

How to Avoid It

Begin your real estate search before or immediately upon receiving your decree. Work with a PR-based real estate agent who understands Act 60 requirements. Ensure the property is titled in your personal name (or jointly with spouse), not in an entity. Keep the deed, closing documents, and utility bills as proof for DDEC compliance filings.

6

Misunderstanding Income Sourcing Rules

Act 60 benefits apply only to Puerto Rico-sourced income. U.S.-sourced income remains fully subject to federal tax even if you're a bona fide PR resident. Tax Law Ventures specifically warned that "sourcing errors happen when U.S.-sourced income is treated as Puerto Rico-source" and that applicants must ensure their service delivery model aligns with their decree (Tax Law Ventures, Jul. 2025).

For personal services income, the sourcing rule is straightforward: income is sourced to where the services are physically performed (IRC §§861-865; Treas. Reg. §1.937-2(b)). If you fly to New York to work with a client for two weeks, that income is U.S.-sourced regardless of where your company is incorporated. For capital gains, the source depends on the taxpayer's residence at the time of sale (IRC §865(a)). For dividends from U.S. corporations, the income is generally U.S.-sourced (IRC §861(a)(2)).

Kostelanetz partner Jay Nanavati told Tax Notes that some clients are "incredulous" when told they can't simply cross the line, sell their company, and have all gains be PR-sourced. They've heard the opposite from friends with aggressive legal opinions — the same opinions the IRS is now challenging (Tax Notes, Nov. 2025).

How to Avoid It

Have your tax attorney map every income stream to a specific source (PR-sourced vs. U.S.-sourced) under IRC §§861-865. Perform all services from Puerto Rico. If you must travel to the mainland, track the days meticulously and apportion income accordingly. Never assume that forming a PR entity automatically makes income PR-sourced — it's where the work is performed that determines the source.

7

Applying with an Incomplete or Sloppy Application

The DDEC processes decree applications through its Single Business Portal, and incomplete applications are the primary cause of delays and denials. INVESTATE PR notes that processing times typically run 3 to 6 months, but delays increase significantly when documents are incomplete or require clarification (INVESTATE PR).

Common documentation gaps include missing identity proof (SSN card, passport), incomplete lease or title documentation for the PR residence, missing bank statements, absent criminal record certifications, and for export services applicants, a vague or incomplete business plan. As of 2025, the DDEC now requires criminal record certifications from the applicant's country or state of previous residence, and a final background verification is conducted before the decree is signed. Decrees are being denied for individuals with convictions for fraud, serious crimes, or patterns of criminal conduct (Tax Law Ventures, Jul. 2025; News Is My Business, Oct. 2025; El Nuevo Día, Dec. 2025).

How to Avoid It

Assemble all documentation before beginning the application. For export services applicants, prepare a detailed business plan that describes eligible activities, identifies clients outside PR, and explains how services will be performed from Puerto Rico. Work with a qualified promoter or PR tax attorney who has experience with the DDEC portal and can pre-screen your application for completeness. The Puerto Rico Court of Appeals has upheld DDEC's authority to deny decrees based on criminal records, so address any background issues with legal counsel before applying (El Nuevo Día, Dec. 2025).

8

No Bona Fide Office or Economic Substance (Export Services)

Chapter 3 (Export Services) decree holders must maintain a bona fide office in Puerto Rico. This means a real physical office with a lease, utilities, internet service, and the equipment needed to conduct your business. A virtual mailbox or co-working space membership is generally insufficient. If your revenue exceeds $3 million per year, you also need at least one full-time PR-based employee (Act 60-2019, Chapter 3).

The IRS examines economic substance carefully during Act 60 audits. They look for evidence that the PR entity is more than a billing conduit: real client deliverables produced from the PR office, evidence of day-to-day operations in PR, local vendor contracts, office photos, and technology infrastructure. A shell company that exists only on paper — or a company that funnels mainland employee work through a PR entity — will not withstand scrutiny (Holland & Knight, Nov. 2025; Pillsbury, Nov. 2025).

How to Avoid It

Sign a lease for a dedicated office space in Puerto Rico. Set up local internet service, utilities, and business phone line. Produce client deliverables from the PR office and document this with time logs, project records, and client communications sent from PR. If you have employees, ensure they're PR-based, on PR payroll, and with proper local tax withholdings. Keep lease agreements, utility bills, and local vendor contracts organized for audit defense.

9

Failing to File Form 8898 and Coordinate U.S./PR Returns

When you establish bona fide PR residency, you must file IRS Form 8898 (Statement for Individuals Who Begin or End Bona Fide Residence in a U.S. Possession) in the year of your move. This form notifies the IRS of your residency change. Failure to file it is a red flag. The GAO report specifically noted that the IRS is now cross-referencing Form 8898 filings with PR tax returns and Act 60 decree records. Any mismatch between PR filings and federal filings will be identified (IRS Form 8898; Procopio, Dec. 2025; Windham Brannon, Feb. 2026).

Additionally, you must continue filing a U.S. Form 1040 each year, reporting worldwide income and excluding PR-sourced income under IRC §933. You also file PR Form 482 (individuals) or 480.20 (corporations), reporting PR income and claiming Act 60 exemptions. The Pillsbury webinar slides noted that late filings or discrepancies between these returns can jeopardize tax incentives (Pillsbury, Nov. 2025).

How to Avoid It

File Form 8898 in the year you move. Engage both a U.S. CPA and a PR CPA (or one who handles both jurisdictions) to ensure your federal return, PR return, and DDEC annual report are consistent. The income excluded on your federal return under §933 should match the PR-sourced income reported on your PR return. Document everything, and coordinate filing timelines between jurisdictions.

10

Using Aggressive Legal Opinions as a Substitute for Compliance

Tax Notes reported in November 2025 that some tax attorneys have provided legal opinion letters arguing taxpayers can source 100% of gains to Puerto Rico — even on pre-move assets — using creative structures involving S-corporations and partnerships. The IRS has explicitly rejected this position. Senator Ron Wyden stated that using such structures to source all gains to PR is "not compliant with the U.S. tax code" and that both the IRS and Hacienda have made this clear (Tax Notes, Nov. 2025).

The Senate Finance Committee investigated at least one case where IRS representatives reportedly advised a tax attorney that his legal opinions were incorrect, yet the attorney continued issuing them to clients. Having a legal opinion letter does not protect you from prosecution if the underlying position is frivolous. The Gajwani criminal prosecution demonstrates that the IRS is willing to pursue criminal charges, not just civil penalties (Tax Notes, Nov. 2025; DOJ, Jun. 2025).

How to Avoid It

Work with reputable tax counsel who will give you honest, conservative advice aligned with IRS guidance — not what you want to hear. Be skeptical of any attorney who promises you can avoid all tax on pre-move gains. If a strategy sounds too good to be true, it probably is. The 5% toll tax election exists specifically to provide a legitimate path for handling pre-move appreciation. Use it. Tax Law Ventures put it bluntly: "Do not rely on ChatGPT for planning to move to Puerto Rico; there is a lot at stake" (Tax Law Ventures, Jul. 2025).

The Bottom Line

Act 60 remains one of the most powerful tax incentive programs available to U.S. citizens — but 2025 and 2026 have brought a fundamental shift in how both the DDEC and the IRS approach compliance. The DDEC audited 1,798 decrees in a single year. The IRS's GAO-recommended data collection systems are coming online. Criminal prosecutions have been secured. Senate investigations are underway.

The program is not going away — it's been extended through 2055. But the margin for error has shrunk to zero. The days when you could move to Puerto Rico on paper, keep your real life on the mainland, and claim zero taxes are over. The program works extraordinarily well for those who genuinely relocate, maintain proper compliance, and work with qualified professionals. It fails catastrophically for those who cut corners.

Every mistake on this list has been identified in actual DDEC audits, IRS examinations, or criminal cases. None are hypothetical. Get the structure right from the start, maintain meticulous records, and invest in professional guidance. The tax savings under Act 60 are measured in millions — the cost of compliance is measured in thousands. The math is obvious.
