Is Puerto Rico Really Tax-Free? The Truth About PR Tax Benefits in 2026 ¿Es Puerto Rico Realmente Libre de Impuestos? La Verdad Sobre Beneficios Fiscales de PR en 2026

Share this article: Compartir este articulo:

"Puerto Rico is tax-free." You've probably heard this claim countless times—on YouTube, in Reddit threads, from crypto influencers, and from real estate promoters. But is it actually true?

The short answer: No, Puerto Rico is not tax-free. But it does offer some of the most generous tax incentives available to U.S. citizens—if you meet specific, legally enforceable requirements and understand exactly what income is and is not exempt.

In this article, we cut through the marketing hype and give you the numbers, laws, and limitations you need to know. We'll cover the actual tax rates under Act 60, standard Puerto Rico tax brackets, IRS Section 933 rules, and real-world examples showing exactly how much tax you'll actually pay.

Critical Update for 2026: Act 38-2026 was signed by the Governor of Puerto Rico in March 2026, extending the Act 60 program through 2055 and changing the rate structure for new applicants. Applications submitted by December 31, 2026 qualify for the current 0% capital gains tax rate. Applications submitted on or after January 1, 2027 will be subject to a 4% rate on capital gains, dividends, and interest income.

Note: Although signed by the Governor, Act 38-2026 is pending final endorsement from the Puerto Rico Fiscal Oversight and Management Board (PROMESA Board). Applicants should confirm final enactment status with qualified counsel before relying on the extended program terms.

The Reality Check: What "Tax-Free" Actually Means

Puerto Rico is not a tax haven in the traditional sense. It's a U.S. territory with its own local tax authority—the Departamento de Hacienda—and its own Internal Revenue Code. Residents pay Puerto Rico income taxes, property taxes, sales taxes, and municipal taxes.

What Puerto Rico Standard Residents Pay

If you move to Puerto Rico without qualifying for any special tax incentives, you'll be subject to Puerto Rico's standard progressive income tax system with rates ranging from 0% to 33% on net taxable income.

Puerto Rico Standard Individual Income Tax Brackets (2026)

Income Range Tax Rate
$0 - $9,000 0%
$9,001 - $25,000 7%
$25,001 - $41,500 14%
$41,501 - $61,500 25%
Over $61,500 33%

Source: PWC Tax Summaries — Puerto Rico Individual Income (2026); Puerto Rico Internal Revenue Code of 2011, as amended.

Additional Taxes for High Earners: Puerto Rico imposes a Gradual Adjustment Tax of 5% on net taxable income exceeding $500,000, capped at 33% of personal exemptions plus $8,895. There's also an Alternate Basic Tax (ABT) system similar to the U.S. Alternative Minimum Tax that can result in rates up to 24% on income over $250,000.

Example 1: Software Engineer Without Act 60

Scenario: Maria is a remote software engineer earning $150,000/year. She moves to Puerto Rico but does NOT apply for Act 60 benefits. All her income is from Puerto Rico sources (work performed in PR for U.S. clients).

Gross Income:                          $150,000
Personal Exemption (single):           -$3,500
Net Taxable Income:                  $146,500
Progressive bracket calculation:
  $0–$9,000 @ 0%                      = $0
  $9,001–$25,000 @ 7%                 = $1,120
  $25,001–$41,500 @ 14%               = $2,310
  $41,501–$61,500 @ 25%               = $5,000
  $61,501–$146,500 @ 33%              = $28,050
  Subtotal (base brackets):             = $36,480
Estimated PR Income Tax: ~$38,000–$40,000*

*Estimate reflects base progressive brackets (~$36,480) plus applicable Gradual Adjustment Tax and Alternate Basic Tax (ABT) on income over $100,000. Puerto Rico has no standard deduction; only a personal exemption of $3,500 (single filer) applies. Actual liability varies based on individual circumstances and applicable credits. Source: PWC Tax Summaries — Puerto Rico Deductions; PR Internal Revenue Code of 2011.

For comparison, the same income in California would face approximately $43,000+ in combined federal and state income taxes.

Act 60: What the Tax Incentives Actually Offer

Act 60 of 2019 (officially titled the "Puerto Rico Incentives Code") consolidates previous tax incentive laws including the former Act 20 (Export Services) and Act 22 (Individual Investors). It offers genuine tax benefits—but only for specific types of income and only if you meet strict residency and compliance requirements.

Chapter 2: Individual Investors (Former Act 22)

Act 60 Chapter 2 Tax Rates

Capital Gains (post-residency appreciation) 0%*

*For decree holders who applied by December 31, 2026. New applicants on or after January 1, 2027: 4%

Dividends from Puerto Rico sources 0%*

*For decree holders who applied by December 31, 2026. New applicants on or after January 1, 2027: 4%

Interest from Puerto Rico sources 0%*

*For decree holders who applied by December 31, 2026. New applicants on or after January 1, 2027: 4%

Pre-move capital gains (held 10+ years) 5%

Gains that accrued BEFORE moving, recognized after 10 years of bona fide residency (applies to "covered property" under IRC §937(a)(2) and Treasury Reg. 1.937-2(f))

Source: Act 60-2019, Section 2021.01; Act 38-2026 amendments

Example 2: Export Services Business Owner

Scenario: David runs a digital marketing agency providing services to U.S. clients. He forms a Puerto Rico LLC, obtains an Act 60 Chapter 3 decree, and performs all work from Puerto Rico. His business generates $500,000 in net income. Because David's annual business volume is well under $3M, he qualifies for the 2% SMB rate for his first 5 years.

Corporate Level (Years 1–5, SMB Rate):
Net Income from Export Services: $500,000
Corporate Tax (2% SMB rate, Years 1–5): $10,000
After-Tax Profit: $490,000
Corporate Level (Years 6+, Standard Rate):
Net Income from Export Services: $500,000
Corporate Tax (4% rate, Year 6+): $20,000
After-Tax Profit: $480,000
Shareholder Distribution (either scenario):
Dividend to David (bona fide resident): After-tax profit
Personal Tax on Dividend (0%): $0
Total Effective Tax Rate: 2% (Yrs 1–5) / 4% (Yr 6+)

For Comparison: If David operated this business in California, he would pay approximately $127,000 in federal income tax (21% corporate + individual rates on distributions) plus $23,500 in California state tax—total of $150,500 (30% effective rate). Act 60 saves him approximately $130,500–$140,500 per year depending on the applicable year of his decree.

Source: Act 60-2019, Chapter 3; BONN LLC Act 60 Practice Guide; McV Law — Act 60 Practice Guide

IRS Section 933: The Federal Foundation

The reason Act 60 works at all is because of Internal Revenue Code Section 933, which provides that income derived from sources within Puerto Rico by a bona fide resident of Puerto Rico is excluded from U.S. federal gross income and exempt from federal taxation.

IRC Section 933(1): "In the case of an individual who is a bona fide resident of Puerto Rico during the entire taxable year, income derived from sources within Puerto Rico (except amounts received for services performed as an employee of the United States or any agency thereof)..."

26 U.S. Code § 933

Three Critical Limitations of Section 933

1

Bona Fide Residency Requirement

You must be a bona fide resident of Puerto Rico for the entire taxable year to claim the exclusion. This requires:

  • Physical Presence Test: At least 183 days in Puerto Rico during the tax year
  • Tax Home Test: Your principal place of business must be in Puerto Rico
  • Closer Connection Test: You must have closer connections to Puerto Rico than to the U.S. or any foreign country (evaluated based on permanent home, family, bank accounts, driver's license, voter registration, etc.)

Source: IRC Section 937(a); Treasury Regulation § 1.937-1

2

Puerto Rico Source Income Only

Section 933 only excludes income that is sourced to Puerto Rico under federal sourcing rules. U.S.-source income remains fully taxable by the IRS.

  • Wages/Services: Sourced where services are performed. If you perform work in the U.S., it's U.S.-source income.
  • Dividends: Sourced to the residence of the paying corporation. Dividends from U.S. corporations = U.S.-source.
  • Interest: Sourced to the residence of the payor.
  • Capital Gains on Marketable Securities: Generally sourced to the taxpayer's tax home at the time of sale. After 10 years of bona fide PR residency, pre-move appreciation on covered property (IRC §937(a)(2); Treasury Reg. §1.937-2(f)) may qualify for preferential treatment.
  • Non-Marketable Securities (private equity, real estate): Gains are apportioned using a days-of-ownership method, which may produce different results than a simple pre/post-move split. Consult a qualified advisor for asset-specific analysis.

Source: IRC Sections 861–865; IRC §937(a)(2); Treasury Regulation §§ 1.937-2 and 1.937-2(f)

3

Federal Employees Exception

Income received for services performed as an employee of the U.S. government or any federal agency is never excluded under Section 933, even if the services are performed entirely in Puerto Rico. This income remains subject to full U.S. federal income tax.

Source: IRC Section 933(1); IRS Publication 570

Example 3: The Bitcoin Investor Who Got It Right

Scenario: Carlos purchased 10 Bitcoin in 2020 for $100,000 ($10,000 per coin). By December 2025, his Bitcoin is worth $800,000. He moves to Puerto Rico on January 15, 2026, establishes bona fide residency for the full 2026 tax year, and obtains an Act 60 Chapter 2 decree by December 31, 2026. He sells all Bitcoin in March 2027 for $1,200,000.

Basis Allocation:
Original Cost Basis (2020): $100,000
Fair Market Value on Move Date (1/15/26): $800,000
Sale Price (March 2027): $1,200,000
Tax Calculation:
Pre-Move Appreciation ($800K - $100K): $700,000
U.S.-source gain, subject to U.S. federal capital gains tax at 20% long-term rate = $140,000
Post-Move Appreciation ($1.2M - $800K): $400,000
Puerto Rico-source gain, 0% tax under Act 60 Chapter 2 decree = $0
Total Tax Paid: $140,000
Effective Tax Rate on Total Gain: 12.7%

Tax Savings: If Carlos had remained in California and sold in 2027, he would have paid approximately $330,000 in combined federal and state capital gains taxes (30% effective rate on $1.1M gain). By moving to Puerto Rico, he saved $190,000.

Important — 10-Year Lookback: If Carlos holds the Bitcoin for 10 years after establishing bona fide residency, the pre-move gain may qualify for a preferential 5% Puerto Rico rate instead of the standard U.S. rate. This lookback applies to covered property under IRC §937(a)(2) and Treasury Reg. §1.937-2(f). Note that non-marketable securities (private company interests, real estate) use a days-of-ownership apportionment method that may produce different results than the simple pre/post-move split shown above. Consult a qualified advisor for asset-specific analysis.

What You Still Pay: Taxes That Don't Go Away

Even with Act 60, you are not exempt from all taxes. Here's what you'll still pay:

Federal Payroll Taxes

Puerto Rico residents pay the same FICA taxes as mainland residents:

  • Social Security: 6.2% (up to wage base limit)
  • Medicare: 1.45% (no limit)
  • Additional Medicare: 0.9% on wages over $200,000

Self-employed individuals pay 15.3% self-employment tax

Sales and Use Tax (IVU)

Puerto Rico's combined sales tax rate:

  • State Level: 10.5%
  • Municipal Level: 1.0%
  • Total: 11.5% on most goods and services

Higher than most U.S. states

Property Taxes

Property taxes are relatively low (typically 0.5% - 1% of assessed value), but Act 60 Export Services decree holders with volume over $3M only receive a 75% exemption—they still pay 25% of the standard rate.

U.S.-Source Income

Any income from U.S. sources (dividends from U.S. corporations, rental income from mainland properties, services performed in the U.S.) remains subject to full U.S. federal income tax—Section 933 does not apply.

Act 60 Annual Fees & Compliance Costs

Annual compliance costs differ significantly between Chapter 2 and Chapter 3. Make sure you're using the right figures for your situation:

Chapter 2 — Individual Investors

  • Annual report filing fee: $5,005/year
  • Annual charitable donation: $10,000/year minimum to approved PR nonprofits (verify current 2026 amount with DDEC — some sources indicate this may have increased)
  • One-time application fee: $5,005
  • Property purchase: Required within 2 years of decree

Source: Act 60-2019, Section 2021.01; RelocatePuertoRico.com; McV Law

Chapter 3 — Export Services

  • Annual report filing fee: $505/year
  • One-time application fee: Varies by business size
  • CPA-audited financials: Required annually
  • Employment verification: Required if revenue exceeds $3M/year

Source: Act 60-2019, Chapter 3; RelocatePuertoRico.com Annual Report Filing; Cordasco CPA

Additional administrative fees (including a potential one-time acceptance fee) may apply. Verify current DDEC fee schedules at incentivos.ddec.pr.gov before submitting your application.

Estate and Gift Taxes

Puerto Rico has its own estate and gift tax system separate from the federal system. Careful estate planning is essential.

IRS Enforcement: They're Watching

The IRS has launched a specific enforcement campaign targeting Act 60 participants who claim bona fide residency but fail to meet the requirements. A December 2025 GAO report found significant gaps in IRS oversight and recommended enhanced audits.

High-tax states like California, New York, and New Jersey are also scrutinizing Act 60 relocations. California alone saw 381 individuals claim Act 60 benefits in 2021, making it a prime target for state audits.

Documentation You Must Maintain

  • Flight records: Every boarding pass and itinerary showing travel to/from Puerto Rico
  • Property records: Proof of Puerto Rico residence purchase (required within 2 years)
  • Closer connection evidence: PR driver's license, voter registration, bank accounts, utility bills
  • Business records: Proof that services/work are performed in Puerto Rico
  • Form 8898: Filed with IRS when you become/cease to be a bona fide resident (if worldwide income exceeds $75,000)
  • Annual Act 60 reports: Filed with Puerto Rico DDEC, including CPA certification

The Bottom Line: Is Puerto Rico Worth It?

Puerto Rico is not "tax-free." But for the right taxpayer—with the right type of income, proper planning, and genuine commitment to residency—Act 60 can deliver substantial, legal tax savings.

Act 60 Makes Sense If You:

  • Have significant capital gains, dividend income, or business income from export services
  • Can genuinely relocate and spend 183+ days/year in Puerto Rico
  • Run a service business that can be performed remotely for non-PR clients
  • Are willing to maintain meticulous compliance documentation
  • Can afford professional tax and legal counsel

Act 60 Does NOT Make Sense If You:

  • Earn primarily W-2 wages from a U.S. employer (no benefit)
  • Have most income from U.S.-source dividends, rental properties, or other mainland assets
  • Cannot actually spend 183+ days/year in Puerto Rico
  • Are trying to avoid taxes on pre-move capital gains without waiting 10 years
  • Are looking for a "set it and forget it" tax strategy

Professional Guidance Required: Act 60 is not a DIY tax strategy. Between IRS Section 933 sourcing rules, bona fide residency tests, Puerto Rico compliance requirements, and state tax exit strategies, there are dozens of ways to get it wrong. Work with a qualified Puerto Rico CPA and tax attorney who specialize in Act 60 compliance.
