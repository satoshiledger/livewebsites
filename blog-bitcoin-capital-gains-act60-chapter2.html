Bitcoin Capital Gains at 0%: The Chapter 2 Investor Playbook for Long-Term Holders Ganancias de Capital en Bitcoin al 0%: La Estrategia del Capítulo 2 para Holders a Largo Plazo

If you're a long-term Bitcoin holder sitting on unrealized gains, you've likely faced this sobering reality: selling in the United States means handing over 20% to 37% of your profit to federal and state taxes. For someone with $2 million in Bitcoin gains, that's up to $740,000 in tax liability—money that could stay in your portfolio if you structure your residency correctly.

Puerto Rico's Act 60, Chapter 2 (formerly Act 22) offers a legal pathway to 0% capital gains tax on Bitcoin acquired and sold after establishing bona fide Puerto Rico residency. This isn't a loophole—it's a congressionally sanctioned tax framework rooted in IRC Section 933 that allows U.S. citizens to exclude Puerto Rico-sourced income from federal taxation while remaining American citizens.

This article provides the complete technical playbook: the exact legal provisions, residency requirements, timing strategies, and compliance protocols that Bitcoin investors must follow to achieve 0% capital gains tax legally and defensibly.

Critical 2026 Update: House Bill 505

As of January 1, 2026, new Act 60 Chapter 2 applicants will face a 4% tax on capital gains, interest, and dividends under pending reform legislation. However, applications submitted on or before December 31, 2025, are grandfathered under the existing 0% rate for the life of the decree—potentially 15 years with a renewal option.

While this article focuses on the legal framework applicable to current decree holders and those grandfathered under the 0% regime, prospective applicants should consult with qualified Puerto Rico tax counsel immediately to understand how HB 505 impacts their specific situation.

The Legal Foundation: Three Laws Working Together

1. IRS Notice 2014-21: Bitcoin as Property

The IRS issued Notice 2014-21 in March 2014, establishing that "convertible virtual currency is treated as property for federal tax purposes." This means Bitcoin sales are capital transactions subject to capital gains tax rates—not ordinary income rates. For long-term holders (assets held over one year), federal rates reach 20% plus 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax, totaling 23.8% before state taxes.

2. IRC Section 933: The Puerto Rico Exclusion

IRC Section 933 provides that "in the case of an individual who is a bona fide resident of Puerto Rico during the entire taxable year, income derived from sources within Puerto Rico" is excluded from U.S. federal gross income. This exclusion is not a credit or deduction—it's a complete exemption from federal taxation on Puerto Rico-sourced income.

Under Treasury Regulation 1.937-2, capital gains from the sale of personal property (including Bitcoin) are sourced based on the taxpayer's residence. If you are a bona fide Puerto Rico resident, your Bitcoin gains are Puerto Rico-sourced—and therefore exempt from federal tax under IRC 933.

3. Act 60 Chapter 2 Section 2021.01: 0% Puerto Rico Tax

Act 60-2019, Section 2021.01(b) provides that "the total net capital gain generated by a Resident Individual Investor related to any appreciation of Securities or other Assets after said Resident Individual Investor becomes a Resident Individual of Puerto Rico, that is recognized before January 1, 2036, shall be fully exempt from income taxes in Puerto Rico."

The law defines "Assets" to include "digital assets based on blockchain technology"—explicitly covering Bitcoin. This means Bitcoin acquired after establishing Puerto Rico residency is subject to 0% Puerto Rico tax and 0% federal tax, resulting in complete tax exemption.

Three Real-World Bitcoin Scenarios

1

Scenario 1: The Clean Post-Relocation Buyer

Facts: Maria establishes bona fide Puerto Rico residency on July 1, 2026. On August 15, 2026, she purchases 5 BTC at $60,000 each ($300,000 total). On January 10, 2028, she sells all 5 BTC at $100,000 each ($500,000 total), realizing a $200,000 gain.

Tax Treatment: Zero. Maria held the Bitcoin for over one year (long-term) and acquired it after becoming a Puerto Rico resident. Under Section 2021.01(b), the entire $200,000 gain is exempt from Puerto Rico tax. Under IRC 933, it's exempt from federal tax.

Result: Maria keeps the full $200,000. A California resident in the 37% federal bracket plus 13.3% state tax would owe $100,600 on the same gain.

2

Scenario 2: The Pre-Move Holder (10-Year Strategy)

Facts: David bought 10 BTC in 2020 at $10,000 each ($100,000 cost basis) while living in New York. By January 1, 2026, when he establishes Puerto Rico residency, the Bitcoin is worth $50,000 per coin ($500,000 total value). He holds for 10+ years and sells in 2037 at $120,000 per coin ($1,200,000 total).

Tax Treatment: Under Section 2021.01(a), the pre-move appreciation ($400,000) recognized after 10 years of Puerto Rico residency is taxed at 5% = $20,000. The post-move appreciation ($700,000) is taxed at 0%. Total Puerto Rico tax: $20,000. Federal tax: $0 under IRC 933.

Result: David pays $20,000 on $1,100,000 in gains (1.8% effective rate). Staying in New York would have cost him approximately $550,000 in federal and state taxes—a savings of $530,000.

3

Scenario 3: The Failed Residency (Audit Catastrophe)

Facts: James obtains an Act 60 decree and claims Puerto Rico residency for 2026. He spends 150 days in Puerto Rico, 180 days in Texas, and maintains his Texas business, bank accounts, and family home. He sells 8 BTC for a $600,000 gain and reports $0 tax.

IRS Response: James fails the presence test (less than 183 days) and the closer connection test (primary ties remain in Texas). The IRS determines he's not a bona fide Puerto Rico resident and assesses federal capital gains tax of $142,800 plus penalties up to 25% ($35,700) plus interest.

Result: James owes $178,500+ and loses his Act 60 decree. Texas also assesses penalties for false residency claims. This is why meticulous compliance documentation is non-negotiable.

The Three Non-Negotiable Residency Tests

To qualify for IRC Section 933 exclusion, you must be a "bona fide resident" of Puerto Rico for the entire taxable year under IRC Section 937. This requires passing all three tests:

Test 1: The Presence Test (183 Days Minimum)

You must be physically present in Puerto Rico for at least 183 days during the taxable year. The IRS counts every single day—partial days do not count. Alternative safe harbors exist (549-day rule across three years, or less than 90 days in the U.S. with under $3,000 U.S. earned income), but the 183-day rule is the most straightforward.

Documentation Required: Boarding passes, flight itineraries, passport stamps, hotel receipts, credit card statements showing Puerto Rico transactions, cell phone location data, utility bills, and calendar logs. The IRS has launched targeted audits of Act 60 participants—incomplete documentation is a red flag.

Test 2: The Tax Home Test

Your "tax home" (principal place of business or employment) must be in Puerto Rico. For Bitcoin investors with no active business, this means demonstrating that Puerto Rico is your center of economic activity. Opening a Puerto Rico bank account, establishing a Puerto Rico LLC for business activities, and conducting investment management from Puerto Rico all strengthen this test.

Test 3: The Closer Connection Test

This is the most scrutinized test. You must demonstrate that you have a closer connection to Puerto Rico than to any U.S. state or foreign country. The IRS examines: where your family lives, where you vote, where you hold a driver's license, where you own a home (you must purchase Puerto Rico property within two years under Act 60), where your bank accounts are, where you belong to social/religious organizations, and where you hold professional licenses.

High-Risk Red Flag: Maintaining your mainland home, keeping your children in mainland schools, or spending more than 100 days annually in any single state can trigger IRS scrutiny. States like California and New York aggressively challenge residency changes.

Act 60 Chapter 2 Compliance Requirements

Obtaining the tax benefits requires formal decree approval and ongoing annual compliance. Here's what's required:

Application Filing Fee

$5,750 total ($750 initial application fee + $5,000 upon approval). Applications are submitted through Puerto Rico's Department of Economic Development and Commerce (DDEC) Single Business Portal.

Annual Charitable Contribution

$10,000 minimum per year to IRS-recognized 501(c)(3) organizations operating in Puerto Rico. At least $5,000 must go to organizations addressing child poverty. This is not optional—failure to contribute can invalidate your decree.

Real Property Purchase Requirement

Within two years of receiving your decree, you must purchase residential real property in Puerto Rico for use as your principal residence. The property must be titled in your name (or jointly with your spouse). Renting long-term does not satisfy this requirement.

Annual Report Filing Fee

$5,000 per year due November 15th. The 2026 compliance portal now requires certified CPA attestation letters, proof of charitable contributions, residency documentation, and detailed breakdowns of income sources and asset holdings (including Bitcoin wallet addresses and transaction histories for crypto investors).

Form 8898 Filing

U.S. citizens claiming bona fide Puerto Rico residency must file IRS Form 8898 ("Statement for Individuals Who Begin or End Bona Fide Residence in a U.S. Possession") with their federal tax return for the year residency begins. This form puts the IRS on notice of your residency change and is a compliance checkpoint.

Critical Timing Strategies for Bitcoin Holders

Strategy 1: The Date-Stamping Requirement

The day you become a bona fide Puerto Rico resident is your "date stamp" for determining which Bitcoin holdings qualify for 0% tax. You must be able to prove—with blockchain records and exchange statements—exactly which Bitcoin you owned before that date (subject to 5% tax after 10 years or normal PR rates otherwise) versus which Bitcoin you acquired afterward (0% tax).

Best Practice: On January 1st of the year you establish residency, generate a complete portfolio snapshot with screenshots, exchange statements, and wallet addresses showing cost basis and acquisition dates for every Bitcoin position. Have a CPA certify this documentation and store it with your permanent tax records.

Strategy 2: The December 31, 2035 Deadline

Act 60 Chapter 2 benefits expire on December 31, 2035. Bitcoin sold after that date is subject to standard Puerto Rico capital gains rates (currently 10-15% depending on holding period). If you're planning long-term Bitcoin accumulation under Act 60, you have approximately 10 years remaining to realize gains under the 0% regime (or longer if HB 505 extension provisions apply to existing decree holders).

Strategy 3: The Year-of-Move Exception

Under Treasury Regulation 1.937-1(f)(1), special rules apply for your year of arrival. You can establish residency mid-year if you meet the closer connection test and spend the majority of days in Puerto Rico for the remainder of the year. However, capital gains realized before you establish residency are taxed at mainland rates. Most advisors recommend establishing residency by January 1st to maximize the 0% tax window.

Five Expensive Mistakes Bitcoin Investors Make

Mistake #1: Claiming Residency Without Meeting 183 Days

This is the most common audit trigger. Some investors believe they can "split" residency between Puerto Rico and their mainland state. You cannot. You must be a bona fide Puerto Rico resident for the entire taxable year, which means 183+ days physically present.

Mistake #2: Selling Pre-Move Bitcoin Within 10 Years

Bitcoin you owned before establishing Puerto Rico residency does not qualify for 0% tax unless you hold it for 10+ years after becoming a resident. Selling earlier triggers regular Puerto Rico capital gains tax (10-20% depending on holding period).

Mistake #3: Poor Record-Keeping

The IRS is targeting Act 60 participants with document requests. You must maintain boarding passes, passport stamps, travel logs, bank statements, utility bills, and Puerto Rico presence calendars for at least 6 years. Missing documentation equals failed audit.

Mistake #4: Forgetting About FBAR and Form 8938

U.S. citizens living in Puerto Rico are still subject to foreign financial account reporting if they hold Bitcoin on non-U.S. exchanges. Failing to file FinCEN Form 114 (FBAR) or Form 8938 can result in penalties up to $10,000 per violation.

Mistake #5: Ignoring State Tax Nexus

California, New York, and other high-tax states aggressively challenge residency changes. If you maintain property, business operations, or family in your former state, that state may claim you remain a resident and attempt to tax your Bitcoin gains. Obtain a formal residency determination letter from your former state tax authority.

What Happens After 2035? The Extension Question

Current Act 60 Chapter 2 decrees expire on December 31, 2035. However, pending legislative proposals (including elements of HB 505) suggest the program may be extended to 2055 with modified terms. For existing decree holders, key questions remain:

  • Will existing 0% decree holders be grandfathered beyond 2035, or will they transition to a 4% rate?
  • Can existing decrees be renewed for an additional 15-year term at current rates?
  • How will changes to global minimum tax frameworks (OECD Pillar Two) impact Puerto Rico's tax incentives?

Planning Guidance: Current decree holders should assume the 0% rate remains available through 2035 and plan Bitcoin liquidation strategies accordingly. Work with Puerto Rico tax counsel to monitor legislative developments and structure holding strategies that maximize tax benefits under current law while remaining flexible for potential reforms.

The Bottom Line for Bitcoin Long-Term Holders

Act 60 Chapter 2 is the only legal mechanism for U.S. citizens to achieve 0% tax on Bitcoin capital gains without renouncing citizenship. The tax savings are extraordinary—potentially hundreds of thousands to millions of dollars for large holders—but the compliance requirements are strict and the IRS audit risk is real.
