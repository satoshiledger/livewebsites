Moving to Puerto Rico for Bitcoin Tax Savings: A Step-by-Step Relocation Blueprint Mudarse a Puerto Rico por Ahorro Fiscal de Bitcoin: Plan de Reubicación Paso a Paso

Share this article: Compartir este articulo:

If you hold a significant amount of Bitcoin and you're staring down a six- or seven-figure federal tax bill on your unrealized gains, you're not alone. Thousands of Bitcoin investors across the mainland United States are exploring a legal, well-established path to dramatically reduce—or even eliminate—their capital gains tax burden: relocating to Puerto Rico under Act 60.

Puerto Rico is a United States territory with the authority to set its own tax policies. Under Act 60 (officially the Puerto Rico Incentives Code, enacted July 1, 2019), bona fide residents who obtain a tax exemption decree can pay as little as 0% on capital gains from assets acquired after establishing residency—and that includes Bitcoin (Act 60-2019, Section 2021.01(a)). For new applicants in 2026, the rate is 4% following the passage of House Bill 505, but even that remains a fraction of the 20%+ federal rate on the mainland (News is My Business, "Puerto Rico House Approves 4% Tax Rate for Future Act 60 Investors").

But this is not a vacation plan—it's a full-life relocation that the IRS is watching closely. This guide walks you through every step, from evaluating whether the move makes financial sense, to passing the bona fide residency test, to staying compliant once you're on the island. We'll also cover the toll tax on pre-move gains, the recent Gajwani prosecution, and the documentation you need to protect yourself from an audit.

Why Bitcoin Investors Are Moving to Puerto Rico

The federal tax treatment of Bitcoin is straightforward—and expensive. The IRS classifies Bitcoin as property, meaning every sale, swap, or disposition triggers a taxable event (IRS Notice 2014-21). Long-term capital gains are taxed at 15% or 20% depending on your income level, and short-term gains are taxed as ordinary income at rates up to 37%. Add in the 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT) and state taxes in places like California or New York, and your effective rate can exceed 50%.

Puerto Rico offers a legal exit from this structure. Under IRC Section 933, bona fide residents of Puerto Rico can exclude Puerto Rico-sourced income from their U.S. federal tax return (IRS Publication 570). When combined with an Act 60 decree, capital gains on Bitcoin acquired after establishing residency are taxed at 0% for pre-2026 decree holders or 4% for those who apply starting January 1, 2026 (Act 60-2019, Section 2021.01(a); HB 505).

Tax Savings Example

Suppose you purchase 10 BTC after establishing residency in Puerto Rico. Over two years, the value appreciates by $500,000. On the mainland, at a combined federal-plus-state rate of roughly 30%, you would owe approximately $150,000 in tax. Under an Act 60 decree at the new 4% rate, you would owe $20,000—a savings of $130,000. Under a pre-2026 decree at 0%, you would owe nothing.

Note: This example is illustrative only. Actual tax outcomes depend on individual circumstances, income levels, and compliance with all Act 60 and IRS requirements. Consult a qualified tax advisor.

The key distinction is that only gains on Bitcoin acquired after you establish bona fide residency qualify for the Act 60 exemption. Gains on Bitcoin you owned before the move remain subject to U.S. taxation, though a separate toll tax election may apply (more on that below). This timing distinction is critical and has been at the center of multiple IRS enforcement actions.

The 2026 Rule Change: HB 505 and the New 4% Rate

Puerto Rico's legislature passed House Bill 505 in 2025, making several important changes to the Act 60 Individual Resident Investor program. The most significant for Bitcoin investors: starting January 1, 2026, new decree applicants face a 4% tax rate on Puerto Rico-sourced capital gains, interest, and dividends—replacing the previous 0% rate (News is My Business, "Puerto Rico House Approves 4% Tax Rate for Future Act 60 Investors").

Those who submitted their decree application before December 31, 2025 are grandfathered under the original 0% rate for the life of their decree—up to 15 years, with a possible 15-year extension (Act 60-2019, Section 2021.01). Additionally, HB 505 extended the Act 60 program through 2055, providing long-term planning certainty for investors (Carrasquillo Law Group, "Client Alert: Potential Changes to Puerto Rico's Incentives Code").

New Residency Requirement for 2026 Applicants

HB 505 also introduced a new prior-residency requirement: to qualify for the Act 60 Individual Investor decree, applicants must demonstrate that they were not a resident of Puerto Rico for the six years immediately prior to their application (PRelocate, "Puerto Rico Legislature Approves Act 60 Extension and New Tax Measures"). This is designed to prevent abuse by longtime island residents who might attempt to "re-enter" the program.

Even at 4%, Puerto Rico's tax rate on investment income remains dramatically lower than the combined federal-and-state rates on the mainland, which can exceed 30–50% depending on where you live. For a Bitcoin investor holding a seven-figure portfolio, the annual savings remain substantial.

Step-by-Step Bitcoin Relocation Blueprint

Moving to Puerto Rico for Bitcoin tax savings is a multi-step process that requires careful planning, professional guidance, and genuine commitment. Here is a comprehensive timeline.

Step 1: Evaluate Your Financial Position (Months 1–2)

Before committing to a relocation, conduct a thorough cost-benefit analysis. The tax savings must outweigh the costs of moving, including the Act 60 application fees, the requirement to purchase real property within two years, the $10,000 annual charitable donation, and ongoing professional fees for tax compliance. If your combined tax savings are under $50,000 per year, the economics may not justify the move.

Critically, engage a Puerto Rico-licensed CPA and a tax attorney who specialize in Act 60 planning before you sell any assets or initiate the move. Establish your Bitcoin cost basis as of the date you plan to relocate—this number will determine the split between pre-move gains (taxable) and post-move gains (exempt or taxed at 4%).

Step 2: Establish Your Cost Basis Snapshot (Month 2)

The IRS treats Bitcoin as property, and capital gains are sourced based on the taxpayer's residence at the time of sale (IRC Section 865; IRS Revenue Ruling 2019-24). This means you must establish the fair market value of your Bitcoin holdings as of the date you become a bona fide Puerto Rico resident. Any appreciation after that date is Puerto Rico-sourced; any appreciation before it is U.S.-sourced.

Document everything: exchange CSV exports, transaction IDs (TXIDs), USD spot rates on each acquisition date, and wallet addresses. Separate each lot into "pre-move" and "post-move" categories. The IRS has made clear through recent enforcement actions that it will not accept shortcuts on this documentation (Holland & Knight, "Puerto Rico's Act 60, Income Sourcing and IRS Scrutiny in the Age of Cryptocurrency").

Step 3: Physically Relocate to Puerto Rico (Months 3–4)

This is where many investors stumble. Relocating means genuinely moving your life to Puerto Rico—not simply renting an apartment and visiting occasionally. You need to transfer your primary residence, open local bank accounts, obtain a Puerto Rico driver's license, register to vote if eligible, and establish meaningful social and community ties on the island.

If you have a spouse and family, ideally they relocate with you. The IRS will compare your connections to Puerto Rico against your connections to the mainland. Maintaining a primary home, your children's schooling, or your country club membership stateside can undermine your residency claim. IRS Publication 570 and Treasury Regulation Section 1.937-1 provide the detailed framework for evaluating these "closer connection" factors.

Step 4: Apply for Your Act 60 Decree (Months 3–5)

Once you are on the island and establishing ties, submit your Individual Resident Investor decree application through the Department of Economic Development and Commerce (DDEC) Single Business Portal. Processing times generally range from 3 to 6 months, though timelines vary.

The decree, once issued, is a binding contract between you and the Government of Puerto Rico. It cannot be unilaterally modified, which provides a layer of legal certainty. However, it requires ongoing compliance—miss a requirement, and the decree can be suspended or revoked.

Step 5: File IRS Form 8898 (Year of Move)

In the tax year you establish bona fide residency, you must file IRS Form 8898 (Statement for Individuals Who Begin or End Bona Fide Residence in a U.S. Territory) if your worldwide gross income exceeds $75,000 (IRS Instructions for Form 8898, Rev. October 2024). This form is filed separately—it does not accompany your Form 1040. Failure to file can result in a $1,000 penalty and, more importantly, can raise questions about the legitimacy of your residency claim (Rachel Farris CPA, "Form 8898 and Puerto Rico's Act 60").

Step 6: Purchase Real Property in Puerto Rico (Within 2 Years)

Act 60 requires that you purchase real property in Puerto Rico within two years of receiving your decree and use it as your primary residence (Act 60-2019, Section 2021.01(b)). This is not optional—it is a condition of keeping your decree active. It also serves as strong evidence that your relocation is genuine, which matters in the event of an IRS audit.

Step 7: Begin Acquiring Post-Move Bitcoin (After Residency Established)

Only after you are a bona fide resident should you begin purchasing Bitcoin that you intend to benefit from under your Act 60 decree. Gains on Bitcoin acquired and sold while you are a qualifying resident will be taxed at 0% (pre-2026 decrees) or 4% (2026+ decrees) rather than at federal rates.

Some investors choose to sell their pre-move Bitcoin before or shortly after relocating (paying the applicable federal tax or electing the toll tax), and then repurchase Bitcoin once residency is established. This "clean break" strategy simplifies the pre-move/post-move accounting, but it triggers a taxable event on the sale. Consult your CPA before executing this approach.

The Bona Fide Residency Test: Three Tests You Must Pass

The IRS determines whether you qualify as a bona fide Puerto Rico resident through three tests defined under IRC Section 937 and Treasury Regulation Section 1.937-1. You must satisfy all three in every tax year you claim Act 60 benefits:

1. Presence Test

Spend at least 183 days physically present in Puerto Rico during the tax year. Alternative safe harbors exist (such as no more than 90 days in the mainland U.S. while earning less than $3,000 from U.S. sources, or 549 days over three years with at least 60 days each year), but the 183-day rule is the most straightforward path.

2. Tax Home Test

Your principal place of business or employment must be in Puerto Rico. If you do not have a principal place of business, your primary residence serves as your tax home. The key is that you cannot maintain a tax home outside Puerto Rico at any point during the tax year.

3. Closer Connection Test

Your primary personal, economic, and social ties must be in Puerto Rico rather than the mainland U.S. or any foreign country. The IRS evaluates factors including where your permanent home is located, where your family lives, where you bank, where you are registered to vote, and where you hold your driver's license.

IRS Enforcement Is Real: The Gajwani Case

In June 2025, Miami investor Suresh Gajwani pleaded guilty to filing a false statement with the IRS in connection with Act 60 tax incentives. According to the Department of Justice, Gajwani submitted false corporate conversion documents in an attempt to shelter approximately $30 million in pre-move capital gains from taxation. He agreed to pay approximately $15.3 million in restitution and faced up to three years in prison (U.S. Department of Justice, "Investor Pleads Guilty to Filing False Form with IRS").

The Gajwani case was the first federal criminal prosecution to emerge from the IRS's dedicated Act 60 audit campaign, which began in January 2021. The IRS has stated that it identified approximately 100 individuals claiming Act 60 benefits who did not appear to meet residency requirements, and more criminal investigations are expected (Holland & Knight, "Puerto Rico's Act 60, Income Sourcing and IRS Scrutiny").

The 5% Toll Tax on Pre-Move Bitcoin Gains

One of the most misunderstood provisions of Act 60 concerns Bitcoin (or any appreciated asset) that you owned before relocating to Puerto Rico. Under Act 60 Section 2021.01(e), there is an elective toll tax that allows you to pay a flat 5% on the unrealized gains that accrued prior to your move. By making this election, you lock in a lower rate on the pre-move appreciation and effectively "reset" your cost basis to the fair market value as of the date you established residency.

If you choose not to make the toll tax election, you must apportion gains when you eventually sell. The pre-move portion (appreciation from original purchase date to residency establishment date) is treated as U.S.-sourced income and taxed at standard federal rates. The post-move portion (appreciation from residency date to sale date) qualifies for Act 60 treatment.

Toll Tax Calculation Example

Imagine you purchased 5 BTC at $20,000 each ($100,000 total). On the date you establish PR residency, BTC is worth $80,000 each ($400,000 total). Your pre-move gain is $300,000.

With toll tax election: You pay 5% × $300,000 = $15,000 to Puerto Rico. Your cost basis resets to $400,000. Any future appreciation is taxed at the Act 60 rate (0% or 4%).

Without toll tax election: When you eventually sell, you must calculate what portion of total gain was pre-move (taxed at federal rates) and what portion was post-move (taxed at Act 60 rates). If BTC has risen significantly post-move, the pre-move portion may still face standard U.S. capital gains rates of 20%+ on the $300,000.

The toll tax election is particularly relevant for Bitcoin holders with large unrealized gains. Whether to elect it depends on the size of your pre-move gains, your expected holding period, and your marginal federal tax rate. This is a decision that should be made in close consultation with your CPA, as Treasury Regulation Section 1.937-2 contains a 10-year lookback rule for certain property owned prior to establishing PR residency.

Ongoing Compliance: What You Must Do Every Year

Receiving your Act 60 decree is not the finish line—it's the starting line. You must maintain compliance every year or risk losing your benefits. Here are the key annual requirements:

1

Pass the Bona Fide Residency Test Every Year

Maintain 183+ days in Puerto Rico, keep your tax home on the island, and demonstrate closer connections to PR than any other jurisdiction. Track your travel meticulously with boarding passes, flight records, and digital calendar logs.

2

Make the $10,000 Annual Charitable Donation

You must donate $10,000 each year to an approved Puerto Rico nonprofit organization. At least 50% must go to organizations focused on combating child poverty (Act 60-2019, Section 2021.01(b)(4)). Keep receipts and verification of nonprofit status.

3

File Your Annual Report with DDEC

The DDEC requires an annual compliance report from every decree holder. Failure to file can result in fines up to $1,000 per violation and, in serious cases, decree suspension (DDEC October 2025 Administrative Release; Act 60 Chapter 12).

4

File Puerto Rico Form 482

As a bona fide PR resident, you must file a Puerto Rico income tax return (Form 482) reporting your worldwide income, then deducting Act 60-exempt gains. Due date is April 15, with extensions available to October 15.

5

File U.S. Form 1040 (If Applicable)

You may still need to file a U.S. federal return if you have U.S.-sourced income, pre-move capital gains, or foreign-sourced income. PR-sourced income exempt under IRC Section 933 is excluded from your 1040, but you must still report everything else (IRS Topic 901; IRS Publication 570).

6

Maintain Your Real Property

Keep the PR property you purchased as your primary residence. Selling it without replacing it—or failing to purchase one within the two-year window—can jeopardize your decree.

2026 Enhanced Reporting for Crypto Holders

Starting in 2026, Puerto Rico's new Act 60 compliance portal requires enhanced annual reporting for cryptocurrency holders, including disclosure of wallet addresses, transaction histories, and asset holdings to verify that capital gains were generated post-residency (Windham Brannon, "IRS Crackdown and Puerto Rico Law Changes"). Maintain meticulous records of every acquisition, sale, and transfer from Day One.

Common Mistakes Bitcoin Investors Make When Relocating

Mistake #1: Treating the Move as a "Paper Relocation"

Buying a condo and visiting a few times a year does not make you a bona fide resident. The IRS has specifically flagged this pattern, noting that approximately half of Act 22/60 beneficiaries may not comply with residency requirements. Your relocation must be genuine—family, business operations, social life, and financial accounts should all center on Puerto Rico.

Mistake #2: Claiming Pre-Move Bitcoin Gains as Exempt

Only gains on Bitcoin acquired after establishing residency qualify for Act 60 treatment. The IRS has prosecuted taxpayers who attempted to source pre-move gains to Puerto Rico, as the Gajwani case demonstrates. Do not rely on aggressive legal opinions that claim otherwise—IRS Chief Counsel Memorandum 202538025 has explicitly rejected such positions.

Mistake #3: Using Entities to Re-Characterize Income

Some investors have attempted to route Bitcoin through S corporations or partnerships to convert U.S.-sourced income into Puerto Rico-sourced income. The IRS has explicitly warned that it will use judicial doctrines—including substance-over-form, economic substance, and sham transaction doctrines—to recast these arrangements (Holland & Knight, "Puerto Rico's Act 60, Income Sourcing and IRS Scrutiny").

Mistake #4: Failing to Document the 183-Day Requirement

Even if you actually spend 183+ days in Puerto Rico, you need proof. The IRS will request boarding passes, flight itineraries, lease agreements, utility bills, cell phone location data, and other evidence. Maintain a contemporaneous travel log from the day you move—retroactive reconstruction is risky and unconvincing.

Mistake #5: Ignoring the Charitable Donation Requirement

The $10,000 annual donation to approved Puerto Rico nonprofits is not optional. Missing it—or routing it to a questionable organization—can result in fines and, in extreme cases, decree revocation. Ensure your chosen nonprofit is certified and that at least half of your donation supports organizations combating child poverty.

Is This Move Right for You? A Decision Framework

Relocating to Puerto Rico is a significant life decision that extends well beyond tax planning. Before committing, consider the following factors:

Good Candidate If...

Your annual tax savings exceed $50,000–$100,000

You work remotely or run a location-independent business

You are genuinely willing to live in Puerto Rico full-time

You have significant unrealized Bitcoin gains with a long time horizon

You can commit to the compliance and documentation burden

May Not Be Right If...

Your tax savings would be modest relative to moving costs

Your business or job requires you to be on the mainland frequently

You have children in mainland schools or aging parents to care for

You are unwilling to maintain 183+ days per year on the island

You plan to sell pre-move Bitcoin (those gains remain U.S.-taxable)

Puerto Rico offers a genuinely attractive tax environment for Bitcoin investors—but it is not a loophole and it is not automatic. The IRS is actively auditing Act 60 participants, multiple criminal cases are proceeding, and congressional scrutiny continues to increase. Those who follow the rules, maintain impeccable documentation, and commit to genuine residency stand to benefit enormously. Those who cut corners risk criminal prosecution and millions in penalties.
