Moving to Puerto Rico for Bitcoin Tax Savings: A Step-by-Step Relocation Blueprint Mudarse a Puerto Rico por Ahorro Fiscal de Bitcoin: Plan de Reubicación Paso a Paso

If you're holding Bitcoin with substantial unrealized gains, the U.S. federal capital gains tax can take a significant bite out of your profits. Long-term gains face a 20% federal rate at the top bracket, plus a 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT) for taxpayers above the $200,000 single / $250,000 married income threshold. Add state taxes in a high-tax state like California (13.3%) and your combined effective rate can approach or exceed 37% — meaning a California resident selling $1 million in long-term Bitcoin gains could owe approximately $371,000 in combined taxes (federal 20% + NIIT 3.8% + CA 13.3%). Note: the NIIT applies only above income thresholds; not all taxpayers are subject to it (IRS Publication 550; IRC Section 1411).

Puerto Rico offers a legal, IRS-approved alternative. Under Chapter 2 of Act 60-2019 (formerly Act 22), bona fide residents of Puerto Rico pay zero percent tax on capital gains from Bitcoin acquired after establishing residency. This isn't a loophole — it's explicit U.S. tax law under Section 933 of the Internal Revenue Code.

But moving to Puerto Rico isn't as simple as buying a condo and filing paperwork. The IRS scrutinizes Puerto Rico relocations, and getting it wrong can trigger audits, penalties, and loss of all tax benefits. This guide walks you through the exact steps to relocate to Puerto Rico for Bitcoin tax savings — legally, strategically, and with full IRS compliance.

Understanding Act 60 Chapter 2: The Individual Investor Tax Incentive

Chapter 2 of Act 60-2019 provides a 100% tax exemption from Puerto Rico income taxes on certain capital gains, interest, and dividends earned after you become a bona fide resident of Puerto Rico. The program applies to individual investors who relocate on or before December 31, 2055 — the sunset was officially extended from 2035 to 2055 when Act 38-2026 was signed into law in March 2026 (Grant Thornton PR; Act 38-2026).

Bitcoin-Specific Tax Treatment

For Bitcoin held as a long-term investment (not trading inventory), gains are sourced to your tax home at the time of sale. Under Section 865(a)(2) of the Internal Revenue Code, if you sell Bitcoin after becoming a bona fide Puerto Rico resident, those gains are Puerto Rico-sourced — meaning they escape U.S. federal tax under Section 933 and are exempt from Puerto Rico tax under your Act 60 decree (IRC Section 933; IRC Section 865).

Important: Bitcoin acquired before you move is subject to the 10-year rule. Gains accrued before establishing residency remain subject to U.S. tax if realized within 10 years of your move. After holding for 10 years as a Puerto Rico resident, pre-move appreciation is eligible for a 5% Puerto Rico tax rate under Act 60, Section 2021.01(e).

🆕 Act 38-2026: Now Law — 0% Window Extended Through December 31, 2026

Act 38-2026 was signed into law, officially amending Act 60-2019's Individual Investor program. The original December 31, 2025 deadline for locking in the 0% rate has been superseded. Under Act 38-2026, investors who submit their Individual Investor decree application on or before December 31, 2026 are grandfathered at the 0% rate on post-residency capital gains, interest, and dividends (Grant Thornton PR; Act 38-2026).

Applications submitted on or after January 1, 2027 will face a 4% tax on Puerto Rico-sourced capital gains, interest, and dividends — still dramatically lower than mainland rates, but a change from the 0% regime that existed since Act 22 in 2012.

Act 38-2026 also permanently extends the program through December 31, 2055 (previously 2035), adds a 6-year prior non-residency requirement for applicants after 2026, and tightens property ownership rules for those same applicants. Note: Act 38-2026 is pending final endorsement from Puerto Rico's Fiscal Oversight and Management Board (FOMB) — a standard procedural step that does not affect the operative rules (Procopio).

Step 1: Pass the IRS Bona Fide Residency Test

To qualify for Act 60 tax benefits, you must be a bona fide resident of Puerto Rico under IRS rules. This is not a Puerto Rico law requirement — it's a federal tax requirement enforced by the IRS under IRC Section 937 and 26 CFR §1.937-1. You must pass all three tests:

1 Presence Test

You must satisfy ONE of these conditions during the tax year (26 CFR §1.937-1(c); IRS Publication 570):

  • Present in Puerto Rico for at least 183 days during the tax year
  • Present for at least 549 days over a 3-year period (current + 2 prior years), with a minimum of 60 days each year
  • Present in the U.S. for no more than 90 days during the tax year
  • More days in Puerto Rico than in the U.S. AND U.S. earned income not exceeding $3,000

Source: 26 CFR §1.937-1(c) and IRS Publication 570. Note: "No significant connection to the U.S." is a factor in the Closer Connection Test (Test 3 below), not the Presence Test.

2 Tax Home Test

Your tax home must be in Puerto Rico for the entire tax year. This is your principal place of business or employment. You cannot maintain a tax home outside Puerto Rico during the tax year. The IRS looks at where you conduct the majority of your business activities, not just where you sleep (26 CFR §1.937-1(d); IRS Publication 570).

3 Closer Connection Test

You must have a closer connection to Puerto Rico than to the U.S. or any foreign country. The IRS evaluates the following factors under 26 CFR §1.937-1(e):

  • Location of your permanent home
  • Where your family resides
  • Where you vote and hold a driver's license
  • Where you conduct personal banking
  • Location of social, cultural, and religious affiliations
  • No significant connection to the U.S. (no permanent home, voter registration, or primary family ties in the U.S.)

Source: 26 CFR §1.937-1(e) and IRS Publication 570

IRS Audit Risk: The "Snowbird" Trap

The IRS actively audits Puerto Rico relocations. If you maintain significant ties to the mainland (keeping your primary home, spending substantial time in the U.S., or conducting business primarily stateside), the IRS can disallow your bona fide residency. This is an "all or nothing" test — fail any one part, and you lose all tax benefits for that year.

Step 2: File IRS Form 8898 — Notify the IRS of Your Residency Change

Once you become a bona fide resident of Puerto Rico, you must file IRS Form 8898 (Statement for Individuals Who Begin or End Bona Fide Residence in a U.S. Territory) for the tax year you establish residency (IRS Form 8898 Instructions, Rev. October 2024).

Filing Requirements

  • Who Must File: U.S. citizens or resident aliens who become or cease to be bona fide residents of Puerto Rico during a tax year with worldwide gross income exceeding $75,000 (threshold applies separately to each spouse).
  • Deadline: By the due date (including extensions) of your Form 1040. Must be filed separately — do not attach to your tax return.
  • Penalty: Failure to file Form 8898 can result in a $1,000 penalty per year (IRS Form 8898 Instructions).

What Form 8898 Requires

Form 8898 asks detailed questions to verify your bona fide residency claim, including (IRS Form 8898 Instructions):

  • Date you became a bona fide resident
  • Number of days present in Puerto Rico during the tax year
  • Location of your permanent home, family, bank accounts
  • Where you are registered to vote
  • Location of your business activities

Pro Tip: Form 8898 is your official declaration to the IRS that you are a Puerto Rico resident. Keep contemporaneous records: travel logs, utility bills, lease/purchase agreements, and business contracts. The IRS uses this form to flag files for potential audit — every answer must be accurate and well-documented.

Step 3: Purchase Real Estate in Puerto Rico (Required Within 2 Years)

Act 60 Chapter 2 requires that you purchase residential real estate in Puerto Rico within two years of receiving your tax decree (Act 60-2019, Section 2062.01). This property must serve as your principal residence for the duration of your decree.

Why the Real Estate Requirement?

Puerto Rico added this requirement to ensure genuine commitment to the island. The government wants investors who truly relocate, not "tax tourists" who claim residency while maintaining primary homes on the mainland.

Note: The original Act 22 (predecessor to Act 60 Chapter 2) required property purchase within three years. Act 60-2019 shortened this to two years. Some older sources still reference the three-year window — the current requirement is two years from decree approval (Act 60-2019, Section 2062.01).

What Qualifies as Residential Property?

  • Single-family homes
  • Condominiums in residential developments
  • Townhomes and planned community properties
  • Custom-built homes on purchased land

Critical: Property Must Be Your Principal Residence

The property must be owned by you (sole owner or jointly with your spouse) and serve as your primary home. A vacation rental you visit twice a year will not satisfy the IRS closer connection test. The property must be where you live, sleep, and maintain your household.

Example: Real Estate Purchase Timeline

  • March 2026: John applies for Act 60 Individual Investor decree
  • October 2026: Decree approved (approximately 6–10 months processing)
  • November 2026: John establishes bona fide residency (meets 183-day test)
  • Deadline: John must purchase residential property by October 2028 (2 years from decree approval)
Rate note for this example: John's application was filed in March 2026 — after the original December 31, 2025 cutoff under prior law. Under Act 38-2026 (now signed into law), the grandfathering window was extended to December 31, 2026. John's March 2026 application falls squarely within this window, so he is grandfathered at the 0% rate (Grant Thornton PR; Act 38-2026).

Step 4: Apply for Your Act 60 Individual Investor Decree

To receive the tax benefits, you must apply for and obtain a tax exemption decree from the Puerto Rico Department of Economic Development and Commerce (DDEC).

Application & Acceptance Fees

  • Filing fee: $750 (one-time, due at application)
  • Decree acceptance fee: $5,000 (one-time, due when decree is granted)
  • Annual report filing fee: Separate fee due with each annual compliance report — confirm current amount with DDEC, as this has changed over program history

Source: Act 60-2019, Section 2062.01; DDEC Single Business Portal

Annual Obligations

  • Charitable donation: $10,000 annually to approved Puerto Rico nonprofits — $5,000 must go to organizations addressing child poverty (Act 60-2019, Section 2062.01(a)(10))
  • Annual compliance report: Due by November 15 each year

Note: One source (Windham Brannon, Feb 2026) suggested this amount may have increased to $15,000. This figure is contradicted by the Act 60 statute and multiple primary sources that consistently state $10,000. Verify the current requirement directly with DDEC before filing.

Eligibility Requirements

  • You must not have been a resident of Puerto Rico at any time from January 17, 2006 to January 17, 2012 (under current Act 60 statute)
  • You must become a bona fide resident of Puerto Rico before the taxable year ending December 31, 2055 (extended from 2035 by Act 38-2026)
  • You must be a U.S. citizen or lawful permanent resident
  • Under Act 38-2026, new applicants after 2026 must certify they were not a PR resident for at least 6 years prior to applying (Grant Thornton PR; Act 38-2026)

Processing Time: Expect 6–12 months from application submission to decree approval. The application submission date — not the approval date — determines your rate eligibility. Under Act 38-2026, applications submitted on or before December 31, 2026 are grandfathered at 0%. Applications on or after January 1, 2027 face the 4% rate (Grant Thornton PR; Act 38-2026).

Common Mistakes That Trigger IRS Audits

Mistake #1: Not Tracking Days Properly

Failing to maintain contemporaneous records of your physical presence. The IRS requires proof you met the 183-day presence test (26 CFR §1.937-1(c)).

Solution: Use a digital tracking app (TripLog, MileIQ) to automatically log entry/exit dates. Keep flight confirmations, hotel receipts, and credit card statements showing Puerto Rico purchases.

Mistake #2: Maintaining a Mainland Primary Residence

Keeping your U.S. home and treating Puerto Rico as a "vacation property" destroys your closer connection test under 26 CFR §1.937-1(e). The IRS will disallow your residency claim entirely.

Solution: Sell or rent out your mainland home. Make Puerto Rico your only permanent residence. Update all official documents to show Puerto Rico address.

Mistake #3: Conducting Business Primarily Stateside

If your primary business meetings, clients, and revenue generation all happen in the U.S., you fail the tax home test (26 CFR §1.937-1(d)) — regardless of how many days you spend in Puerto Rico.

Solution: Relocate your business operations to Puerto Rico. Establish a bona fide office. Conduct meetings via video from Puerto Rico. Document that your principal place of business is Puerto Rico.

Mistake #4: Selling Pre-Move Bitcoin Too Soon

Bitcoin purchased before you moved is subject to the 10-year rule under Act 60, Section 2021.01(e). Selling within 10 years of establishing residency means pre-move appreciation is still taxable by the U.S.

Solution: Hold pre-move Bitcoin for at least 10 years after establishing residency. After 10 years, pre-move appreciation qualifies for a 5% Puerto Rico rate. Post-move Bitcoin: 0% (or 4% for new decrees).

Mistake #5: Missing Annual Compliance Deadlines

Forgetting to file your annual Act 60 compliance report by November 15 or missing the $10,000 charitable donation can result in decree suspension — meaning you lose all tax benefits retroactively (Act 60-2019, Chapter 12).

Solution: Set calendar reminders for September (make charitable donation), November 15 (file compliance report), and April 15 (file Form 8898 if required).
