Can I Keep My US Business and Move to Puerto Rico? Income Sourcing Rules Explained ¿Puedo Mantener Mi Negocio en EE.UU. y Mudarme a Puerto Rico? Reglas de Fuente de Ingreso

Share this article:Compartir este articulo:

This is the question we get more than almost any other: "I have a successful business on the mainland. If I move to Puerto Rico, can I keep running it and get the Act 60 tax benefits?"

The short answer: yes, you can keep your US business. But whether the income from that business qualifies for Act 60's preferential rates depends entirely on the income sourcing rules — a complex web of federal statutes (IRC Sections 861–865, 933, and 937) and Treasury Regulations (particularly Treas. Reg. § 1.937-2) that determine where your income is considered earned. Get the sourcing wrong, and you've moved to an island while still paying full mainland taxes.

The Fundamental Rule

IRC Section 933 excludes Puerto Rico-sourced income from U.S. federal tax for bona fide PR residents. But it does not exclude U.S.-sourced income. So the entire question of whether your business income qualifies for Act 60 treatment comes down to one thing: where is the income sourced? The answer depends on the type of income and where the income-generating activities physically occur (IRS Publication 570; Holland & Knight, Nov. 2025).

The Income Sourcing Framework: Which Rules Apply to Your Income?

Under IRC Section 937(b)(1) and Treas. Reg. § 1.937-2(b), the sourcing rules of IRC Sections 861 through 865 are applied to determine whether income is Puerto Rico-sourced, with appropriate substitutions (replacing "United States" with "Puerto Rico" and "United States resident" with "bona fide resident of Puerto Rico"). Each type of income has its own sourcing rule (Cornell Law Institute — 26 CFR § 1.937-2):

Income Type Sourcing Rule Key Citation
Personal services income (salary, consulting fees, freelance income) Sourced to the location where the services are physically performed IRC §861(a)(3); Treas. Reg. §1.861-4
Capital gains (stocks, crypto, personal property) Sourced to the taxpayer's residence/tax home at the time of sale IRC §865(a); Treas. Reg. §1.937-2(f)
Interest income Sourced to the residence of the payer IRC §861(a)(1); Treas. Reg. §1.937-2(i)
Dividends Sourced to the location where the paying corporation is organized; special threshold rules for PR corporations IRC §861(a)(2); Treas. Reg. §1.937-2(g)
Rental income Sourced to the location of the property IRC §861(a)(4); IRS Pub. 570
Business income (trade or business) Sourced based on where business activities are conducted; apportionment may be required IRC §§861-865; Treas. Reg. §1.937-2

Personal Services Income: Where You Work Is Where It's Sourced

This is the rule that catches the most people off guard. For income from personal services — consulting, coaching, freelance work, professional services — the source is determined by where the services are physically performed. Not where the client is located. Not where the entity is incorporated. Where you are sitting when you do the work (IRC §861(a)(3); Treas. Reg. §1.861-4(b); IRS Publication 570).

Example: This Works

Maria is a marketing consultant. She moves to Puerto Rico, establishes bona fide residency, sets up a PR entity with an Act 60 Chapter 3 decree, and performs all consulting work from her office in San Juan for clients in New York, London, and Singapore. Her income is PR-sourced (services performed in PR for clients outside PR) and qualifies for the 4% corporate rate.

Example: This Doesn't Work

David is a software consultant. He incorporates a company in Puerto Rico and obtains an Act 60 Chapter 3 decree. But he continues to fly to client offices in San Francisco two weeks per month to perform work. The income attributable to services performed in California is U.S.-sourced — not PR-sourced — regardless of where the company is incorporated. That portion is subject to full federal tax. As Holland & Knight warned: if the taxpayer performs services outside Puerto Rico, that portion of income is U.S.-sourced and subject to U.S. tax (Holland & Knight, Nov. 2025).

The Practical Implication

If you want your service income to be PR-sourced, you must physically perform the services from Puerto Rico. Every trip to the mainland to work at a client site, attend meetings where services are rendered, or perform billable work creates U.S.-sourced income. Track your location for every working day. Keep contracts, billing records, and travel logs that demonstrate where services were performed. The IRS has made clear it will scrutinize this in Act 60 audits (Caplin & Drysdale).

Can I Keep My Mainland Entity? The Entity Structure Question

You can keep a mainland entity — but incorporation location does not determine income sourcing. This is one of the most critical distinctions in Act 60 planning, and one of the areas where aggressive practitioners have given incorrect advice. As one analysis put it: incorporation does not determine sourcing — activity does (Starving Artists, Feb. 2026).

Scenario A: Keep Your Delaware LLC, Work From PR

You have a Delaware LLC that provides consulting services. You move to Puerto Rico, establish residency, and perform all work from PR. The Delaware LLC still exists, but you're doing the work in PR.

Result: The service income is PR-sourced because you're performing services in PR. However, the Delaware LLC does not have an Act 60 Chapter 3 decree. You cannot get the 4% rate through a mainland entity — you'd need to either form a new PR entity with a decree or restructure.

Better approach: Form a Puerto Rico entity, obtain an Act 60 Chapter 3 decree, and route eligible export service income through the PR entity. The mainland entity can remain for non-PR activities or can be dissolved.

Scenario B: Form a New PR Entity, Contract with Your Mainland Business

You form a PR LLC with an Act 60 decree. Your mainland business subcontracts services to the PR entity, which performs the work from Puerto Rico. The PR entity invoices the mainland entity at arm's length rates.

Result: The PR entity's service income is PR-sourced if services are performed from PR. This is a common and accepted structure. But it must reflect economic substance — the PR entity must have a real office, perform real services, and charge market-rate fees. Transfer pricing between related entities will be scrutinized.

Key risk: If the IRS determines the PR entity is a sham — with no real employees, no real office, and no real services performed — it may disregard the entity and re-source the income to the mainland, where the actual services were performed.

Scenario C: You Own a C-Corp with U.S. Operations

You own a C-Corporation incorporated in Delaware with employees on the mainland performing services. You move to PR and obtain a Chapter 2 decree for personal investment income. You continue as a shareholder.

Dividends: Dividends from a U.S.-incorporated C-Corp are generally U.S.-sourced under IRC §861(a)(2). They do not become PR-sourced simply because you moved. These dividends remain subject to federal tax. There is a special rule for PR corporations: if the corporation is organized in PR and meets a threshold where 80%+ of gross income is PR-sourced and 50%+ comes from active PR business, dividends may be treated as PR-sourced (Treas. Reg. §1.937-2(g); Anchin).

Capital gains on selling the C-Corp: Under IRC §865(a), gain from selling personal property (including stock) is sourced to the taxpayer's residence. If you are a bona fide PR resident at the time of sale and the stock was acquired after establishing residency, the gain is generally PR-sourced. But the 10-year lookback rule under Treas. Reg. §1.937-2(f) applies to stock owned before the move.

The Pass-Through Entity Trap

Pass-through entities (S-Corps, partnerships, LLCs taxed as partnerships) create some of the most complex sourcing issues for PR relocators. This is the exact area the IRS has targeted in its enforcement campaign, as illustrated by the Gajwani case and IRS CCM 202538025.

The Core Problem

A pass-through entity's income flows through to its owners for tax purposes. But the source of that income is determined at the entity level — based on where the entity's activities occur — not at the owner level based on where the owner lives. An S-Corp cannot rely on IRC §937 (which applies only to individuals) to re-source income to Puerto Rico. IRS CCM 202538025 and AM 2024-005 explicitly rejected this approach (Holland & Knight, Nov. 2025; Pillsbury, 2025).

In practical terms: if you own a partnership or S-Corp that operates on the mainland, the income flowing through to you retains its U.S.-source character even after you become a PR resident. Moving to PR does not re-source income that is earned through mainland activities. The Gajwani case involved precisely this kind of attempted re-sourcing — using an S-Corp election to make C-Corp gains flow through to an individual PR resident — and resulted in criminal prosecution (DOJ, Jun. 2025).

The Caplin & Drysdale analysis further noted that if a PR resident has a U.S. office or fixed place of business that is a material factor in producing capital gains, the gains may be treated as effectively connected with a U.S. trade or business — and therefore U.S.-sourced regardless of the owner's residence (IRC §865(e)(2); Caplin & Drysdale).

What Actually Works: Business Models That Qualify for Act 60

Given the sourcing rules, certain business models are natural fits for Act 60, while others require significant restructuring to produce PR-sourced income. Here's the practical reality:

✓ Remote Service Businesses (Consulting, Software, Marketing, Design)

If you provide services remotely and can serve clients from anywhere, Puerto Rico works extremely well. Form a PR entity, obtain a Ch. 3 decree, and perform all services from your PR office. Clients must be outside PR. No on-site mainland work. This is the classic Act 60 export services model.

✓ SaaS and Digital Products

Software-as-a-service businesses where development, management, and customer support are performed from PR. The service is delivered digitally to clients worldwide. This fits the export services model cleanly, provided all key personnel work from PR.

✓ Investment Income (Post-Move Assets)

Capital gains on assets acquired after establishing PR residency are sourced to PR under IRC §865(a). Under a Chapter 2 decree, these gains are taxed at 0% (pre-2026 decrees) or 4% (post-2025). This includes stocks, Bitcoin, and other personal property bought after the move.

⚠ Partially Qualified: Hybrid Businesses with Some Mainland Activity

If your business requires occasional mainland travel, you can still benefit from Act 60 — but you must carefully apportion income. Days spent working on the mainland generate U.S.-sourced income. Days working from PR generate PR-sourced income. Keep meticulous records. The IRS will require you to demonstrate the apportionment methodology (IRS Pub. 570; Caplin & Drysdale).

✗ Does Not Qualify: Mainland-Based Operations Billed Through a PR Shell

Forming a PR entity that invoices clients while employees continue to work on the mainland does not create PR-sourced income. This is precisely the structure the IRS has described as non-compliant. Income follows where the services are actually performed, not where the billing entity is incorporated (Starving Artists, Feb. 2026; Holland & Knight, Nov. 2025).

✗ Does Not Qualify: U.S. Rental Properties

Rental income from U.S. real estate is U.S.-sourced under IRC §861(a)(4) based on the property's location. Moving to PR does not change the source. Your U.S. rental income remains federally taxable regardless of your Act 60 decree.

✗ Does Not Qualify: U.S. Corporate Dividends

Dividends from a U.S. corporation are generally U.S.-sourced under IRC §861(a)(2). Moving to PR does not re-source these dividends. Only dividends from PR-organized corporations meeting the 80%/50% threshold under Treas. Reg. §1.937-2(g) qualify as PR-sourced (Anchin; The Tax Adviser).

How to Restructure Your Business for Maximum PR-Source Income

For business owners with existing mainland operations, the key is restructuring — before or shortly after the move — to maximize the portion of income that qualifies as PR-sourced. Here is the standard framework:

Step 1: Form a Puerto Rico Entity

Incorporate an LLC or corporation in Puerto Rico. Obtain an Act 60 Chapter 3 decree for eligible export service activities. The PR entity must have a bona fide office in PR (lease, utilities, physical presence). The entity must be real — not a paper company.

Step 2: Migrate Service Delivery to PR

Move the core income-generating activities — the actual performance of services — to Puerto Rico. If you're a consultant, you consult from PR. If you're a developer, you code from PR. If you manage a team, you manage from PR. The services must be physically performed on the island.

Step 3: Restructure Client Contracts

New contracts should flow through the PR entity. Existing contracts may need to be renegotiated or assigned to the PR entity. Ensure contracts specify that services will be delivered from Puerto Rico and do not require on-site mainland work.

Step 4: Address Mainland Employees

If you have employees on the mainland, their services are U.S.-sourced. Either relocate key personnel to PR, or accept that income attributable to mainland employee activities will not qualify for the 4% rate. Avoid the temptation to bill mainland employee work through the PR entity at the 4% rate — this is exactly what the IRS is looking for in Act 60 audits.

Step 5: Maintain the Mainland Entity for U.S.-Source Income

Keep the mainland entity active for any income that will remain U.S.-sourced — mainland rental properties, U.S. client on-site work, mainland employee activities. This avoids commingling PR-sourced and U.S.-sourced income within a single entity, which simplifies compliance and audit defense.

The PR Corporation Dividend Threshold

For decree holders who form a PR corporation and draw income as dividends rather than salary, the dividend sourcing rules create a significant planning opportunity — and a potential trap.

Under Treas. Reg. §1.937-2(g), dividends from a PR corporation are treated as PR-sourced if the corporation meets a "possessions corporation" threshold during a testing period: 80% or more of gross income must be derived from PR sources, and 50% or more must come from the active conduct of a trade or business in PR. If the corporation meets this threshold, the entire dividend to a bona fide PR resident shareholder is treated as PR-sourced (Anchin; The Tax Adviser).

Under a Chapter 2 decree, PR-sourced dividends are exempt from Puerto Rico income tax at 0% (pre-2026 decrees) or 4% (post-2025). Under IRC §933, they are excluded from federal tax. Under a Chapter 3 decree, dividends distributed from exempt export service income are 100% exempt from PR tax (Act 60-2019, Chapter 3).

The Trap

If the PR corporation fails the 80%/50% threshold — for example, because a significant portion of work is actually performed on the mainland — a prorated portion of the dividends may be treated as non-PR-sourced. This means U.S.-sourced dividends subject to federal tax. Anchin emphasized the importance of proper bookkeeping to establish whether the threshold was met. If it's not met, the taxpayer must determine the proportion of work performed per location and apply that ratio to the dividend (Anchin).

What the IRS Looks For in Income Sourcing Audits

The IRS Act 60 campaign, launched in January 2021, specifically targets income sourcing as a primary audit focus. The GAO reported that the IRS identified 179 taxpayers failing residency requirements in a single review, and enforcement is accelerating (GAO-26-107225, Dec. 2025). Here are the specific documentation areas the IRS examines:

Where Were Services Actually Performed?

The IRS will examine travel records, airline manifests, credit card geolocation data, cell phone tower records, and calendar/meeting schedules to determine where you physically were when income-generating work was done. A contract that says "services performed from Puerto Rico" is meaningless if flight records show you were in New York.

Does the PR Entity Have Economic Substance?

The IRS looks for a real office (not a virtual mailbox), real equipment, real employees (if required), real client deliverables, and evidence that the entity is more than a billing conduit. Lease agreements, office photos, utility bills, internet service records, and local vendor contracts all help establish substance.

Are Transfer Prices Between Related Entities at Arm's Length?

If your PR entity contracts with a related mainland entity, the IRS will examine whether the fees charged are comparable to what unrelated parties would charge. Artificially shifting income to the PR entity through inflated intercompany charges is a red flag.

Is the Salary Reasonable?

If you're the owner of a Ch. 3 entity, the IRS examines whether your salary (taxed at ordinary rates) is reasonable relative to the work you perform, or whether you've suppressed salary to maximize the 4% corporate rate. Document your salary methodology with comparable market data.

The Bottom Line

Yes, you can keep your U.S. business and move to Puerto Rico. But the tax benefits don't come from moving your address — they come from moving your actual income-generating activities. The income sourcing rules are mechanical: personal services income is sourced where performed, capital gains are sourced to residence at time of sale, dividends are sourced to the payer's location, and rental income is sourced to the property's location.

The business owners who succeed under Act 60 are those whose work can genuinely be performed from Puerto Rico — remote consultants, software developers, digital marketers, fund managers, SaaS entrepreneurs, and similar professionals who serve clients outside PR without on-site mainland obligations. They form real PR entities, perform real work from real PR offices, and maintain documentation that proves it.

The business owners who fail are those who treat PR incorporation as a sourcing trick — forming shell entities, keeping mainland operations unchanged, and hoping the IRS won't notice. In 2026, the IRS is noticing. The restructuring must be real, the relocation must be genuine, and the income sourcing must reflect where the work actually happens. Get this right, and Act 60 can save you hundreds of thousands of dollars per year. Get it wrong, and you've added an expensive island lifestyle to your existing federal tax bill.
