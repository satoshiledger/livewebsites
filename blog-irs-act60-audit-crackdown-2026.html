The period of lax IRS enforcement on Puerto Rico Act 60 tax incentives is officially over. In December 2025 (publicly released December 12), the U.S. Government Accountability Office (GAO) published a critical report that exposed the IRS's failure to monitor taxpayers claiming Act 60 benefits — and laid the groundwork for the most aggressive enforcement campaign Puerto Rico has ever seen.

If you hold an Act 60 decree — or are considering relocating to Puerto Rico — this is the most important tax compliance update of 2026. The coordination between the IRS, Puerto Rico's Department of Economic Development and Commerce (DDEC), and federal prosecutors has reached unprecedented levels. Criminal cases are being filed, advisors are receiving grand jury subpoenas, and civil audits are expanding rapidly.

This article breaks down the four major developments driving the 2026 enforcement wave: the GAO report findings, the IRS compliance campaign expansion, Puerto Rico's own October 2025 audit initiative, and the first criminal prosecution under the new enforcement regime — the Gajwani case.

The GAO Bombshell: Four Years of Missing Data and Hundreds of Millions in Lost Revenue

The U.S. Government Accountability Office (GAO) published its report titled Puerto Rico: IRS Should Improve Oversight of Taxpayers Claiming Exemption from Federal Taxes (GAO-26-107225) on December 8, 2025, with public release on December 12, 2025. The report is significant because the GAO is an independent, nonpartisan agency that audits and evaluates federal programs — including tax administration — to provide Congress with objective information.

Key Finding: The IRS Operated Blind for Four Years

The GAO Report criticizes the IRS for operating without updated information on Act 60 participants for nearly four years and failing to act on referrals identifying individuals who failed to substantiate the 183-day residency requirement. Until 2025, IRS was unable to obtain complete data on taxpayers claiming Puerto Rico's resident investor incentive with Social Security numbers to help ensure compliance with federal tax laws.

The Revenue Impact: Hundreds of Millions in Federal Losses, $637 Million in Puerto Rico Forgone Revenue

The GAO report quantified the scale of the program and its federal impact. Puerto Rico granted a total of 5,852 resident investor decrees from 2012 through 2024, with the largest number of decrees granted in 2021. The number of decrees granted peaked in 2021, coinciding with the increase in teleworking following the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic.

Here's the financial reality: A taxpayer's average federal taxable income declined significantly by thirty-nine percent (39%) after relocating to Puerto Rico and the taxpayer's average federal income tax paid also significantly declined by forty-six percent (46%), thereby contributing to federal revenue losses in the hundreds of millions annually. Additionally, Puerto Rico's Treasury (Hacienda) estimated forgone revenue from the resident investor incentive at $637 million for 2022. Note: this figure represents estimated forgone revenue — taxes Hacienda would have collected absent the incentives — and does not incorporate potential behavioral responses by taxpayers (e.g., some may not have relocated to Puerto Rico at all without the incentive). Source: GAO-26-107225.

California Leads the Pack — and Faces the Heat

According to the GAO, 381 individuals who claimed the Act 60 resident investor incentive in 2021 had moved from California alone — representing approximately 17% of the roughly 2,200 total recipients that year, making California by far the single largest source state and a likely target for heightened scrutiny from both the IRS and the California Franchise Tax Board. Source: GAO-26-107225.

Why does this matter? California imposes a 13.3% top state income tax rate. If California's Franchise Tax Board successfully challenges a taxpayer's Puerto Rico residency claim, that individual could face both federal and California state back taxes, plus penalties and interest. For someone with $10 million in capital gains, that's a $1.33 million state tax liability — on top of federal exposure.

The IRS Campaign Goes Into High Gear: What "Campaign 685" Really Means

In 2021, the Internal Revenue Service (IRS) announced a compliance initiative, called a campaign, to address concerns that some recipients of Puerto Rico's resident investor incentive may not be meeting their federal tax obligations. This initiative is formally known as the "Puerto Rico Act 22 Campaign" and was one of 46 active Large Business and International campaigns that IRS had in place as of July 2025. Source: GAO-26-107225.

What is an IRS "Campaign"?

According to IRS, the objective of this campaign is to address noncompliance in this area through a variety of means including examinations, outreach, and education letters. Unlike a random audit, a campaign is a strategic, multi-year enforcement initiative targeting a specific issue the IRS has identified as high-risk.

The campaign involves education letters, civil audits, referrals to IRS Criminal Investigation, and coordination with the Department of Justice Tax Division for potential criminal prosecution.

What Changed in 2025: The Data Pipeline Opened

For four years, the IRS campaign struggled because it lacked current, complete data. The campaign only recently began showing results, in part, due to the complexity of high-income and high-wealth audits, IRS not prioritizing the effort, and communication gaps between IRS and Puerto Rico. Source: GAO-26-107225.

That bottleneck was finally resolved in 2025. The IRS and Puerto Rico's Hacienda (Treasury Department) established procedures to regularly exchange taxpayer data. Now, the IRS has access to the full list of decree holders, their Social Security numbers, and their Puerto Rico tax filings — which can be cross-referenced against federal returns to identify discrepancies.

The Three Audit Focus Areas

Based on the GAO report and practitioner guidance, the IRS is focusing on three compliance pillars:

1. Bona Fide Residency Under IRC Section 937

To qualify as a bona fide Puerto Rico resident, you must meet three tests: (1) Presence Test (183 days in Puerto Rico per year, or alternate safe harbor), (2) Tax Home Test (principal place of business in Puerto Rico), and (3) Closer Connection Test (more significant personal, family, economic, and social ties to Puerto Rico than to any other jurisdiction).

The IRS now has the technology to match your claimed presence days against airline records, credit card transactions, and cell phone location data.

2. Income Sourcing Under IRC Sections 937, 865, and Treas. Reg. § 1.937-2

Only Puerto Rico-source income is exempt from federal tax. The IRS is scrutinizing: (a) Capital gains on property held before becoming a bona fide resident (these are U.S.-source and fully taxable under the 10-year lookback rule in Treas. Reg. § 1.937-2(f)); (b) Service income sourced where the services are performed, not where the client is located; (c) Passive income from entities that are not Puerto Rico corporations.

3. Form 8898 Accuracy and Consistency

Form 8898 ("Statement for Individuals Who Begin or End Bona Fide Residence in a U.S. Possession") must be filed the year you establish Puerto Rico residency. Any mismatch between Puerto Rico filings and federal filings, including Form 8898, will now be readily identified. Source: GAO-26-107225.

Puerto Rico's Own Crackdown: The DDEC October 2025 Audit Blitz

The IRS isn't the only enforcement authority ramping up scrutiny. In October 2025, Puerto Rico's Department of Economic Development and Commerce (DDEC) announced its most aggressive compliance initiative to date.

DDEC October 2025 Actions

  • The Office of Business Incentives (OIN), part of DDEC, audited nearly 1,800 Act 60 (and former Act 22 and Act 20) decree holders in 2025. Source: Tax Notes, Oct. 27, 2025 — Puerto Rico Steps Up Act 60 Tax Compliance Enforcement.
  • DDEC now requires criminal background checks to be completed in the same year the decree is processed, with a final public record search before signing. Source: Tax Notes, Oct. 27, 2025.
  • Instituted an annual reporting requirement for all incentive programs. Source: Tax Notes, Oct. 27, 2025.
  • The committee will evaluate decree holders referred to it and will have the ability to impose fines of up to $10,000 for violations or recommend revocation of a decree. Source: Tax Notes, Oct. 27, 2025.

The New Mandatory Reporting Portal (2026)

According to some tax practitioners, enhanced reporting requirements expected in 2026 may include certified CPA letters, proof of residency documentation, and a breakdown of income sources and Act 60-eligible activities. Some practitioners also expect that crypto traders may be required to declare wallet addresses, transaction histories, and asset holdings to ensure capital gains are derived post-residency. No official DDEC regulation implementing these specific requirements has been published as of this article's publication date. Readers should monitor DDEC's Single Business Portal at incentivos.ddec.pr.gov for official guidance before filing.

Annual Charitable Contribution Requirement

New resident investor incentive recipients are required to make an annual $10,000 charitable donation. The deadline remains December 31 of each tax year, and failing to comply may risk your decree status. Source: Act 60-2019, Section 2021.01; GAO-26-107225.

The First Criminal Case: United States v. Suresh Gajwani

Miami investor Suresh Gajwani pleaded guilty on June 13, 2025 to willfully making false statements to avoid paying $7 million in capital gains tax, and on August 20, 2025, was sentenced to one year of probation. Source: IRS Criminal Investigation press release (June 16, 2025); Tax Notes — Puerto Rico Steps Up Act 60 Tax Compliance Enforcement (Oct. 27, 2025). This is the first federal criminal prosecution to emerge from the IRS Act 60 compliance campaign — and it sends a clear message.

Case Summary: What Gajwani Did Wrong

In 2018, Gajwani was a resident of Miami-Dade County. In October 2019, Gajwani owned a company that held stocks and options that had substantially appreciated in value by tens of millions of dollars. In anticipation of the tax on those gains, Gajwani sought to take advantage of a tax incentive program offered pursuant to Puerto Rico Act 60. Source: DOJ press release, June 16, 2025.

Here's the problem: Gajwani did not become a bona fide resident of Puerto Rico until January 1, 2020, which was after the stock portfolio had accrued built-in gains. To sidestep this issue, in January 2020, Gajwani submitted a false document to the IRS that claimed that the company had intended to convert as of January 1, 2019. Source: DOJ press release, June 16, 2025.

In 2019, Gajwani's company had a portfolio with approximately $30 million in built-in gains. Had the IRS not allowed Gajwani's company to convert to an S Corporation retroactively, the company would have owed approximately $7 million in capital gains taxes for 2019. Source: IRS Criminal Investigation press release, June 16, 2025.

The Consequences: $15.3 Million in Restitution

Gajwani agreed to repay approximately $15.3 million in restitution (taxes, interest, and penalties) to the IRS, and faced a maximum penalty of three years in prison. He received one year of probation rather than prison time, but the financial penalty was devastating — more than double the original $7 million tax liability. Source: IRS Criminal Investigation press release, June 16, 2025; Tax Notes, Oct. 27, 2025.

The Bigger Picture: Grand Jury Subpoenas for Advisors

The Gajwani case is not an isolated incident. Federal prosecutors are now investigating the advisors who provided faulty legal opinions. Federal investigators have served grand jury subpoenas on large law firms seeking documents related to Puerto Rico tax planning. On September 18, 2025, Bloomberg Tax reported that the global law firm Baker McKenzie was served with a grand jury subpoena from the U.S. Attorney's Office in Miami seeking documents related to Puerto Rico tax planning — a fact subsequently confirmed by Baker McKenzie itself. Source: Bloomberg Tax, Baker McKenzie Pulled Into Probe of Puerto Rico Tax Shelter (Sept. 18, 2025); Tax Notes, Nov. 6, 2025.

This represents a major escalation: the government is building cases not just against noncompliant taxpayers, but against the professionals who facilitated the schemes. As reported by Bloomberg Tax and confirmed by Baker McKenzie, the subpoena sought all records related to work by two Baker partners concerning "Puerto Rican Tax Planning Structures."

What This Means for Current and Prospective Decree Holders in 2026

With renewed coordination between federal and Puerto Rico authorities, the IRS has begun expanding examinations and is preparing to issue letters that will signal the start of deeper inquiries into taxpayer residency and sourcing positions. Source: GAO-26-107225.

Warning Signs of an Impending Audit

Any IRS correspondence referencing residency, presence in Puerto Rico, lifestyle factors, travel records, or income sourcing should be treated as the beginning of a potential examination. Any mismatch between Puerto Rico filings and federal filings, including Form 8898, will now be readily identified. Act 60 questionnaires or letters should be viewed as early warnings. Source: GAO-26-107225; CPA Practice Advisor, Oct. 16, 2025.

Do NOT ignore these letters. They are not informational — they are the first step in an audit process that can lead to civil penalties or criminal referral.

The Seven-Point Compliance Checklist for 2026

1. Maintain Detailed Travel Logs

Keep flight manifests, boarding passes, hotel receipts, and credit card statements showing physical presence in Puerto Rico for 183+ days per year.

2. Document Your Closer Connection

Driver's license, voter registration, bank accounts, children's school enrollment, gym memberships, utility bills — all must be Puerto Rico-based.

3. File Form 8898 Accurately

This form is due the year you establish residency. Any discrepancy between Form 8898 and your actual circumstances is an automatic red flag.

4. Apply the 10-Year Lookback Rule

Capital gains accrued BEFORE you became a bona fide resident are U.S.-source income and fully taxable. Do NOT try to retroactively convert these gains.

5. Make the $10,000 Annual Charitable Contribution

$5,000 must go to a DDEC-approved children's charity. Deadline: December 31 each year. Keep official receipts.

6. File the DDEC Annual Report

Starting in 2026, this report may require a certified CPA letter, proof of residency documentation, and income source breakdown. Late filings trigger automatic fines.

7. Monitor DDEC for Crypto Reporting Requirements

Some tax practitioners expect 2026 DDEC annual reports to require crypto holders to report wallet addresses and transaction histories. No official rule has been published yet. Check incentivos.ddec.pr.gov for updates before filing.

Three Real-World Scenarios: Who's at Risk in 2026?

Scenario 1: The "Zoom Worker" Who Never Left New York

Profile: Software engineer who obtained an Act 60 decree in January 2023. Claims to spend 183 days in Puerto Rico but continues working remotely for a New York employer from his Manhattan apartment.

Risk Level: EXTREME. The IRS will cross-reference his IP address logs (available via subpoena to his employer), credit card transactions, and cell phone location data. His service income is sourced where the services are performed — New York — and is fully taxable. He also fails the Closer Connection Test because his primary residence, social connections, and family are all in New York.

Potential Liability: Three years of back taxes on $250,000/year salary = $225,000 in federal tax + 25% accuracy penalty + interest + New York State tax liability. Total exposure: $350,000+.

Scenario 2: The Bitcoin Trader Who Sold Pre-Residency Gains

Profile: Crypto investor who accumulated 50 BTC from 2017-2020 while living in California. Moved to Puerto Rico on February 1, 2024 and obtained an Act 60 decree. Sold all 50 BTC in March 2024 at $70,000/BTC for total proceeds of $3.5 million. Claimed the gain was Puerto Rico-source and paid 0% tax.

Risk Level: HIGH. Under Treasury Regulation § 1.937-2(f), gains on property held before becoming a bona fide resident are U.S.-source income. The 10-year lookback rule applies to intangible property including Bitcoin. The gain is fully taxable as U.S.-source capital gain.

Potential Liability: Assuming $500,000 cost basis and $3 million gain: $714,000 federal capital gains tax (23.8%) + California Franchise Tax Board assessment for prior residency + 25% penalty = $1.2 million+ total exposure.

Scenario 3: The Compliant Entrepreneur Who Did It Right

Profile: Tech entrepreneur who sold his California home in December 2022, moved his entire family to Puerto Rico in January 2023, enrolled his children in Puerto Rico schools, obtained a Puerto Rico driver's license, registered to vote, and established all bank accounts in Puerto Rico. He founded a SaaS company in Puerto Rico in March 2023 (qualifying for Act 60 Chapter 3 export services at 4% corporate rate). He maintains detailed travel logs showing 220+ days in Puerto Rico each year. He files Form 8898, files his DDEC annual reports on time, and makes his $10,000 annual charitable contributions by December 31.

Risk Level: LOW. This taxpayer meets all three bona fide residency tests, sources his income correctly, and maintains meticulous documentation.

Outcome: If audited, he will prevail. His decree is safe. He is the model Act 60 participant — and exactly the type of investor Puerto Rico wants to attract.
