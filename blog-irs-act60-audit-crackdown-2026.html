IRS Crackdown on Act 60: What the 2026 Enforcement Wave Means for Decree Holders Ofensiva del IRS Contra la Ley 60: Lo Que la Ola de Cumplimiento 2026 Significa

Share this article: Compartir este articulo:

The era of light-touch IRS enforcement of Puerto Rico's Act 60 is over. On December 12, 2025, the U.S. Government Accountability Office published a landmark report — GAO-26-107225 — concluding that the IRS lacked complete data on taxpayers claiming the resident investor incentive and had not prioritized examinations of potential noncompliance. The GAO made three recommendations, and the IRS agreed with all of them (GAO, "Puerto Rico: IRS Should Improve Oversight of Taxpayers Claiming Exemption from Federal Taxes," Dec. 2025).

That report landed on top of a year that already saw the first federal criminal prosecution arising from the IRS's Act 60 audit campaign, a series of Chief Counsel memoranda challenging aggressive income-sourcing positions, congressional demands for Treasury reports on revenue losses, and DDEC's own enhanced compliance portal rolling out for the 2026 filing year. For the roughly 5,800 individuals holding resident investor decrees and nearly 3,900 businesses holding export service decrees, 2026 is the year the enforcement apparatus becomes fully operational.

Why This Matters Now

If you hold an Act 60 decree — whether Chapter 2 (Individual Investor) or Chapter 3 (Export Services) — and your residency documentation, income sourcing, or annual compliance filings have gaps, the time to fix them is before the IRS contacts you. Once an examination begins, the cost and complexity escalate dramatically.

Timeline: How We Got Here

The IRS did not wake up to Act 60 enforcement overnight. The trajectory has been building for years, but the pace accelerated dramatically in 2025.

Jan 2021

The IRS Large Business & International Division announces a formal audit campaign targeting U.S. taxpayers claiming Act 22/Act 60 benefits who may not meet residency or income-sourcing requirements (IRS LB&I, "Introduction to Puerto Rico Acts 20 and 22").

Jul 2023

The IRS publicly announces it has identified approximately 100 individuals claiming Act 22/60 incentives who do not appear to meet the law's residency requirements. The agency signals that many cases may proceed to criminal investigation (Tax Notes, "IRS Enforcement of Act 60 Comes Into View," Jun. 2025).

Aug 2023

Puerto Rico officials share an audit with the IRS identifying 179 decree holders who did not provide evidence meeting a residency requirement. According to the GAO report, an IRS official reviewed a few cases before determining the referrals did not need to be prioritized (GAO-26-107225, Dec. 2025).

May 2024

The New York Times reports that an IRS whistleblower accused the agency of neglecting to enforce abuse of Puerto Rico tax incentives. The Senate Finance Committee requests information about the IRS's efforts (Tax Notes, Jun. 2025).

Mar 2025

The U.S. Department of Justice charges Miami investor Suresh Gajwani with filing false documents to shield approximately $80 million in capital gains through Act 60 — the first criminal case arising from the IRS audit campaign (DOJ, Southern District of Florida, Jun. 2025).

May 2025

Congressional lawmakers, led by Rep. Nydia Velázquez, formally request that the FY2026 appropriations bill direct Treasury to report on IRS efforts to reduce tax avoidance under Acts 20, 22, and 60 — including the number of civil and criminal investigations, back taxes collected, and estimated federal revenue losses (Velázquez letter, May 2025).

Jun 2025

Gajwani pleads guilty and agrees to pay approximately $15.3 million in restitution (taxes, interest, and penalties). He is later sentenced to one year of probation (IRS Criminal Investigation, Jun. 2025; Tax Notes, Nov. 2025).

Sep 2025

The Senate Finance Committee sends a letter to at least one high-profile Act 60 decree holder regarding the treatment of partnership flow-through gains as Puerto Rico-sourced income, signaling direct congressional scrutiny of specific taxpayer strategies (Senate Finance Committee, Sep. 2025).

Nov 2025

The IRS reports it is in the final stages of preparing educational letters tailored to the Act 60 taxpayer population. Holland & Knight publishes a detailed analysis warning that the IRS will enforce sourcing rules rigorously, citing the Gajwani case and Chief Counsel Memorandum 202538025 (GAO-26-107225; Holland & Knight, Nov. 2025).

Dec 2025

The GAO publishes report GAO-26-107225 with three recommendations. The IRS agrees with all three. The era of data gaps and deprioritized referrals is officially ending.

The GAO Report: What It Found and Why It Matters

The GAO report — formally titled "Puerto Rico: IRS Should Improve Oversight of Taxpayers Claiming Exemption from Federal Taxes" — is the product of an audit that began in December 2023 and concluded in December 2025. It is the most comprehensive federal examination of the Act 60 enforcement landscape to date.

Key GAO Findings

Scale of the program: Puerto Rico granted more than 5,800 resident investor incentive decrees and nearly 3,900 export service business decrees from 2012 through 2024 (GAO-26-107225).

Foregone revenue: Puerto Rico's Treasury Department (Hacienda) projects that from 2020 through 2026, the government will forgo approximately $4.4 billion due to the resident investor incentive and $1.8 billion due to the export service business incentive (GAO-26-107225).

Data gaps: Until 2025, the IRS was unable to obtain complete data on taxpayers claiming the investor incentive with Social Security numbers to ensure compliance. The IRS also had no plans to obtain updated data from Puerto Rico (ABC News, Dec. 2025; GAO-26-107225).

Failed referrals: When Puerto Rican officials shared a list of 179 taxpayers who failed to provide evidence of meeting residency requirements, an IRS official reviewed a few cases and determined they did not need to be prioritized (GAO-26-107225).

Slow campaign results: The IRS's 2021 audit campaign only recently began producing results, in part due to the complexity of high-income and high-wealth audits, the IRS not prioritizing the effort, and communication gaps between the IRS and Puerto Rico agencies (GAO-26-107225; ABC News, Dec. 2025).

The Three GAO Recommendations (All Accepted by IRS)

Recommendation 1: Establish procedures to regularly obtain from Hacienda current data on all recipients of the Puerto Rico resident investor incentive, incorporating leading collaboration practices.

Recommendation 2: Establish written procedures to review cases of potential noncompliance among resident investor incentive recipients referred by Puerto Rico government agencies.

Recommendation 3: Consider avenues to promote voluntary compliance, such as sending educational letters that explain compliance requirements related to the taxpayer's probable tax situation.

The practical implication: the IRS will now receive systematic, ongoing data from Hacienda about every decree holder. Every Form 8898 filed declaring a change of residency to Puerto Rico can be cross-referenced against the DDEC decree database, Puerto Rico tax filings, and the taxpayer's federal return. Mismatches between what you report on Form 482 (PR tax return) and Form 1040 (US federal return) will be flagged automatically. The infrastructure for large-scale enforcement is being built right now (Procopio, "GAO Report Signals Intensified IRS Scrutiny of Puerto Rico Act 60 Residents," Dec. 2025).

The Gajwani Case: The First Criminal Prosecution from the Audit Campaign

The case of United States v. Suresh Gajwani is the first federal criminal prosecution to emerge from the IRS's Act 60 audit campaign, and it has become the reference point for every subsequent enforcement discussion.

According to DOJ court filings and IRS Criminal Investigation, Gajwani was a resident of Miami-Dade County in 2018. By October 2019, his company (Nimco II Corp., a Delaware C corporation) held stocks and options — including a substantial Tesla position — that had appreciated by tens of millions of dollars. Anticipating the tax liability on those gains, Gajwani sought to use Act 60's capital gains exemption. However, he did not become a bona fide Puerto Rico resident until January 1, 2020 — after the gains had already accrued (DOJ, Southern District of Florida, Jun. 2025).

Acting on advice from an accountant and an attorney, Gajwani submitted IRS Form 2553 in January 2020 to retroactively convert Nimco to an S Corporation as of January 1, 2019 — before his PR residency. The filing included a false "reasonable cause" statement. The strategy rested on the theory that S Corp status would allow the capital gains to flow through to Gajwani as an individual PR resident and be treated as Puerto Rico-sourced. The IRS later determined that both legal positions underlying the strategy were wrong: an S Corporation cannot rely on IRC Section 937 (which applies only to individual residency), and built-in gains accrued before establishing PR residency cannot be exempt from federal tax under Act 60 (Holland & Knight, Nov. 2025; DOJ, Jun. 2025).

Gajwani: Key Takeaways

Criminal, not civil: This was a criminal prosecution with potential prison time — not just a civil penalty. Gajwani faced up to three years and agreed to $15.3 million in restitution.

Reliance on counsel is not a defense: Gajwani obtained an opinion letter from a tax attorney. He was still prosecuted. The attorney's legal positions were described by IRS senior representatives as incorrect (Tax Notes, Nov. 2025; Senate Finance Committee, Sep. 2025).

Pre-move gains are the key trigger: The prosecution centered on the attempt to treat pre-residency appreciation as Puerto Rico-sourced income — the single most common aggressive position the IRS is targeting.

More cases are expected: Tax professionals have noted that the defense bar lacks clarity on why the government chose the criminal rather than civil route for this particular case, suggesting the line between civil audit and criminal prosecution is being drawn case by case (Tax Notes, "Tax Bar Eyes Puerto Rico Act 60 Criminal Case Selection," Nov. 2025).

What the IRS Is Looking For: The Five Audit Triggers

Based on the GAO report, the Gajwani prosecution, IRS Chief Counsel Memorandum 202538025, and analysis from Holland & Knight, Procopio, and Pillsbury, the IRS is focused on five primary areas when examining Act 60 decree holders.

1. Residency Compliance — The 183-Day Test

The IRS verifies whether you genuinely spent 183 or more days in Puerto Rico during the tax year. They check flight records, passport stamps, credit card transaction geolocation, utility usage patterns, and medical records. Simply owning property in Puerto Rico or visiting occasionally does not satisfy the bona fide residency requirements under IRC Section 937 (Procopio, Dec. 2025; Windham Brannon, Feb. 2026).

The GAO found that more than half of Act 22/60 beneficiaries may not have complied with the residency requirements, according to an IRS whistleblower's letter to lawmakers (Tax Notes, Jun. 2025).

2. Income Sourcing — Pre-Move vs. Post-Move Gains

This is the most actively litigated area. The IRS examines whether capital gains claimed as Puerto Rico-sourced actually accrued after the taxpayer established bona fide residency. Under IRC Section 865, capital gains on personal property are sourced to the taxpayer's residence at the time of sale. Under the 10-year lookback rule in Treasury Regulation 1.937-2, gains on certain property owned before establishing PR residency may not be considered PR-sourced if sold within 10 years (Holland & Knight, Nov. 2025).

CCM 202538025 reinforced that taxpayers cannot re-characterize U.S.-sourced gains as PR-sourced through S Corporations or partnerships — a direct repudiation of strategies like the one in the Gajwani case (Holland & Knight, Nov. 2025; Pillsbury, 2025).

3. Tax Home and Closer Connection Tests

Beyond the 183-day presence test, the IRS evaluates whether your principal place of business is in Puerto Rico (tax home test) and whether your strongest personal and economic ties — family, banking, driver's license, voter registration, community involvement — are in Puerto Rico rather than any mainland location (closer connection test). Under IRC Section 937 and Treasury Regulation 1.937-1, all three tests must be met simultaneously. Failing any one of them disqualifies you from bona fide resident status.

4. Reasonable Salary (Chapter 3 Decree Holders)

For export services businesses paying the 4% corporate rate, the IRS and Puerto Rico's Office of Industrial Tax Exemption (OITE) both scrutinize whether the decree holder is paying themselves a reasonable salary. Salary income is taxed at Puerto Rico's standard individual rates (up to 33%), while corporate profit above salary receives the 4% rate. Setting an artificially low salary to maximize the amount of income taxed at 4% is a known audit trigger. The IRS uses comparable salary data and industry benchmarks to challenge unreasonable allocations.

5. Annual Compliance Filings and Charitable Donations

The DDEC requires annual reports from all decree holders. Chapter 2 holders must maintain PR real property, make $10,000 in annual charitable donations (with at least half directed to organizations addressing child poverty), and file through the DDEC compliance portal. For 2026, the enhanced portal requires certified CPA letters, proof of residency, and income source breakdowns. Failure to file can result in fines of up to $1,000 per violation and potential decree revocation (Act 60, Chapter 12; Windham Brannon, Feb. 2026).

DDEC's Enhanced Compliance Portal: What's New for 2026

Puerto Rico's Department of Economic Development and Commerce (DDEC) has been upgrading its compliance infrastructure in parallel with the IRS's enforcement push. The enhanced compliance portal rolling out for the 2026 filing year represents a significant increase in reporting requirements for decree holders.

2026 Enhanced Compliance Requirements

Certified CPA letters: The annual report now requires a letter from a Puerto Rico-licensed CPA attesting to the accuracy of your income source classifications and Act 60-eligible income.

Proof of residency documentation: Beyond the annual report form, you must submit supporting evidence — lease agreements, utility bills, bank statements, property deeds — demonstrating ongoing PR residency.

Income source breakdowns: A detailed breakdown of income by source (PR-sourced vs. U.S.-sourced) and by Act 60 eligibility category (exempt capital gains, exempt dividends, exempt interest, taxable salary, etc.).

Cryptocurrency holdings: For crypto holders, the portal now requests wallet addresses, transaction histories, and documentation of pre-move vs. post-move asset categorization.

Charitable donation verification: Receipts from approved PR nonprofit organizations, with at least 50% of the $10,000 minimum directed to child poverty organizations. Note: under HB 505, the donation requirement for new applicants after January 1, 2026 has increased to $15,000 annually (Windham Brannon, Feb. 2026).

This data is no longer siloed. Under the GAO's first recommendation — which the IRS has accepted — Hacienda and the DDEC will share decree holder data with the IRS on an ongoing basis. Every inconsistency between your DDEC annual report, your Puerto Rico Form 482, and your federal Form 1040 becomes a potential examination trigger.

Cryptocurrency: The IRS's Highest-Priority Act 60 Target

The intersection of Act 60 and cryptocurrency is where the IRS has directed its sharpest scrutiny. Pillsbury Law's analysis noted that ambiguous rules and limited IRS enforcement had created what practitioners describe as a "gray area" in tax compliance — particularly around whether gains on pre-residency crypto holdings could be sourced entirely to Puerto Rico (Pillsbury, "Uncertainty Clouds Puerto Rico's Act 60 Tax Breaks," 2025).

The IRS has now moved to close that gray area through multiple channels. CCM 202538025 cautioned that taxpayers cannot simply re-characterize U.S.-sourced gains as PR-sourced through entity structures. Holland & Knight's analysis emphasized that cryptocurrency is treated as property under IRS Revenue Ruling 2019-24, and gains are sourced under IRC Section 865 to the taxpayer's residence at the time of sale — not to where the crypto was acquired or where the exchange is located (Holland & Knight, Nov. 2025).

Tax attorneys have specifically flagged opinion letters from certain practitioners that advised clients that pre-move crypto gains could be treated as PR-sourced — positions the IRS has described as incorrect. The Gajwani prosecution and the Senate Finance Committee's direct inquiries to high-profile decree holders suggest that taxpayers who relied on such opinions may face examination (Tax Notes, "Everyone Has an Opinion (Letter) on Puerto Rico's Act 60," Nov. 2025).

If You Hold Crypto Under Act 60

Document the fair market value of every crypto position as of your bona fide residency date. Tag each lot as pre-move or post-move. Maintain verifiable exchange data, TXIDs, and USD spot rates. If you've been treating pre-move crypto gains as PR-sourced based on an opinion letter, consult independent tax counsel immediately — before the IRS contacts you.

What Decree Holders Should Do Right Now: A Compliance Checklist

Procopio's analysis was direct: any IRS correspondence referencing residency, presence in Puerto Rico, lifestyle factors, travel records, or income sourcing should be treated as the beginning of a potential examination. Taxpayers with concerns about prior filings, documentation, or residency support should address them promptly before the IRS initiates contact (Procopio, Dec. 2025).

✓ Audit Your Own Residency Documentation

Compile flight records, boarding passes, passport stamps, PR utility bills, bank statements, lease/deed, driver's license copy, voter registration, medical records, and school enrollment records (if applicable). Create a day-by-day presence log for each tax year. Identify any year where you may have fallen short of 183 days.

✓ Verify Your Income Source Classifications

Review every income item on your Form 482 and Form 1040. Ensure that only genuinely post-move, PR-sourced income is claimed as Act 60-exempt. If you have partnership K-1 income, S Corp flow-through income, or pre-move crypto gains that you've treated as PR-sourced, have that position reviewed by independent counsel.

✓ Confirm Your DDEC Filings Are Current

Ensure every annual report has been filed. Verify charitable donation receipts are on file. If you've missed a year, file a delinquent report immediately — the penalty is $1,000 per violation, but an unfiled report is a much larger red flag than a late one.

✓ Prepare for the Enhanced Compliance Portal

Gather the documentation you'll need for the 2026 enhanced filing: CPA letter, income source breakdown, residency evidence, crypto wallet addresses (if applicable), and charitable donation records. Don't wait until the filing deadline to assemble these materials.

✓ Review Form 8898

If you never filed Form 8898 (Statement for Individuals Who Begin or End Bona Fide Residence in a U.S. Possession) in the year you established PR residency, consult your CPA about late filing options. The IRS now has the ability to cross-reference this form against DDEC decree data.

✓ Engage Both a US Tax Attorney and a PR CPA

Act 60 compliance sits at the intersection of federal and territorial tax law. You need a U.S. tax attorney who understands IRC Sections 933, 937, 861-865, and the Treasury Regulations — and a Puerto Rico CPA who understands Form 482, DDEC reporting, and the SURI system. If you receive any IRS correspondence, do not respond without counsel.

The Political Dimension: Congressional Pressure and HB 505

The enforcement wave is not occurring in a vacuum. There is significant congressional pressure to demonstrate that Act 60 benefits are not being abused. Multiple lawmakers have publicly criticized the program. Rep. Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez has asserted that the policy is driving up wealth inequality on the island while reducing federal tax revenue that funds Social Security and Medicare (ABC News, Dec. 2025). Rep. Nydia Velázquez introduced the "Fair Taxation of Digital Assets in Puerto Rico Act" in April 2025, which would have ended the tax-free crypto regime entirely (BTCC, 2025).

Puerto Rico's Governor, Jennifer González-Colón, responded by supporting HB 505, which extended the Act 60 program through 2055 but imposed a 4% tax on passive income for new applicants after January 1, 2026, while grandfathering pre-2026 decree holders at 0%. This legislative compromise preserved the program while acknowledging concerns about revenue loss — but it did not reduce the IRS's enforcement appetite for existing decree holders who may not be in compliance.

The GAO report specifically noted that Puerto Rico's economy has shown "little or no growth" since the incentives were introduced in 2012, while acknowledging that multiple external factors (hurricanes, earthquakes, the pandemic) make it impossible to isolate the incentives' impact. This finding gives enforcement-minded lawmakers additional ammunition to argue for stricter oversight (GAO-26-107225).

The Bottom Line

Act 60 remains a powerful, legal tax incentive. The GAO report, the Gajwani prosecution, and the enhanced compliance requirements do not change the fundamental benefits of the program for decree holders who genuinely relocate to Puerto Rico, meet all residency tests, properly source their income, and maintain their annual filings. Holland & Knight put it clearly: Act 60 rewards compliance, not creativity (Holland & Knight, Nov. 2025).

What has changed is the enforcement environment. The IRS now has the data, the congressional mandate, the inter-agency coordination, and a criminal prosecution precedent. The DDEC has an enhanced compliance portal requiring documentation that was previously optional or informal. There are no more data gaps for noncompliant decree holders to hide behind.

For decree holders who have been doing everything right, 2026 should bring no surprises. For those with gaps — a year under 183 days, pre-move gains classified as PR-sourced, missed DDEC filings, incomplete charitable donation records — the time to address them is now.
