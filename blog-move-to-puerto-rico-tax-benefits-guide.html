Moving to Puerto Rico for Tax Benefits: The Complete 2026 Relocation Checklist Mudarse a Puerto Rico por Beneficios Fiscales: Lista Completa de Reubicación 2026

Share this article: Compartir este articulo:

Puerto Rico has emerged as one of the most compelling legal tax jurisdictions for U.S. citizens seeking to dramatically reduce their tax burden without renouncing citizenship or moving abroad. Under Act 60-2019 (Puerto Rico Incentives Code), new residents who meet strict IRS requirements can legally access 0% tax on Puerto Rico-sourced capital gains, 100% exemption on dividends and interest, and a 4% corporate tax rate for qualifying export service businesses.

But moving to Puerto Rico for tax benefits is not as simple as buying a condo and spending six months on the beach. The IRS actively scrutinizes Puerto Rico relocations through the bona fide residency test codified in IRC Section 937, and failure to meet the requirements can result in back taxes, 25% penalties, and disqualification from Act 60 benefits.

This comprehensive 2026 guide walks you through the exact legal requirements, IRS filing obligations, real estate purchase rules, and step-by-step relocation strategy to successfully relocate to Puerto Rico under Act 60—backed by official IRS publications and Puerto Rico government sources.

Why Move to Puerto Rico? The Tax Benefits Explained

Under Section 933 of the U.S. Internal Revenue Code, U.S. citizens who are bona fide residents of Puerto Rico are exempt from U.S. federal income tax on Puerto Rico-sourced income. This creates a unique opportunity: you remain a U.S. citizen with full passport and travel rights, but you pay Puerto Rico tax rates instead of federal rates—and Puerto Rico's Act 60 offers exceptionally low rates.

Act 60 Chapter 2: Individual Resident Investor Incentives

0% Tax on Capital Gains

100% exemption on capital gains from securities and digital assets (including Bitcoin) accrued after becoming a bona fide resident of Puerto Rico.

0% Tax on Dividends & Interest

100% exemption on all dividend and interest income sourced from Puerto Rico, accrued after establishing bona fide residence.

Act 60 Chapter 3: Export Services Incentives

4% Corporate Tax Rate

Businesses providing eligible export services to clients outside Puerto Rico pay only 4% tax on net income (reduced to 2% for businesses under $3 million in revenue for the first five years).

Eligible services include: consulting, software development, financial services, marketing, accounting, legal services, engineering, and dozens of other professional services performed in Puerto Rico for non-Puerto Rico clients.

Critical Distinction: Capital gains and passive income benefits under Chapter 2 apply ONLY to income accrued AFTER you become a bona fide resident. Pre-move gains remain taxable under U.S. federal law. Strategic timing of your relocation is essential.

Real-World Tax Savings Examples

Example 1: Software Consultant

Maria, a software consultant in California, earns $500,000 annually serving clients nationwide. Her combined federal and state tax rate is approximately 45% ($225,000 in taxes).

After relocating to Puerto Rico and obtaining an Act 60 Chapter 3 decree, she operates her consulting business from Puerto Rico. On $500,000 in eligible export service income, she pays:

  • 4% corporate tax: $20,000
  • Dividend distributions: 0% (100% exempt)
  • Total annual tax savings: $205,000

Example 2: Bitcoin Long-Term Holder

David holds Bitcoin purchased in 2020 for $50,000, now worth $500,000 (unrealized gain: $450,000). He moves to Puerto Rico in 2026 when his Bitcoin is worth $500,000.

In 2028, he sells his Bitcoin for $800,000. Tax treatment:

  • Pre-move gain ($450,000): Subject to U.S. federal capital gains tax (~20% + 3.8% NIIT = ~$107,100)
  • Post-move gain ($300,000): 0% tax under Act 60 Chapter 2
  • Total tax: $107,100 instead of ~$179,100 (had he remained stateside)
  • Tax savings: $72,000

Note: If David had purchased Bitcoin AFTER becoming a Puerto Rico resident, the entire gain would be 0% taxed.

Example 3: Passive Investor

Jessica receives $200,000 annually in dividend income from a diversified stock portfolio. Living in New York, she pays approximately 40% in combined taxes ($80,000).

After relocating to Puerto Rico under Act 60 Chapter 2, dividend income from Puerto Rico-sourced investments is 100% exempt. She redirects investments to Puerto Rico-based companies and pays:

  • Puerto Rico tax on dividends: $0
  • Annual tax savings: $80,000

Note: Dividends from U.S. corporations remain U.S.-sourced and subject to federal tax. Strategic portfolio repositioning is required.

The IRS Bona Fide Residency Test: Three Requirements You Must Meet

To qualify for Act 60 tax benefits, you must establish bona fide residency in Puerto Rico as defined by IRC Section 937(a) and detailed in IRS Publication 570. The IRS applies a three-part test, and you must pass ALL three tests during the taxable year:

1 Physical Presence Test

You must meet ONE of the following physical presence requirements:

  • Present in Puerto Rico for at least 183 days during the tax year (the most common method)
  • Present in Puerto Rico for at least 549 days during the current and two preceding tax years, with a minimum of 60 days in each year
  • Present no more than 90 days in the U.S. during the tax year
  • Present in Puerto Rico for more days than in the U.S. AND U.S. earned income does not exceed $3,000
  • No significant connection to the U.S. (no permanent home, voter registration, or spouse/minor child in the U.S.)

Source: IRS Publication 570 (2025), Chapter 2; IRS.gov Moving to or from a U.S. Territory/Possession

2 Tax Home Test

Your tax home (principal place of business or employment) must be in Puerto Rico during the tax year. You cannot have a tax home outside Puerto Rico.

The IRS determines your tax home based on where you conduct the majority of your business activities. If you work remotely, your tax home is where you physically perform the work—not where your clients are located.

Source: IRC Section 937(a)(2); IRS Publication 570 (2025), Tax Home Test

3 Closer Connection Test

You must demonstrate a closer connection to Puerto Rico than to the United States or any foreign country. This is the most subjective test and requires establishing meaningful ties to Puerto Rico.

Factors the IRS considers:

  • Location of your permanent home
  • Where your family resides
  • Where you maintain bank accounts and financial assets
  • Location of social, religious, cultural, and political organizations you belong to
  • Where you hold a driver's license
  • Where you are registered to vote
  • Country of residence indicated on official forms
  • Location of your personal property (vehicles, furniture, jewelry)

Source: IRS Publication 570 (2025), Closer Connection Test; Treasury Regulation § 1.937-1(c)

IRS Audit Risk: The IRS actively audits Puerto Rico relocations. In the recent United States v. Suresh Gajwani case and IRS Chief Counsel Memorandum 202538025, the IRS emphasized that taxpayers must keep meticulous records proving bona fide residency, including travel logs, banking records, lease agreements, and utility bills. Failure to meet all three tests can result in full disqualification, back taxes, and 25% penalties.

Act 60 Mandatory Requirements: What You Must Do

In addition to meeting the IRS bona fide residency test, Act 60 decree holders must comply with specific Puerto Rico government requirements:

1. Purchase Real Estate Within Two Years

Under Act 60 Chapter 2 (Individual Resident Investor), you must purchase residential property in Puerto Rico within the first two years of obtaining your tax decree. This property must be your primary residence for the duration of the decree and cannot be rented out.

Source: Act 60-2019, Section 2062(d); multiple legal sources confirm this requirement was added to ensure genuine relocation commitment.

2. Annual Charitable Contribution: $10,000

Starting in the second year of your decree, you must contribute $10,000 annually to nonprofit organizations in Puerto Rico:

  • At least $5,000 must go to nonprofits focused on eradicating child poverty in Puerto Rico
  • The remaining $5,000 can be donated to any eligible Puerto Rico nonprofit certified under Section 1101.01 of the Puerto Rico Internal Revenue Code

Source: Act 60-2019, Section 2062(e)

3. Apply for and Obtain a Tax Exemption Decree

You must submit an application through the Puerto Rico Department of Economic Development and Commerce (DDEC) via the Single Business Portal. Application fees:

  • Application filing fee: $750
  • One-time decree issuance fee: $5,000 (paid upon approval)
  • Annual compliance report fee: $5,000 (increased from $300 in recent years)

Source: Act 60-2019 application process; Puerto Rico DDEC Single Business Portal requirements

4. 10-Year Prior Residency Restriction

To be eligible for Act 60 Chapter 2, you must not have been a resident of Puerto Rico at any time during the 10 years preceding January 1, 2019 (i.e., you must not have lived in Puerto Rico from January 1, 2009 to January 1, 2019). This requirement ensures the program attracts new residents rather than existing Puerto Rico residents claiming benefits.

Source: Act 60-2019, Section 2062; eligibility requirements for Individual Resident Investor

IRS Form 8898: Required Reporting for New Residents

When you move to Puerto Rico and become a bona fide resident, you must notify the IRS by filing Form 8898: Statement for Individuals Who Begin or End Bona Fide Residence in a U.S. Territory.

Who Must File Form 8898?

You must file Form 8898 if:

  • You take the position that you became a bona fide resident of Puerto Rico during the tax year, AND
  • Your worldwide gross income for that year exceeds $75,000

Important: For married couples, the $75,000 threshold applies to each spouse separately. If both spouses have income exceeding $75,000, each must file a separate Form 8898.

Source: IRS Form 8898 Instructions (Rev. October 2024); IRS.gov Residents of U.S. Territories

When and Where to File

Deadline: Form 8898 must be filed by the due date (including extensions) of your Form 1040 or Form 1040-NR for the year you became a bona fide resident.

Mailing Address:

Internal Revenue Service
3651 S. IH 35 MS 4301 AUSC
Austin, TX 78741

Source: IRS Form 8898 Instructions (Rev. October 2024)

Penalty for Failure to File: If you are required to file Form 8898 and fail to do so (or provide incomplete or incorrect information), you may be subject to a $1,000 penalty per tax year. This penalty applies even if you qualify for bona fide residency—failure to report the change is a separate violation.

Source: IRS Publication 570 (2025); IRC Section 937(c)

The Complete 2026 Relocation Checklist

Follow this step-by-step checklist to ensure full compliance with IRS and Puerto Rico government requirements:

1 Pre-Move: Strategic Planning (3-6 Months Before Relocation)

Consult a Puerto Rico tax attorney and CPA to assess eligibility and develop a relocation strategy

Calculate your potential tax savings based on current income, capital gains, and business structure

Determine your "move date" (the date you establish bona fide residence) to calculate pre-move vs. post-move gains

Begin researching Puerto Rico real estate (you have 2 years to purchase, but starting early is wise)

2 Month 1: Establish Physical Presence

Move to Puerto Rico and establish your primary residence (rent initially if not ready to purchase)

Begin counting your 183 days for the physical presence test

Keep a detailed travel log documenting every day spent in/out of Puerto Rico (critical for IRS audit defense)

3 Months 1-2: Establish Puerto Rico Ties

Open local bank accounts at Puerto Rico banks

Obtain a Puerto Rico driver's license (surrender your U.S. state license)

Register to vote in Puerto Rico (unregister from U.S. state voter rolls)

Establish utility accounts in your name (electric, water, internet)

Update your mailing address with banks, credit cards, IRS, and all institutions

Join local organizations (social, professional, religious, cultural) to demonstrate closer connection

4 Month 2-3: Apply for Act 60 Decree

Submit Act 60 application through Puerto Rico DDEC Single Business Portal

Pay $750 application fee

Wait for approval (processing typically takes 6-12 months, but application date locks in your benefits)

Pay $5,000 decree issuance fee upon approval

5 Within 2 Years: Purchase Real Estate

Purchase residential property in Puerto Rico within 2 years of obtaining your decree

Property must be titled in your name (or jointly with spouse) and serve as your primary residence

6 Tax Filing Year 1: Report Your Residency Change

File IRS Form 8898 for the year you became a bona fide resident (if worldwide income exceeds $75,000)

File Form 1040 reporting U.S.-sourced income and income from sources outside Puerto Rico

File Puerto Rico income tax return (Form 482.0) reporting Puerto Rico-sourced income

Do NOT report Puerto Rico-sourced income on U.S. return (exempt under Section 933)

7 Ongoing Compliance

Maintain 183+ days physical presence in Puerto Rico each year

File annual Act 60 compliance report with Puerto Rico government (November 15 deadline)

Pay $5,000 annual report fee

Make $10,000 annual charitable contribution (starting Year 2)

Maintain meticulous records (travel logs, receipts, contracts, banking records) for IRS audit defense

Common Mistakes That Trigger IRS Audits

Mistake #1: "Snowbird" Status

Spending 183 days in Puerto Rico but maintaining strong U.S. ties (permanent home, family, business operations in the U.S.). The IRS will argue you failed the closer connection test and remain a U.S. resident for tax purposes.

Mistake #2: Performing Services Outside Puerto Rico

If you claim Act 60 export services benefits but perform billable work while traveling in the U.S., that income is U.S.-sourced and taxable at federal rates. The IRS requires meticulous travel logs to prove where services were performed.

Mistake #3: Renting Instead of Purchasing

Failing to purchase property within 2 years violates Act 60 requirements and can result in decree revocation. The property purchase demonstrates genuine commitment to Puerto Rico residency.

Mistake #4: Ignoring Form 8898

Failing to file Form 8898 in the year you become a bona fide resident is a separate $1,000 penalty—even if you otherwise qualify. This is a common oversight that's easily avoidable.

Mistake #5: Poor Recordkeeping

The IRS expects contemporaneous records proving physical presence, tax home, and closer connection. Reconstructing records after an audit notice is nearly impossible. Keep travel logs, receipts, lease agreements, and banking records from Day 1.
