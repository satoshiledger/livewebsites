Bitcoin ETF Gains and Act 60: Tax Treatment for PR Residents Holding IBIT, FBTC, and Spot ETFs Ganancias de ETF de Bitcoin y Ley 60: Tratamiento Fiscal para Residentes de PR con IBIT y FBTC

Share this article:Compartir este articulo:

Since the SEC approved spot Bitcoin ETFs in January 2024, funds like BlackRock's iShares Bitcoin Trust (IBIT) and Fidelity's Wise Origin Bitcoin Fund (FBTC) have attracted hundreds of billions in capital. For Puerto Rico Act 60 decree holders, the critical question is straightforward: do gains on these ETFs qualify for Act 60's favorable capital gains treatment?

The short answer is yes — with important caveats around timing, sourcing, and ETF structure. This guide breaks down exactly how spot Bitcoin ETFs, futures-based ETFs, and the legacy Grayscale trust are treated under Act 60, and what you need to document to ensure your ETF gains qualify.

Key Principle

Bitcoin ETF gains are capital gains. Under IRC §865(a), capital gains on personal property are sourced to the taxpayer's residence at the time of sale. If you are a bona fide PR resident with a Chapter 2 decree, and you purchased the ETF shares after establishing residency, the gains are PR-sourced and eligible for 0% (pre-2026 decree) or 4% (post-2025 decree) treatment. Pre-move shares follow the split holding period rules (Holland & Knight, Nov. 2025; LegalClarity, Dec. 2025).

How Spot Bitcoin ETFs Are Structured for Tax Purposes

Not all Bitcoin ETFs are taxed the same way. The structure of the ETF determines how the IRS classifies your gains, which in turn affects the Act 60 analysis.

Spot ETFs (IBIT, FBTC, GBTC): The Cleanest Act 60 Play

Spot Bitcoin ETFs like IBIT and FBTC are structured as exchange-traded grantor trusts. Under this structure, shareholders are treated as owning a proportionate interest in the trust's underlying Bitcoin holdings. The IRS treats Bitcoin as property under Notice 2014-21, and Green Trader Tax confirmed that spot Bitcoin ETFs are taxed as property — not securities (Green Trader Tax, Jul. 2025; Aug. 2025).

When you sell shares of IBIT or FBTC, you realize a capital gain or loss based on the difference between your cost basis and the sale price. The ETF does not generate dividends or interest — only capital gains on sale. This is the simplest structure for Act 60 purposes: capital gains sourced to PR under IRC §865(a) when you are a bona fide PR resident at the time of sale (etf.com, May 2025; Green Trader Tax).

Futures ETFs (BITO): More Complex

Bitcoin futures ETFs like BITO invest in Bitcoin futures contracts, not Bitcoin itself. Gains may fall under IRC Section 1256, which imposes a 60/40 split (60% long-term, 40% short-term) and mark-to-market accounting — meaning unrealized gains are taxed annually regardless of whether you sell (etf.com, May 2025; Mezzi, Nov. 2025).

For Act 60 purposes, the Section 1256 mark-to-market treatment creates a complication: you may owe tax on unrealized gains each year. The sourcing of Section 1256 contract gains for PR residents is less clearly established than straightforward capital gains on property sales. Consult a PR tax attorney before relying on Act 60 treatment for futures-based ETF gains.

Sourcing Bitcoin ETF Gains Under Act 60

The Act 60 benefit depends entirely on whether the gain is PR-sourced. For capital gains on personal property (which includes both direct Bitcoin and Bitcoin ETF shares), the sourcing rule under IRC §865(a) is that gains are sourced to the taxpayer's residence at the time of sale. Holland & Knight's November 2025 analysis confirmed this: gains on property acquired after becoming a PR resident are PR-sourced and eligible for Act 60 exemption (Holland & Knight, Nov. 2025; LegalClarity, Dec. 2025).

Scenario 1: ETF Shares Purchased AFTER Establishing PR Residency

Tax treatment: You are a bona fide PR resident with a Chapter 2 decree. You purchase IBIT shares through your brokerage account after establishing residency. Two years later, you sell the shares at a profit. The entire gain is PR-sourced under IRC §865(a) because you were a PR resident when you both acquired and sold the property.

Act 60 result: Pre-2026 decree: 0% tax on the gain. Post-2025 decree: 4% tax. Under IRC §933, the PR-sourced gain is also excluded from U.S. federal tax. Total effective rate: 0% or 4%.

Scenario 2: ETF Shares Purchased BEFORE Establishing PR Residency

Tax treatment: You owned IBIT shares while living on the mainland and then moved to Puerto Rico. Under Treas. Reg. §1.937-2(f), these are "split holding period" property. The appreciation that accrued while you were a mainland resident is U.S.-sourced and subject to U.S. federal tax. Only the appreciation that accrues after you establish bona fide PR residency is PR-sourced.

The 10-year lookback rule: Under Treas. Reg. §1.937-2(f), for property acquired before becoming a PR resident and sold within 10 years of establishing residency, the gain must be bifurcated. The pre-move appreciation is U.S.-sourced; the post-move appreciation is PR-sourced. You cannot claim the entire gain as PR-sourced (Holland & Knight, Nov. 2025; Tax Notes, Nov. 2025).

The 5% toll tax option: Alternatively, under Act 60-2019, §2021.01(e), you can elect to pay a 5% toll tax on the unrealized appreciation as of your residency date. After paying the toll tax, all subsequent appreciation is PR-sourced and eligible for 0%/4% treatment.

Scenario 3: Selling Pre-Move ETF Shares Immediately After Moving

This is the Gajwani trap. If you buy IBIT on the mainland, move to Puerto Rico, and immediately sell, the IRS will scrutinize whether the gain is genuinely PR-sourced. The Gajwani prosecution involved exactly this pattern with cryptocurrency — buying assets pre-move, establishing residency, and claiming the gains were exempt. Holland & Knight noted that the IRS will not allow taxpayers to simply move and immediately sell pre-move assets without scrutiny (Holland & Knight, Nov. 2025).

Do not do this. The appreciation on pre-move ETF shares remains U.S.-sourced regardless of when you sell. The 10-year lookback rule applies. Use the toll tax election or hold the shares and only claim exemption on post-move appreciation.

Spot Bitcoin ETF vs. Direct Bitcoin: Tax Comparison for Act 60 Residents

For Act 60 purposes, the tax treatment of spot Bitcoin ETF gains and direct Bitcoin gains is essentially identical. Both are treated as capital gains on property, both are sourced under IRC §865(a) to the taxpayer's residence at the time of sale, and both qualify for Chapter 2 treatment when PR-sourced.

Key Advantage of ETFs for Act 60 Residents

Bitcoin ETFs held in a standard brokerage account generate clean Form 1099-B reporting, making compliance documentation straightforward. Your broker reports cost basis, proceeds, and holding period. This is considerably simpler than tracking direct Bitcoin transactions across multiple wallets and exchanges. For Act 60 compliance — where meticulous documentation is essential — ETF reporting through a conventional brokerage creates a cleaner audit trail (CCN, Oct. 2025; Green Trader Tax).

Tax Loss Harvesting Bitcoin ETFs as a PR Resident

Tax loss harvesting works differently for Act 60 residents than for mainland investors, because the Act 60 benefit eliminates or drastically reduces the tax on gains — which means losses have less value on the PR side.

When Harvesting Still Matters

Pre-move ETF shares: If you hold ETF shares purchased before establishing PR residency, losses on those shares can offset U.S.-sourced gains on your federal return. This is the primary scenario where tax loss harvesting remains valuable — for the U.S. tax side of your filing.

Swap strategy: You can sell IBIT at a loss and immediately buy FBTC (or vice versa). Because these are different ETFs from different issuers, the swap does not trigger the wash sale rule — even if wash sale rules were applied to Bitcoin ETFs, which remains uncertain for grantor trust structures. Your Bitcoin exposure stays the same; you just hold a different fund (BTC ETF Calc, Feb. 2026).

Losses Have Limited Value for Post-Move Gains

TokenTax and Koinly both noted that because personal capital gains are taxed at 0% under Act 60 for pre-2026 decree holders, capital losses on post-move assets provide essentially no benefit — there's no gain to offset. At the corporate level (Chapter 3), business losses can be deducted against export income before the 4% calculation. But for individual investors under Chapter 2, losses from post-move holdings are largely unusable on the PR side (TokenTax, Jun. 2025; Koinly, Jul. 2025).

Legislative Risk: The Velázquez Bill and Future of Crypto Tax Benefits

Act 60 decree holders should be aware that the tax treatment of Bitcoin ETFs in Puerto Rico is under active legislative scrutiny. In April 2025, Rep. Nydia Velázquez (D-NY) introduced the "Fair Taxation of Digital Assets in Puerto Rico Act" (H.R. 2982), which would amend IRC §865 to subject digital asset income earned by PR residents to U.S. income tax — effectively eliminating the Act 60 capital gains benefit for all cryptocurrency and Bitcoin ETF gains (Tax Notes, Nov. 2025).

Puerto Rico's Governor Jenniffer González-Colón responded by pushing through the Act 60 extension to 2055 and the HB 505 reform that raises the rate to 4% for new applicants — positioning PR as committed to the program long-term while addressing some of the criticism. The Velázquez bill has not advanced through committee as of early 2026, but it signals ongoing Congressional interest in limiting Act 60 benefits for digital asset investors specifically (Tax Notes, Nov. 2025; PR Advantage, Nov. 2025).

What This Means for ETF Holders

Current decree holders are grandfathered under existing rules. Your decree is a contract with the government of Puerto Rico and cannot be unilaterally modified. However, federal legislation could change the IRC §933 exclusion or §865 sourcing rules. If the Velázquez bill or similar legislation passes, PR-sourced crypto gains might no longer be excluded from federal tax — even for existing decree holders. This is a risk factor, not a certainty. Work with tax counsel who monitors legislative developments.

Compliance and Documentation for ETF Holders

The 2026 compliance environment for Act 60 decree holders with Bitcoin ETFs requires meticulous documentation. Windham Brannon noted that the new Act 60 compliance portal requires enhanced annual reporting, including a breakdown of income sources and Act 60-eligible activities. For crypto-related gains specifically, decree holders must now declare wallet addresses, transaction histories, and asset holdings to ensure gains are properly classified as post-residency (Windham Brannon, Feb. 2026).

Tag Every Lot as Pre-Move or Post-Move

For each ETF purchase, record the date, number of shares, cost basis, and whether you were a bona fide PR resident at the time of purchase. Shares purchased before residency are pre-move lots (subject to bifurcation or toll tax). Shares purchased after residency are post-move lots (fully eligible for Act 60). TokenTax recommends tagging each lot explicitly in your records (TokenTax, Jun. 2025).

Keep Brokerage Statements and 1099-B Forms

Your broker issues Form 1099-B showing proceeds, cost basis, and holding period for each sale. This is your primary documentation for the IRS. Ensure the brokerage account is in your name (not an entity's) for Chapter 2 purposes, and that the account address reflects your PR residence.

File Correctly Across Jurisdictions

Report post-move ETF gains on PR Form 482, claim exemption in Schedule B3, and attach your decree. On U.S. Form 1040, exclude PR-sourced gains under IRC §933 and report only U.S.-sourced income (including any pre-move appreciation). Ensure both returns are consistent — the IRS is cross-referencing PR and federal filings (Procopio, Dec. 2025; Pillsbury, Nov. 2025).

Document Fair Market Value at Residency Date

For pre-move ETF shares, record the FMV (share price) on the date you establish bona fide PR residency. This is the line that separates pre-move appreciation (U.S.-sourced) from post-move appreciation (PR-sourced). Use the closing price on your residency establishment date, documented with a screenshot or brokerage statement.

The Bottom Line

Spot Bitcoin ETFs are one of the cleanest investment vehicles for Act 60 Chapter 2 decree holders. Shares purchased after establishing bona fide PR residency generate capital gains that are PR-sourced under IRC §865(a), excluded from federal tax under IRC §933, and taxed at 0% (pre-2026 decree) or 4% (post-2025 decree) under Act 60.

The simplicity of ETF reporting through standard brokerage Form 1099-B makes compliance documentation cleaner than direct Bitcoin holdings. The absence of dividends, interest, or other income distributions means the only taxable event is the sale — giving you full control over when gains are realized.

The critical mistake to avoid is treating pre-move ETF shares as fully PR-sourced. Any appreciation that accrued before you established residency remains U.S.-sourced and subject to federal tax. Use the toll tax election or properly bifurcate gains under the 10-year lookback rule.

And keep your eye on Washington. The Velázquez bill and broader Congressional scrutiny of Act 60 crypto benefits represent a real, if uncertain, risk. Plan accordingly with qualified tax counsel who understands both Act 60 and the evolving federal regulatory landscape for digital assets.
