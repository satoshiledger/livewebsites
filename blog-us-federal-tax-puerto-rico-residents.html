US Federal Taxes and Puerto Rico: What Mainland Expats Need to Know Impuestos Federales de EE.UU. y Puerto Rico: Lo Que Expatriados del Continente Necesitan Saber

Share this article:Compartir este articulo:

One of the most common misconceptions about moving to Puerto Rico is that you stop paying federal taxes entirely. You don't. Puerto Rico is a U.S. territory — not a foreign country — and its residents remain U.S. citizens subject to specific federal tax obligations. What changes is which income the IRS can tax, not whether the IRS exists in your life.

The key mechanism is IRC Section 933, which excludes PR-sourced income from federal taxation for bona fide residents. But several categories of federal tax — payroll taxes, self-employment tax, taxes on U.S.-sourced income, and federal employment income — still apply in full. Understanding exactly where the federal obligation ends and the PR obligation begins is essential for every mainland expat relocating under Act 60.

The Core Rule

IRC §933(1): If you are a bona fide resident of Puerto Rico during the entire taxable year, income derived from sources within Puerto Rico is excluded from your U.S. federal gross income. The exclusion does not apply to income from services performed as a U.S. government employee (26 U.S.C. §933; LII/Cornell Law).

Federal Taxes You DON'T Pay as a Bona Fide PR Resident

Section 933 is the foundation of the Act 60 tax advantage. As a bona fide PR resident for the entire tax year, you exclude from federal income tax all income sourced within Puerto Rico. Combined with Act 60's favorable local rates, this creates the dramatic tax reduction that attracts mainland investors and entrepreneurs (LegalClarity, Dec. 2025; Anchin, Jul. 2025).

PR-Sourced Wages and Business Income

If you work for a private employer in Puerto Rico, or operate a business from Puerto Rico, and the services are performed in PR, that income is PR-sourced and excluded from federal tax under §933. It is instead subject to Puerto Rico income tax under the PR Internal Revenue Code — and potentially to the favorable 4% rate under an Act 60 Chapter 3 decree (Anchin, Jul. 2025; Florida Bar, Jan. 2019).

PR-Sourced Capital Gains (Post-Move Assets)

Capital gains on assets acquired after establishing bona fide PR residency are PR-sourced under IRC §865(a) and excluded from federal tax under §933. Under Act 60 Chapter 2, these gains are taxed at 0% (pre-2026 decree) or 4% (post-2025 decree) by Puerto Rico. Combined effect: near-zero total tax on post-move investment gains (Holland & Knight, Nov. 2025; LegalClarity, Dec. 2025).

PR-Sourced Interest and Dividends

Interest from PR-based financial institutions and dividends from PR corporations (where the corporation earns substantially all income from PR sources) are PR-sourced and excluded from federal tax. Under Act 60 Chapter 2, these are taxed at 0% or 4% locally. However, dividends from U.S. mainland corporations (e.g., Apple, Microsoft) are generally U.S.-sourced under IRC §861(a)(2) and remain federally taxable even for PR residents (LegalClarity, Dec. 2025; Evolved Tax, Sep. 2025).

Federal Taxes You STILL Pay in Puerto Rico

The §933 exclusion applies only to federal income tax on PR-sourced income. Several major federal tax obligations continue to apply in full regardless of your residency status:

FICA and Self-Employment Tax: The Tax Nobody Escapes

FICA taxes (Social Security and Medicare) apply in Puerto Rico identically to the mainland. The IRS confirmed that wages paid in PR are subject to FICA under the same rules that apply in the 50 states (IRS Topic 903; CPA Puerto Rico; PWC). The combined employee/employer rate is 15.3% (12.4% Social Security + 2.9% Medicare). Self-employed individuals pay the full 15.3% as self-employment tax, reported on Form 1040-SS for PR residents who are not otherwise required to file Form 1040 (IRS Form 1040-SS).

This is the most significant federal tax that continues for PR residents. Even if your PR-sourced business income is exempt from federal income tax under §933 and taxed at just 4% by Puerto Rico under Act 60, you still owe 15.3% in self-employment tax on your net self-employment earnings to the IRS. For a business owner netting $300,000 from a PR-based export services company, that's approximately $38,600 in self-employment tax — paid to Washington, not San Juan (IRS Topic 903; IRS Pub. 570).

U.S.-Sourced Income: Still Fully Taxable

Section 933 only excludes PR-sourced income. Any income derived from U.S. sources remains subject to federal income tax at full rates, even if you are a bona fide PR resident. Common examples for mainland expats who relocate include rental income from U.S. properties you retained, dividends from U.S. corporations, interest from U.S. bank accounts, income from services physically performed in the U.S. (business trips, client visits), and Social Security benefits (may be partially taxable) (LegalClarity, Dec. 2025; US Expat Tax Service, Jul. 2025).

Anchin noted that for C-corporations operating in both the U.S. and Puerto Rico, the sourcing analysis must determine how much work is performed in each location and apply that ratio to dividends. If the PR threshold requirement isn't met, the dividend income may not qualify for the §933 exclusion (Anchin, Jul. 2025).

Federal Government Employees: No Exclusion

Section 933 explicitly carves out an exception: income received for services performed as an employee of the United States or any agency thereof is not excludable, even if you are a bona fide PR resident and the services are performed in Puerto Rico. Federal employees, military personnel, federal judges, and contractors working for federal agencies owe federal income tax on those wages regardless of residence (26 U.S.C. §933(1); LegalClarity, Dec. 2025; IRS Pub. 1321, Oct. 2025). Note: employees of the Puerto Rico government are not considered U.S. government employees and their wages are excludable under §933.

Federal Filing Requirements for PR Residents

Whether you must file a U.S. federal return depends on whether you have any non-PR-sourced income above the filing threshold. Here's the decision framework:

Scenario A: All Income PR-Sourced, No U.S.-Sourced Income

If you are a bona fide PR resident for the entire tax year and all your income is from sources within Puerto Rico, you generally are not required to file a U.S. federal income tax return (Form 1040). However, you must still file Form 1040-SS if you have self-employment income, to report and pay self-employment tax (IRS Form 1040-SS; US Expat Tax Service, Jul. 2025).

You file only the PR income tax return (Form 482 for individuals or Form 480.20 for corporations) reporting worldwide income and claiming Act 60 exemptions.

Scenario B: Some U.S.-Sourced Income

If you have any income from sources outside of Puerto Rico — U.S. rental income, U.S. dividends, income from services performed on the mainland — and that income exceeds the standard filing threshold, you must file Form 1040. On the Form 1040, you report worldwide income, then exclude the PR-sourced income under §933. You pay federal tax only on the non-excluded (U.S.-sourced) portion (IRS Pub. 1321, Oct. 2025; LegalClarity, Dec. 2025).

You also file the PR Form 482, reporting worldwide income and paying PR tax on the PR-sourced portion. The result is dual filing — but ideally not double taxation, because each jurisdiction taxes different income streams.

Scenario C: Year of Move (Transition Year)

The year you establish PR residency is the most complex. If you qualify as a bona fide PR resident for the entire year (possible if you moved early enough and meet the presence, tax home, and closer connection tests using the year-of-move exception in Treas. Reg. §1.937-1(f)(1)), you can claim the §933 exclusion for the full year. Otherwise, you must prorate income between the pre-move period (federally taxable) and post-move period (potentially excludable) (IRS Pub. 570; Evolved Tax, Sep. 2025).

File Form 8898 in the year of your move to notify the IRS of your change in residency status. This is not optional — failure to file is a red flag the IRS specifically looks for when auditing Act 60 decree holders (IRS Form 8898; Procopio, Dec. 2025).

Allocating Deductions Between Jurisdictions

When you exclude PR-sourced income under §933, you cannot claim deductions or credits allocable to that excluded income on your federal return. Section 933 states this explicitly: no deductions (other than the personal exemption) or credits properly allocable to excluded income are allowed (26 U.S.C. §933; 26 CFR §1.933-1(c)).

IRS Publication 1321 provides the allocation methodology: multiply each deduction by a fraction where the numerator is gross income subject to federal tax and the denominator is total gross income from all sources (including the excluded PR income). For example, if 80% of your total income is PR-sourced and excluded, you can only claim 20% of your itemized deductions (like real estate taxes and mortgage interest) on your federal return (IRS Pub. 1321, Oct. 2025).

Common Mistake

Many expats claim full itemized deductions on their federal return even when most of their income is excluded under §933. The IRS requires proportional allocation. If audited, claiming full deductions against a small amount of federally taxable income while excluding the bulk under §933 will trigger an adjustment and potential penalties.

Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT) for PR Residents

The 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT) under IRC §1411 applies to bona fide PR residents who have a federal income tax return filing obligation — specifically, if their modified adjusted gross income from non-territory sources exceeds the threshold ($200,000 single / $250,000 married filing jointly). PR-sourced investment income excluded under §933 is not subject to NIIT, but U.S.-sourced investment income (dividends from U.S. corporations, interest from U.S. banks, rental income from U.S. property) can trigger the tax (IRS Pub. 570, 2025).

This matters for Act 60 residents who retain significant U.S. investments. If you hold a diversified portfolio with U.S.-sourced dividends and interest, those amounts count toward both your federal income tax obligation and the NIIT threshold. Plan your investment holdings accordingly — shifting to PR-based assets or tax-advantaged accounts can reduce NIIT exposure.

Pre-Move Assets and the 10-Year Lookback

Assets you owned before establishing PR residency get special treatment. Under Treas. Reg. §1.937-2(f), if you were a U.S. citizen or resident alien (but not a PR bona fide resident) during any of the 10 years preceding the sale, gains on investment property owned before your move are not considered PR-sourced — even if you sell them as a PR resident. The gain attributable to the pre-move holding period remains U.S.-sourced and subject to federal capital gains tax (Holland & Knight, Nov. 2025; Evolved Tax, Sep. 2025).

This is the rule that prevents the "buy on the mainland, sell in Puerto Rico" strategy. Only appreciation that accrues after you establish bona fide PR residency qualifies as PR-sourced under Act 60. The pre-move appreciation is bifurcated and remains federally taxable, or you can elect the 5% toll tax under Act 60-2019, §2021.01(e) to settle the pre-move liability at a flat rate.

Annual Filing Checklist for Mainland Expats in PR

1. PR Income Tax Return (Form 482) — Due April 15

Report worldwide income. Claim Act 60 exemptions in Schedule B3. Attach your decree. PR taxes PR-sourced income; U.S.-sourced income is reported but exempt from PR tax under the mirror of §933.

2. U.S. Form 1040 (if applicable) — Due April 15 (auto-extension to June 15 for PR residents)

Report worldwide income. Exclude PR-sourced income under §933. Pay federal tax on U.S.-sourced income only. Allocate deductions proportionally using the IRS Publication 1321 methodology. Bona fide PR residents receive an automatic 2-month extension to June 15, but tax owed is still due April 15 (IRS Pub. 1321).

3. Form 1040-SS (if no Form 1040 required) — Due April 15

If all your income is PR-sourced and you have no federal income tax filing obligation, but you have self-employment income, file Form 1040-SS to report and pay self-employment tax (Social Security + Medicare) to the IRS (IRS Form 1040-SS).

4. Form 8898 (year of move only)

File in the year you establish bona fide PR residency. Notifies the IRS of your residency change. Failure to file is a red flag. Attach to your Form 1040 or file separately (IRS Form 8898).

5. Ensure Consistency Between Returns

The income excluded on your federal return under §933 must match the PR-sourced income reported on your PR return. The IRS and Hacienda are now sharing data, and any discrepancy between your Form 1040 and Form 482 will be identified (Procopio, Dec. 2025; Windham Brannon, Feb. 2026).

The Bottom Line

Moving to Puerto Rico under Act 60 does not make you invisible to the IRS. You remain a U.S. citizen, you still pay FICA and self-employment tax, you still owe federal tax on any U.S.-sourced income, and you still must file federal returns when applicable. What Section 933 does is exclude your PR-sourced income from federal income tax — and when combined with Act 60's favorable local rates, the result is a dramatic reduction in your total tax burden.

The key to making this work is clean income sourcing. Every dollar must be classified as either PR-sourced (excluded from federal tax, subject to PR tax at Act 60 rates) or U.S.-sourced (subject to federal tax, excluded from PR tax). Get the sourcing wrong, and you face both federal penalties for under-reporting and potential loss of your Act 60 decree.

Work with both a U.S. CPA and a PR CPA — or one firm handling both jurisdictions — to ensure your returns are consistent, your deductions are properly allocated, and your income streams are correctly sourced. The cost of professional compliance is a fraction of the savings Act 60 provides when done right.
