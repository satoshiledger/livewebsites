LLC vs S-Corp vs C-Corp in Puerto Rico: Which Is Right for Your Business? LLC vs S-Corp vs C-Corp en Puerto Rico: ¿Cuál Es Correcta para Su Negocio?

Written by the team at Satoshi Ledger LLC — Puerto Rico tax compliance and business formation specialists. Last reviewed March 27, 2026.

Share this article: Compartir este articulo:

If you're forming a business in Puerto Rico in 2026, you're facing one of the most consequential decisions in your company's lifecycle: choosing between an LLC, S-Corp, or C-Corp structure. Get this wrong, and you could leave tens of thousands of dollars on the table—or worse, trigger unintended tax consequences that follow you for years.

Here's what makes Puerto Rico uniquely complex: the island operates under its own tax code (the Puerto Rico Internal Revenue Code of 2011, as amended), separate from the U.S. federal system. Entity classification rules changed dramatically when Act 52-2022 was signed into law on June 30, 2022. That legislation expanded Puerto Rico's entity classification framework for the first time to include two new categories: disregarded entities and conduit entities (McConnell Valdés).

Puerto Rico does not have a traditional "S-Corp" election like the mainland United States. Instead, the island uses pass-through entities (formerly called "Corporations of Individuals") and disregarded entities—concepts that are similar but governed by entirely different rules.

This guide will break down the real-world tax treatment of each entity type, explain the critical 2022 reforms, and show you—with dollar-for-dollar examples—which structure makes sense for your business.

Understanding Puerto Rico's Entity Classification System

Critical Point

Under Puerto Rico's tax code, LLCs are taxed as corporations by default. To be treated as a pass-through entity or disregarded entity, the owner must proactively file an election with the Puerto Rico Treasury (Grant Thornton PR, "Three Become One"). This is the opposite of U.S. federal treatment, where single-member LLCs are disregarded by default.

The Three Core Entity Types in Puerto Rico

C-Corporation

Default classification for LLCs. Taxed at entity level. Preferred for Act 60 decree holders.

Pass-Through Entity

Similar to S-Corp or partnership. Income flows to owners. Election required.

Disregarded Entity

Single-member LLC only. Ignored for tax purposes. New in 2022.

Following the 2022 reforms, any corporation, partnership, or LLC in Puerto Rico may now elect pass-through entity treatment, subjecting the entity to the same partnership rules under the PR Code (Grant Thornton PR, "Three Become One"). This flexibility is powerful—but it requires proactive election and annual maintenance.

Option 1: C-Corporation (LLC Taxed as Corporation)

How It Works

Under Puerto Rico's Internal Revenue Code, a limited liability company formed in the territory is, by default, taxed as a corporation at corporate tax rates (Grant Thornton PR, "Disregarded Entities"). Most business owners never file an election—they simply accept the default C-Corp treatment.

Standard Corporate Tax Rates (Without Incentives)

Puerto Rico's progressive corporate tax system can approach 37.5% at higher income levels (Puerto Rico Internal Revenue Code of 2011). The effective rate depends on your net taxable income. For standard corporations without tax incentive decrees, Puerto Rico's rates are among the highest in the U.S. and its territories.

Reality Check

A 37.5% top corporate rate is a significant burden for any business. The value proposition for Puerto Rico lies not in the standard rates, but in the tax incentive decrees available under Act 60-2019.

C-Corp + Act 60 Decree: The Optimal Combination

The vast majority of Act 60 decree holders structure their businesses as Puerto Rico LLCs that elect to be taxed as C corporations. Puerto Rico does not offer an S-Corp election—and under Act 60, there is no need for one. Dividends distributed from exempt operations are 100% exempt from PR income tax for bona fide Puerto Rico residents, eliminating the traditional "double taxation" concern associated with C-Corp structures (Act 60-2019, Chapter 3; note: dividends distributed to non-residents may be subject to PR withholding tax).

When C-Corp Makes Sense

  • You hold (or plan to apply for) an Act 60 export services decree
  • You want dividend distributions to be 100% tax-free (as a PR resident)
  • You need the legal separation between entity and owner income
  • You're comfortable with the "reasonable salary" compliance requirement
  • Your business generates significant retained earnings

Option 2: Pass-Through Entity (Puerto Rico's "S-Corp" Equivalent)

How It Works

Puerto Rico's pass-through entity functions similarly to a federal S-Corp: the entity itself pays no corporate income tax, and income flows directly to shareholders who report it on their individual returns (CPA Puerto Rico). Before Act 52-2022, this structure was known as a "Corporation of Individuals" under the PR Code.

Under amendments introduced by Act 52-2022, effective for taxable years beginning on or after January 1, 2022, entities that were previously taxed as a Corporation of Individuals were automatically converted to Pass-Through Entities under the unified PTE framework (Grant Thornton PR, "Three Become One"). The terminology changed, but the core concept remains the same: income flows through to the owners.

Who Can Elect Pass-Through Treatment

Act 52-2022 Reform: Eligibility Requirements Were Eliminated

Under the old "Corporation of Individuals" regime, entities had to satisfy strict gross income tests to qualify for pass-through treatment. Act 52-2022 eliminated those thresholds entirely.

As of January 1, 2022, any corporation, limited liability company (LLC), or partnership may elect to be treated as a Pass-Through Entity—regardless of income composition, activity type, or passive income levels. PTEs that were formerly Special Partnerships or Corporations of Individuals no longer have to comply with the 70% or 90% gross income requirements or any specific activity requirements (Grant Thornton PR, "Three Become One"; RSM Puerto Rico).

The election is made by filing Form SC 6045 together with the income tax return for the taxable year for which the election is effective. All owners must consent. Once made, the entity cannot change its classification again for 60 months (Administrative Determination 23-01).

How Income Is Taxed

All income items—gains, losses, deductions, and credits—retain their character as they pass through to each owner, who then incorporates them into their individual Puerto Rico tax return (CPA Puerto Rico). The entity itself files an informational return but owes no entity-level income tax.

If the owner is a Puerto Rico resident individual, this income is subject to Puerto Rico's progressive individual income tax rates, which range from 0% on income up to $9,000 to 33% on income over $61,500 (Puerto Rico Internal Revenue Code of 2011). Note that high-income earners may also be subject to Puerto Rico's Alternative Basic Tax (ABT) under Section 1021 and the Gradual Adjustment Tax under Section 1022 of the PR Code, which can push effective rates above the standard bracket rates shown.

Self-Employment Tax Consideration

Puerto Rico residents who are not required to file a U.S. income tax return must still file Form 1040-SS to report self-employment income and, if applicable, pay self-employment tax to the U.S. federal government (IRS Publication 570). This is a 15.3% federal tax on net earnings from self-employment—a significant cost that C-Corp structures can minimize through reasonable salary planning.

Note on Form 1040-SS: Form 1040-PR (the Spanish-language equivalent) was discontinued after tax year 2022. Form 1040-SS now serves all Puerto Rico self-employment filers in both English and Spanish, effective for tax year 2023 and forward (IRS).

When Pass-Through Makes Sense

  • Your business primarily serves Puerto Rico clients (not eligible for Act 60 export services)
  • You prefer simplicity—no reasonable salary calculations
  • Your income is below the top PR individual tax bracket
  • You want to avoid corporate-level tax entirely
  • You're comfortable with self-employment tax obligations

Option 3: Disregarded Entity (Single-Member LLC)

The 2022 Game-Changer

Act 52-2022 recognized disregarded entities for Puerto Rico income tax purposes for the first time (Grant Thornton PR, "Disregarded Entities"). Before 2022, every LLC was taxed as a corporation unless it elected pass-through treatment. The legislature's stated goal was to simplify compliance for Puerto Rico's working class and align the island with treatment available in all U.S. states (Act 52-2022, Statement of Motives).

DRE Effective Dates — Two Timelines

  • PR LLCs with a single PR resident individual owner: DRE treatment available for taxable years beginning after December 31, 2021.
  • Foreign (non-PR) single-member LLCs: DRE treatment available for taxable years beginning after December 31, 2022.

(McConnell Valdés; RSM Puerto Rico)

How It Works

A disregarded entity is a legal entity that is ignored as separate from its owner solely for income tax computation purposes (Grant Thornton PR, "Disregarded Entities"). The entity is not required to file a separate income tax return; all income, expenses, gains, and losses are reported directly on the owner's personal Puerto Rico tax return (Form 482)—exactly like a sole proprietorship, but with the legal liability protection of an LLC (RSM Puerto Rico).

Important Limitation

DRE treatment is available only to single-member LLCs whose sole owner is a PR resident individual (RSM Puerto Rico). If you have multiple owners, or if the sole owner is not a PR resident individual, you must choose between C-Corp or pass-through treatment.

Tax Treatment

Income from a disregarded entity is treated identically to self-employment income. The owner reports all activity on their individual Puerto Rico tax return (Form 482) and, if required, pays self-employment tax to the U.S. federal government on Form 1040-SS (IRS Publication 570).

Note: although the entity is ignored for income tax purposes, it is still required to file all other tax returns for which business entities are responsible—including municipal license taxes, property taxes, employment taxes, informative returns, and Sales and Use Tax (Grant Thornton PR, "Disregarded Entities").

When Disregarded Entity Makes Sense

  • You're a solo entrepreneur or freelancer with no employees
  • Your annual net income is below $75,000
  • You want maximum simplicity—one tax return, no payroll
  • You don't qualify for Act 60 (e.g., local clients only)
  • You want to match U.S. federal tax treatment for easier compliance

Annual Filing Requirements & Deadlines

Regardless of entity type, all Puerto Rico businesses face rigorous filing obligations with both Puerto Rico Hacienda (Department of the Treasury) and—if applicable—the IRS.

C-Corporation Filing Requirements

  • Form 480.20 (Corporation Income Tax Return) – Due the 15th day of the fourth month following the close of the taxable year (April 15 for calendar-year filers) (Puerto Rico Internal Revenue Code of 2011)
  • Quarterly estimated tax payments required if liability exceeds $1,000
  • LLCs pay an annual $150 fee to the Puerto Rico Department of State, due on or before April 15 each year; failure to file or pay results in penalties (Puerto Rico Department of State)
  • Audited financial statements required if annual revenue exceeds $3,000,000 (RSM Puerto Rico)

Pass-Through Entity Filing Requirements

  • Form 480.30 (Pass-Through Entity Return) – Due the 15th day of the third month following the close of the taxable year (March 15 for calendar-year filers) (Puerto Rico Internal Revenue Code of 2011)
  • Individual partners/shareholders file Form 482 (Individual Return) by April 15
  • Must issue K-1 equivalent informative schedules to all owners
  • Quarterly estimated tax payments required at the owner level

Disregarded Entity Filing Requirements

  • No separate entity income tax return—all income reported on owner's Form 482 (RSM Puerto Rico)
  • Individual return due April 15
  • Must maintain separate books and records for the LLC
  • Annual $150 LLC fee due to Puerto Rico Department of State
  • U.S. Form 1040-SS required for self-employment tax reporting (IRS Publication 570)
  • Entity must still file municipal license taxes, property taxes, and Sales and Use Tax returns separately (Grant Thornton PR, "Disregarded Entities")

How to Choose: Decision Framework

Choose C-Corporation If:

  • You're applying for (or already hold) an Act 60 export services decree
  • Your primary revenue comes from clients outside Puerto Rico
  • You want 100% tax-free dividend distributions (as a PR resident)
  • You're comfortable setting and documenting a reasonable salary
  • Your business generates over $150,000 in annual net income

Choose Pass-Through Entity If:

  • Your business primarily serves local Puerto Rico clients (not export-eligible)
  • You prefer flow-through treatment to avoid entity-level tax
  • You have multiple owners and want profit-sharing flexibility
  • You don't qualify for Act 60 incentives
  • Your net income is below $100,000 annually

Choose Disregarded Entity If:

  • You're a solo entrepreneur with no employees or co-owners, and you are a PR resident individual
  • Your net income is below $75,000 annually
  • You want maximum simplicity—one tax return, no corporate formalities
  • You serve local clients only and don't qualify for Act 60
  • You want alignment with U.S. federal tax treatment

Warning: Entity Election Is Not Easily Reversible

Act 52-2022 clarifies that once an entity elects to change its tax classification, it cannot request another change for 60 months (Administrative Determination 23-01). Special rules also apply when changing from a pass-through entity to a corporation or disregarded entity, or vice versa—these conversions can trigger deemed sales, liquidations, and other tax events (McConnell Valdés). Get this right from day one.
