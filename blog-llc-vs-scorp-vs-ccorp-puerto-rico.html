LLC vs S-Corp vs C-Corp in Puerto Rico: Which Is Right for Your Business? LLC vs S-Corp vs C-Corp en Puerto Rico: ¿Cuál Es Correcta para Su Negocio?

Share this article: Compartir este articulo:

Choosing the right business entity in Puerto Rico is not the same exercise as choosing one on the mainland. The rules are different, the tax defaults are different, and if you're operating under an Act 60 export services decree, the entity decision directly controls whether you pay 4% or 33% on your business income. Get the structure wrong, and you could forfeit the very tax benefit that brought you to the island.

This guide breaks down the three primary business structures available in Puerto Rico—the LLC, the S-Corp equivalent (called a "Corporation of Individuals"), and the C-Corp—with a focus on how each interacts with Act 60. We'll also cover Puerto Rico's unique LLC default rule, the disregarded entity option introduced by Act 52-2022, and why the overwhelming majority of Act 60 decree holders use an LLC taxed as a C corporation.

This is not generic "which entity is best" advice. This is Puerto Rico-specific guidance for entrepreneurs, service providers, and Bitcoin investors who need to structure their operations for maximum compliance and minimum tax under Act 60.

The Critical Difference: Puerto Rico LLCs Default to Corporate Taxation

If you're coming from the mainland, the single most important thing to understand about Puerto Rico business entities is this: a Puerto Rico LLC is, by default, taxed as a corporation under Puerto Rico's Internal Revenue Code. This is the opposite of the U.S. federal default, where a single-member LLC is treated as a disregarded entity and a multi-member LLC is treated as a partnership (Grant Thornton PR, "Puerto Rico Opens its Doors for Disregarded Entities").

This means that if you form a PR LLC and do nothing else, your entity will be subject to corporate-level income tax and any distributions to you as a member will be treated as dividends—creating the classic double-taxation structure. The standard corporate tax rate in Puerto Rico is comprised of an 18.5% base tax plus a graduated surtax, reaching an effective combined rate of up to approximately 37.5% on income above certain thresholds (PwC Tax Summaries, "Puerto Rico – Corporate – Taxes on Corporate Income").

Why This Matters for Act 60

Under Act 60's export services incentive (Chapter 3), the corporate tax rate drops to just 4% on qualifying income, and dividends distributed from the exempt operation are 100% exempt from Puerto Rico income tax (Act 60-2019, Chapter 3, Sections 2031.01 et seq.). This completely eliminates the double-taxation concern. It is precisely why the LLC-taxed-as-C-Corporation structure is the dominant approach for Act 60 decree holders—you get corporate-level taxation at 4% plus tax-free dividends, combined with the liability protection and operational flexibility of an LLC (PuertoRicoLLC.com, "LLC vs Corporation: Reasonable Salary Act 60 Guide 2026").

A PR LLC can elect alternative tax treatment. By filing Form SC 6045 with the Puerto Rico Treasury Department, an LLC may elect to be treated as a pass-through entity (partnership) or, since Act 52-2022, as a disregarded entity for single-member LLCs (Grant Thornton PR; RSM Puerto Rico, "Treasury Department Issues Guidance on Entity Classification Provisions"). However, these elections change the tax calculus significantly and are not necessarily advantageous under Act 60.

Entity-by-Entity Breakdown

Puerto Rico LLC (Limited Liability Company)

The Puerto Rico LLC is formed by filing a Certificate of Formation with the PR Department of State. The filing fee is $250, and processing is typically one business day with expedited options available ($100 for 24-hour processing, $500 for 2-hour processing) (14 LPRA 3952; PR Department of State). The LLC must include "LLC" or "Limited Liability Company" in its name and maintain a registered agent with a physical Puerto Rico address.

Operationally, LLCs in Puerto Rico enjoy the same flexibility found on the mainland: no requirement for a board of directors, no mandatory annual meetings, fewer formalities than a corporation, and management flexibility through the operating agreement (BizLatinHub, "Entity Types in Puerto Rico: A Guide to Business Structures"). Members are shielded from personal liability for the entity's obligations.

Advantages

Limited liability protection for members

Flexible management structure—no board required

Can elect corporate, partnership, or disregarded entity tax treatment

Preferred structure for Act 60 export services

Lower formation cost ($250) with fast processing

Disadvantages

Defaults to corporate taxation (unlike mainland U.S.)

Must affirmatively elect pass-through treatment via Form SC 6045

Cannot issue stock (no equity fundraising via shares)

May confuse mainland-based advisors unfamiliar with PR rules

Act 60 Bottom Line: The LLC taxed as a C corporation is the gold standard for Act 60 Chapter 3 (Export Services) decree holders. Corporate income is taxed at 4%, and dividends to the owner are 100% exempt. The "double taxation" that normally makes C-Corp structures unattractive is completely neutralized by the Act 60 dividend exemption.

Puerto Rico C Corporation

A Puerto Rico C corporation is formed by filing a Certificate of Incorporation with the PR Department of State. The filing fee is $150 for a for-profit corporation (Christie's Real Estate PR, "PR Real Estate: Owning vs. LLC"). Unlike an LLC, a corporation requires appointment of directors and officers at formation, must hold annual board meetings, record minutes, and comply with stricter corporate formalities.

Without an Act 60 decree, a domestic corporation faces the standard Puerto Rico corporate tax rate: an 18.5% normal tax plus a graduated surtax that brings the effective rate to approximately 37.5% on higher income levels (PwC Tax Summaries). Dividends distributed to nonresident individuals or foreign shareholders are subject to a 10% withholding tax (PF-CPA, "Domestic Corporation"). However, if the corporation holds an Act 60 Chapter 3 decree, the rate drops to 4% with fully exempt dividend distributions.

Advantages

Can issue stock for equity fundraising

Established corporate governance structure

Lower filing fee ($150)

Recognized legal structure for larger enterprises

Disadvantages

More formalities: board meetings, minutes, annual reports

Double taxation without Act 60 (37.5% corporate + 10% dividend withholding)

Less management flexibility than an LLC

Cannot elect pass-through treatment as easily as an LLC

Act 60 Bottom Line: A C-Corp with an Act 60 decree functions identically to an LLC-taxed-as-C-Corp for tax purposes: 4% corporate rate, tax-free dividends. The choice between forming an actual C-Corp versus an LLC-taxed-as-C-Corp often comes down to operational preferences—the LLC's lighter formality requirements make it the more popular choice for sole operators and small teams.

Puerto Rico "S-Corp" — The Corporation of Individuals

Puerto Rico does not recognize the federal S-Corp election (Form 2553) for Puerto Rico income tax purposes. Instead, Puerto Rico has its own pass-through entity structure called the "Corporation of Individuals" (Corporación de Individuos), which functions similarly to an S-Corp on the mainland (PF-CPA, "Corporation of Individuals").

A Corporation of Individuals is not subject to corporate-level income tax. Instead, income, deductions, gains, losses, and credits flow through to the shareholders, who report them on their individual tax returns—just like a mainland S-Corp or partnership. To qualify, the entity must file an election with the Puerto Rico Treasury, and at least 90% of its gross income must be derived from a trade or business in Puerto Rico, with passive income limited to no more than 10% (PF-CPA, "Corporation of Individuals").

Here is the critical restriction: corporations that enjoy tax exemption under Act 60's Incentives Acts are ineligible for Corporation of Individuals treatment (PF-CPA, "Corporation of Individuals"). This means that if you hold an Act 60 decree, you cannot use this pass-through structure for the exempt entity.

Advantages

Pass-through taxation—no corporate-level tax

Avoids double taxation for non-Act 60 businesses

Losses can pass through to shareholders

Disadvantages

Cannot be used with Act 60 decrees

90% PR-sourced income requirement

Passive income capped at 10%

All income flows through at individual PR rates (up to 33%)

Act 60 Bottom Line: Not available for Act 60 decree holders. The Corporation of Individuals is useful for Puerto Rico-based businesses that do not hold an Act 60 decree and want pass-through taxation to avoid corporate-level tax. But for anyone with an export services decree, this option is off the table.

The Disregarded Entity Option (Act 52-2022)

Before 2022, Puerto Rico did not recognize disregarded entities for local income tax purposes. This created a significant disconnect: a single-member LLC that was a disregarded entity for U.S. federal tax purposes was still treated as a corporation in Puerto Rico. Act 52-2022 (Puerto Rico Public Finances Stabilization Act) changed this by allowing single-member Puerto Rico LLCs to elect disregarded entity treatment for PR income tax purposes (Grant Thornton PR, "Puerto Rico Opens its Doors for Disregarded Entities").

Under the disregarded entity election, the LLC is ignored for income tax purposes, and all income, deductions, and credits are reported directly on the owner's individual Puerto Rico tax return. This simplifies compliance and eliminates the corporate-level return entirely. The election is made by filing Form SC 6045 electronically with the corresponding income tax return (RSM Puerto Rico, "Treasury Department Issues Guidance on Entity Classification Provisions").

Important Caveat for Act 60 Holders

While the disregarded entity option provides welcome simplification for many Puerto Rico business owners, it is not the preferred structure for Act 60 export services. The LLC-taxed-as-C-Corporation approach remains dominant for Act 60 because it creates the necessary separation between corporate income (taxed at 4%) and the owner's personal compensation (taxed at ordinary PR rates). Without this entity-level separation, the Act 60 salary-versus-dividends optimization strategy does not work (PuertoRicoLLC.com, "LLC vs Corporation: Reasonable Salary Act 60 Guide 2026").

The disregarded entity election is most useful for solo entrepreneurs with non-Act 60 businesses, real estate holding entities, or administrative LLCs that do not generate Act 60-qualifying income.

The Reasonable Salary Rule: Why Entity Structure Matters

Under Act 60's export services incentive, every business owner and employee must be paid a reasonable salary for the work they perform, and that salary is taxed at Puerto Rico's ordinary individual income tax rates—up to 33% (PR Internal Revenue Code of 2011, as amended). Only the profit remaining after salary and other expenses flows through at the 4% Act 60 rate. Dividends from the exempt operation are then distributed tax-free.

This creates a powerful optimization dynamic: every dollar allocated to salary is taxed at up to 33%, while every dollar that remains as corporate profit is taxed at just 4% and distributed as a tax-free dividend. Setting a salary that is defensibly reasonable—high enough to withstand an audit by the Office of Industrial Tax Exemption (OITE) and Hacienda, but not so high that it unnecessarily surrenders the tax benefit—is the central tax planning challenge for Act 60 decree holders.

How This Compares to Mainland S-Corp Strategy

On the mainland, S-Corp owner-employees minimize salary to reduce the 15.3% FICA tax on wages, since S-Corp distributions are not subject to self-employment tax. In Puerto Rico under Act 60, the incentive to minimize salary is far larger: the differential is not 15.3% but potentially up to 29 percentage points (33% salary rate vs. 4% corporate rate). However, the risk of setting salary too low is also higher—OITE actively reviews salary levels during compliance audits, and the consequences of being found noncompliant can include losing the decree entirely (PuertoRicoLLC.com, "LLC vs Corporation: Reasonable Salary Act 60 Guide 2026").

This salary-versus-dividends optimization is only possible when there is entity-level taxation—which is why the C-Corp or LLC-taxed-as-C-Corp structure is essential for Act 60. Pass-through structures like the Corporation of Individuals or an LLC taxed as a partnership do not create this separation; all income flows directly to the individual at individual rates.

IRS Sourcing and Entity Risks: What the Gajwani Case Taught Us

The choice of entity has implications not only for Puerto Rico taxes but also for how the IRS analyzes your income sourcing. In the high-profile Gajwani prosecution, the defendant attempted to use an S-Corp election to re-characterize pre-move gains as Puerto Rico-sourced. The IRS rejected this position, and the IRS Office of Chief Counsel subsequently issued guidance (AM 2024-005) confirming that S corporations cannot rely on IRC Section 937 (which applies only to individuals) to source income to Puerto Rico (Holland & Knight, "Puerto Rico's Act 60, Income Sourcing and IRS Scrutiny in the Age of Cryptocurrency").

Additionally, tax professionals at Pillsbury Law have noted that an S corporation, being a U.S. resident entity by definition, cannot invoke the sourcing rules that apply to individuals who have established PR residency (Pillsbury Law, "Uncertainty Clouds Puerto Rico's Act 60 Tax Breaks"). This means that using an S-Corp to hold appreciated assets and then claiming Act 60 benefits on the gains is a strategy the IRS is actively targeting.

The takeaway for entity selection: use structures that are straightforward and consistent with IRS guidance. An LLC taxed as a C-Corp in Puerto Rico, paired with a properly documented reasonable salary, is the approach that has the strongest compliance foundation. Exotic entity arrangements designed to re-source income invite scrutiny.

Which Structure Is Right for Your Business?

You Hold (or Plan to Apply for) an Act 60 Export Services Decree

Best choice: Puerto Rico LLC taxed as a C Corporation. This gives you liability protection, minimal formalities, and the entity-level taxation required for the 4% rate plus tax-free dividends. This is the structure used by the vast majority of Act 60 decree holders.

You're Building a Larger Enterprise with Investors

Consider: Puerto Rico C Corporation. If you need to issue stock, raise equity capital, or plan for a future acquisition, a formal corporation may be more appropriate. It can also hold an Act 60 decree with identical tax benefits. The trade-off is greater formality and governance requirements.

You're a Solo Operator Without an Act 60 Decree

Consider: PR LLC with disregarded entity election or Corporation of Individuals. If you are not operating under Act 60 and want simplicity, the disregarded entity option (for single-member LLCs since Act 52-2022) or the Corporation of Individuals pass-through election may reduce your compliance burden and avoid double taxation.

You're a Bitcoin Investor with a Chapter 2 Decree

Entity structure is less critical. Act 60 Chapter 2 (Individual Resident Investors) applies to your personal capital gains, interest, and dividends—not to business income. You may not need a business entity at all for your investment activity, though some investors form holding structures for asset protection or estate planning. Consult your tax attorney on the specifics.

Quick Formation Checklist for a PR LLC

1

Choose Your Entity Name

Verify availability with the PR Department of State Registry of Corporations and Entities. Must include "LLC" or "Limited Liability Company."

2

Designate a Registered Agent

Must be a person or entity with a physical address in Puerto Rico. The registered agent receives official correspondence and legal notices.

3

File the Certificate of Formation

File online with the PR Department of State. Filing fee: $250. Processing: approximately 1 business day (expedited options available).

4

Draft an Operating Agreement

While not required to be filed, an operating agreement governs management, profit distribution, and member rights. Essential for multi-member LLCs and strongly recommended for single-member LLCs.

5

Obtain an EIN and Register with SURI

Obtain a federal Employer Identification Number (EIN) from the IRS. Then register with Puerto Rico's SURI (Sistema Unificado de Rentas Internas) portal at Hacienda for local tax purposes.

6

Elect Tax Classification (if Not Accepting Default)

If you want your LLC treated as a pass-through or disregarded entity rather than a corporation, file Form SC 6045 with your first PR income tax return. If you are using the default C-Corp treatment (standard for Act 60), no additional election is needed.

7

Apply for Your Act 60 Decree (If Applicable)

Submit your export services decree application through the DDEC Single Business Portal. Processing typically takes 3–6 months.
