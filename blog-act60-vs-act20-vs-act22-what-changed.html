Act 38-2026 Is Now Law: Everything That Changed for Act 60 Individual Investors La Ley 38-2026 Es Ahora Ley: Todo Lo Que Cambió para Inversionistas Individuales de la Ley 60

Written by the team at Satoshi Ledger LLC — Puerto Rico tax compliance and business formation specialists. Published March 27, 2026.

Share this article: Compartir este articulo:

Breaking: Puerto Rico's governor signed Act 38-2026 into law in March 2026, officially amending the Act 60 Individual Investor program. This article covers every change and what it means for current decree holders, pending applicants, and prospective investors.

For months, investors, tax attorneys, and relocation advisors across the country have been tracking House Bill 505 — the Puerto Rico legislation that would reshape the Act 60 Individual Investor program. The waiting is over. Act 38-2026 is now signed law.

The headline: the 0% capital gains rate is still available — but the deadline to lock it in is now December 31, 2026. Applicants after that date face a 4% rate. The program itself has been extended through 2055. And several eligibility and compliance rules have changed in ways that will affect tax planning for both new and existing decree holders.

Here is a precise breakdown of every change Act 38-2026 made, what stayed the same, and what you need to do depending on where you stand today.

Change 1: The 0% Deadline Was Extended to December 31, 2026

This is the change most investors have been waiting for. Under the original Act 60 rules, the window to apply for a decree at the 0% rate closed on December 31, 2025. Many investors who were still weighing the move missed that deadline.

Act 38-2026 reopened it. Investors who submit their Individual Investor decree application on or before December 31, 2026 are grandfathered into the 0% rate for the remaining life of their decree (Grant Thornton PR). The application date — not the move date, not the approval date — is what counts.

What this means in practice

You can file your application today, in July, in October — any time through December 31, 2026 — and lock in the 0% rate. You don't need to have physically moved yet. You don't need approval in hand. The filing date is your stake in the ground. After that date, all new applications face 4%.

One procedural note

Act 38-2026 is pending final endorsement from Puerto Rico's Fiscal Oversight and Management Board (FOMB). This is a standard procedural step for Puerto Rico legislation and is not expected to alter the law's operative provisions. Still, confirm current status with your advisor before filing (Procopio).

Change 2: Program Extended Through December 31, 2055

Under the original Act 60, the entire Individual Investor program was set to expire on December 31, 2035. For investors with long-term planning horizons — or anyone whose decree might run past that date — this was a material uncertainty.

Act 38-2026 extends the program's availability through December 31, 2055 — an additional 20 years (Grant Thornton PR). This provides long-term certainty for investors, especially those considering applying under the new 4% regime after 2026 who want to know the program will still exist for decades.

What about existing decrees?

Existing decrees under Act 60 or Act 22 remain valid through December 31, 2035, as originally granted. They are not automatically extended to 2055. However, existing decree holders may voluntarily renegotiate their decrees to adopt the new terms — including the 2055 sunset — at their election (Grant Thornton PR; Act 38-2026).

Change 3: New 4% Tax Rate for Applications After December 31, 2026

Investors who apply on or after January 1, 2027 will face a 4% Puerto Rico income tax on post-residency capital gains, interest, and dividends — replacing the 0% rate that has been the program's signature benefit since its origins as Act 22 in 2012 (Grant Thornton PR; Act 38-2026).

To be clear: 4% is still extraordinarily low. Compare it to the combined federal + state rate a California resident faces on capital gains — which can approach or exceed 37%. Even at 4%, Puerto Rico's program remains one of the most favorable tax environments available to U.S. citizens. The 5% rate on pre-move gains recognized after 10 years of residency is also unchanged.

Change 4: New 6-Year Prior Non-Residency Requirement (Post-2026 Applicants)

Under the original Act 60 statute, the prior residency restriction was narrow: applicants simply could not have been Puerto Rico residents between January 17, 2006 and January 17, 2012. For the vast majority of mainland investors, this was easy to satisfy.

Act 38-2026 replaces that restriction with a rolling 6-year lookback. Investors applying on or after January 1, 2027 must certify they were not a resident of Puerto Rico at any point during the 6 years preceding their application (Grant Thornton PR; Act 38-2026). This is designed to prevent prior PR residents from cycling out and back in to refresh their decree benefits.

Does this affect you?

If you are a mainland U.S. resident applying for the first time, this almost certainly does not affect you. The new rule targets people who previously held PR residency and left. If you held an Act 60 or Act 22 decree before, moved away, and now want to reapply, you may be subject to this restriction depending on timing. Consult a qualified advisor.

Change 5: Primary Residence Can No Longer Be Held Through an LLC

This is the most underreported change in Act 38-2026, and it has real tax planning consequences. Under prior law, decree holders could purchase their primary residence through any ownership structure — including a limited liability company (LLC). Some investors used this flexibility for estate planning, liability protection, or other structuring reasons.

For applications filed on or after January 1, 2027, Act 38-2026 restricts how the primary residence can be held. The property must be owned directly by the individual or through a trust. Ownership through an LLC or other legal entity no longer satisfies the requirement (Grant Thornton PR; Act 38-2026).

Why this matters for tax planning

Many investors have historically purchased PR real estate through an LLC for asset protection and privacy. Under Act 38-2026, post-2026 applicants who do this will not satisfy the primary residence requirement — putting their entire decree at risk. Trusts remain permitted, so estate planning structures using a trust can still work. Review your ownership structure with your advisor before purchasing.

Applies only to post-2026 applicants

Existing decree holders who applied by December 31, 2026 are not affected by this change for their current decree term. This restriction applies only to applications filed on or after January 1, 2027 (Grant Thornton PR).

What Did NOT Change

Amid all the changes, it's worth being equally clear about what Act 38-2026 left untouched. The following requirements and benefits remain exactly as they were:

Bona fide residency test

183-day presence test, tax home test, and closer connection test remain unchanged (26 CFR §1.937-1)

$10,000 annual charitable donation

Still required — $5,000 to a qualifying child poverty nonprofit, $5,000 to any qualifying PR nonprofit (Act 60-2019, Section 2062.01)

Real estate purchase within 2 years

Must purchase a primary residence within 2 years of decree approval — unchanged for all applicants

Annual compliance report

Due November 15 each year. Failure to file can result in decree suspension

5% toll tax on pre-move gains

Bitcoin and other assets held before establishing PR residency: gains recognized after 10 years of residency are taxed at 5% in Puerto Rico (Act 60, Section 2021.01(e))

Existing decree holders fully grandfathered

All current Act 60 and Act 22 decrees remain valid at existing rates through December 31, 2035 unless revoked

Export services 4% corporate rate

Act 60 Chapter 3 (formerly Act 20) export services benefits are entirely unaffected by Act 38-2026

IRC Section 933 federal exclusion

Bona fide PR residents still exclude PR-sourced income from U.S. federal tax. Nothing in Act 38-2026 changes federal law

What You Should Do Right Now — By Situation

✅ I already have an Act 60 or Act 22 decree

Nothing changes for you right now. Your decree is fully grandfathered at your existing rates through December 31, 2035. You may voluntarily elect to renegotiate to the new Act 38-2026 terms — including the 2055 sunset — but this is optional. If your decree expires in 2035 and you want to continue, you would then be subject to the new rules. Talk to your advisor about whether renegotiation makes sense for your situation.

🔵 I missed the December 31, 2025 deadline — can I still get 0%?

Yes. Act 38-2026 extended the 0% grandfathering deadline to December 31, 2026. If you haven't applied yet, you still have the rest of 2026 to file and lock in the 0% rate. The application submission date is what counts — not when you move, not when DDEC approves it. File as soon as your situation allows.

🟠 I'm planning to apply in 2027 or later

You will be subject to the 4% rate on post-residency capital gains, interest, and dividends. The program still runs through 2055, so you have time. But the 0% benefit is gone for you. You'll also face the 6-year prior non-residency requirement and the no-LLC primary residence rule. Puerto Rico is still an exceptional tax jurisdiction at 4% — but plan accordingly and get the LLC vs. trust ownership question resolved before you purchase property.

🟣 I have a pending application filed before December 31, 2026

You are grandfathered at 0% — your application date controls. However, Act 38-2026 gives you an option: you may request that DDEC evaluate your pending application under the new post-2027 rules instead, if the new terms are more favorable for your situation (e.g., the 2055 sunset may be valuable for your long-term planning). This is a one-way election — evaluate carefully before requesting it (Grant Thornton PR).
