Act 60 vs Act 20 vs Act 22: What Changed and What It Means for You Ley 60 vs Ley 20 vs Ley 22: Qué Cambió y Qué Significa para Usted

Share this article: Compartir este articulo:

If you've spent any time researching Puerto Rico's tax incentive programs, you've almost certainly encountered three numbers thrown around interchangeably: Act 20, Act 22, and Act 60. The confusion is understandable. These laws are deeply interrelated — and yet they are not the same thing. Getting them mixed up can lead to costly misunderstandings about what you qualify for, what requirements apply, and how the rules have evolved since 2012.

This guide cuts through the noise. We'll explain what Act 20 and Act 22 originally provided, how Act 60 consolidated them in 2019, what changed along the way, and how the 2026 HB 505 reform reshapes the landscape for new applicants. Whether you're a business owner eyeing Puerto Rico's 4% corporate tax rate or an individual investor considering relocating for capital gains benefits, this article will give you the historical context and current-law clarity you need.

Key Takeaway

Act 60 did not eliminate Act 20 or Act 22 — it consolidated them (along with approximately 76 other incentive laws) into a single unified code. The core tax benefits survived, but eligibility requirements, compliance obligations, and, as of 2026, the individual investor tax rate have all changed (Act 60-2019; HB 505).

The Origin Story: Act 20 and Act 22 (2012)

On January 17, 2012, the Puerto Rico government enacted two landmark pieces of legislation designed to attract foreign capital and talent to the island during a prolonged economic downturn (Act No. 20-2012; Act No. 22-2012). Together, these laws represented Puerto Rico's boldest effort to leverage its unique tax status as a U.S. territory that sets its own income tax policies, separate from federal and state tax codes.

Act 20: The Export Services Act

Act 20, officially known as the "Act to Promote the Export of Services," targeted service-based businesses willing to operate from Puerto Rico while serving clients located outside the island. The law's stated purpose was to develop Puerto Rico into an international export services center and diversify the island's economic drivers (Act No. 20-2012, Sec. 1). Eligible businesses — spanning consulting, software development, financial advisory, marketing, call centers, and dozens of other service categories — could apply for a tax exemption decree from the Department of Economic Development and Commerce (DDEC).

Original Act 20 Benefits

4% corporate income tax rate on net income from export services (compared to up to 37.5% under standard Puerto Rico corporate rates and 21% U.S. federal corporate rate)

100% tax exemption on dividend distributions from exempt income

90% property tax exemption on real and personal property used in the exempt operation

60% municipal license tax exemption

Under the original Act 20, businesses were required to employ at least three full-time Puerto Rico-resident employees within six months of commencing operations, increasing to five employees within two years (Act No. 20-2012, Sec. 3). Tax exemption decrees were valid for 20 years — a generous timeline that gave businesses long-term planning certainty.

Act 22: The Individual Investors Act

Act 22, officially the "Act to Promote the Relocation of Individual Investors to Puerto Rico," was the companion law aimed at high-net-worth individuals. Its premise was simple but powerful: if you became a bona fide resident of Puerto Rico, your Puerto Rico-sourced passive investment income would be exempt from Puerto Rico income taxes (Act No. 22-2012, Sec. 3).

Original Act 22 Benefits

0% tax on capital gains realized after establishing bona fide Puerto Rico residency

0% tax on interest income earned after establishing residency

0% tax on dividend income earned after establishing residency

Tax exemption period through December 31, 2035

To qualify, an individual had to demonstrate that they had not been a resident of Puerto Rico for at least 15 years prior to the law's enactment date of January 17, 2012 — a requirement that was later amended to 6 years in 2017 (Act No. 22-2012, as amended). Because Puerto Rico-sourced income is excluded from U.S. federal taxation under IRC Section 933 for bona fide Puerto Rico residents, the combination of zero Puerto Rico tax and zero federal tax on qualifying income made this program exceptionally attractive to investors, particularly those holding appreciated assets like Bitcoin and other digital currencies.

Why Puerto Rico Needed a Consolidation: The Path to Act 60

By 2019, Puerto Rico's incentive landscape had become a sprawling maze. The Department of Economic Development and Commerce (DDEC) conducted an analysis and found that approximately 76 separate laws or programs were promoting investment through the granting of incentives across various government agencies. Of those, 58 stimulated economic activity and 18 addressed social needs. The total fiscal cost of these economic incentive programs exceeded $7.46 billion, of which 81% was classified as opportunity cost — meaning revenue the government was foregoing (Act 60-2019, Preamble).

The problem was clear: too many overlapping laws, managed by too many different agencies, with inconsistent terms, filing requirements, and compliance standards. Businesses and individuals navigating the system often needed multiple legal teams just to determine which incentives they could combine and what each required. The government itself struggled to track the effectiveness of each program or measure return on investment.

The Core Problem Act 60 Solved

Puerto Rico had roughly 76 overlapping incentive laws costing over $7.46 billion in foregone revenue, managed by different agencies with no unified framework. Act 60 consolidated them into a single code to create a streamlined, measurable system with standardized application processes and compliance requirements (Act 60-2019, Preamble).

Enter Act 60: The Puerto Rico Incentives Code (2019)

On July 1, 2019, the Governor of Puerto Rico signed Act 60-2019 into law — officially titled the "Puerto Rico Incentives Code" (Código de Incentivos de Puerto Rico). All new applications submitted from January 1, 2020 onward would be processed under this unified framework. The Incentives Code is organized into six Subtitles, with the tax incentives codified under Subtitle B across 12 separate chapters (Act 60-2019; PwC Tax Summaries, "Puerto Rico — Corporate — Tax Credits and Incentives").

Critically, Act 60 did not eliminate Act 20 or Act 22. It absorbed them. Act 20's export services incentives became Chapter 3 of the Incentives Code. Act 22's individual investor incentives became Chapter 2. Other incentive programs — for manufacturing (formerly Act 73), tourism (formerly Act 74), young entrepreneurs, creative industries, and more — were also folded into their respective chapters. Existing decree holders under the old laws retained their grandfathered benefits for the duration of their decrees.

Chapter 2: Individual Resident Investors (Formerly Act 22)

Under Act 60 Chapter 2 (Section 2021.01), the core benefits of the old Act 22 were preserved for decree holders approved before 2026: 0% tax on Puerto Rico-sourced capital gains, interest, and dividends accrued after establishing residency. However, Act 60 introduced several new requirements that the original Act 22 did not have.

What Changed for Individual Investors (Act 22 → Act 60 Chapter 2)

Real property requirement: Decree holders must now purchase real property in Puerto Rico within two years of receiving their decree, to be used as their primary residence throughout the decree term (Act 60-2019, Sec. 2021.01).

Charitable donation: An annual $10,000 donation to a certified Puerto Rico nonprofit is now mandatory. At least half must go to organizations working to eradicate child poverty (Act 60-2019, Sec. 2021.01(b)(4)).

Prior residency exclusion period extended: The exclusion period was expanded to 10 years before the Act's effective date (applicants must not have been PR residents since January 1, 2010).

Decree term shortened: From a term ending December 31, 2035 (under Act 22) to a 15-year initial term with a 15-year extension option under Act 60.

Annual DDEC reporting: Decree holders must file an annual report with the DDEC, with fines up to $1,000 per violation for non-compliance (Act 60-2019, Chapter 12).

Crypto explicitly covered: Act 60 explicitly extends capital gains treatment to cryptocurrencies and digital assets based on blockchain technology, which was not specifically addressed in the original Act 22.

Chapter 3: Export Services & Commerce (Formerly Act 20)

Act 60 Chapter 3 maintained the signature 4% corporate income tax rate for qualifying export service businesses, along with the 100% exemption on dividend distributions from exempt income. However, the compliance and eligibility requirements were refined.

What Changed for Export Services Businesses (Act 20 → Act 60 Chapter 3)

Employment requirement revised: The original Act 20 required 3 employees within 6 months, scaling to 5 within 2 years. Act 60 now requires at least 1 full-time PR-resident employee for businesses with revenue exceeding $3 million/year. Businesses at or below $3 million have no minimum employee count (Act 60-2019, Ch. 3).

SMB rate added: Businesses with revenue of $3 million or less receive a reduced 2% tax rate for the first 5 years, then 4% thereafter. A 1% rate is available for "Novel Pioneer Activities" (Act 60-2019, Ch. 3).

Property tax exemption adjusted: Businesses with revenue over $3 million receive a 75% property tax exemption. Smaller businesses get 100% exemption for 5 years, then 75% (Act 60-2019, Ch. 3).

Decree term shortened: From 20 years under Act 20 to 15 years with a 15-year extension option under Act 60.

Blockchain services added: The list of eligible activities now explicitly includes blockchain-related services, which were not listed in the original Act 20.

CPA-audited financials required: Export service decree holders must now submit CPA-audited financial statements as part of annual compliance, along with employment verification for companies above $3M revenue.

The 2026 Game-Changer: HB 505 and the End of 0% for New Applicants

The most significant change to Puerto Rico's individual investor program since its 2012 inception came through House Bill 505 (HB 505), which was approved by Puerto Rico's Senate in mid-2025 and extended the program through 2055 while imposing a new 4% tax rate on passive income for new applicants. Starting January 1, 2026, any individual who applies for an Individual Resident Investor decree will pay a flat 4% tax on capital gains, interest, and dividends — replacing the 0% rate that made Act 22 and Act 60 Chapter 2 famous (HB 505; San Juan Daily Star, "Senate Passes Extension to Individual Investors Act," June 2025).

HB 505 Key Provisions

New 4% tax rate: Applies to capital gains, PR-sourced interest, and PR-sourced dividends for all individual investor decree applications filed on or after January 1, 2026.

Grandfathering clause: Individuals who submitted their decree applications before December 31, 2025 are grandfathered at the 0% rate for the full term of their decrees (up to 15 years with a 15-year extension).

Program extended to 2055: The previous 2035 sunset date is pushed out by 20 years, providing long-term planning certainty for investors and the government alike.

6-year non-residency requirement: New applicants must certify that they were not residents of Puerto Rico for at least 6 years prior to applying.

Transition option: Current decree holders may transition into the new 4% model once their existing decrees expire after 2035.

The reform also introduced a parallel benefit for local Puerto Rico residents: any individual, estate, or trust on the island can voluntarily elect the same 4% fixed rate on locally sourced interest, dividends, and certain long-term capital gains. This provision was designed to create tax parity between mainland relocatees and long-time island residents, addressing years of criticism that Act 22/Act 60 decree holders enjoyed benefits unavailable to native Puerto Ricans (PRelocate, "Puerto Rico Governor Jenniffer González-Colón Proposes Changes to Act 60," April 2025).

Common Misconceptions About Act 20, Act 22, and Act 60

"Act 20 and Act 22 were merged into one program."

This is one of the most common errors. Act 20 became Chapter 3 of the Incentives Code and Act 22 became Chapter 2 — they remain separate incentive programs with distinct eligibility criteria, separate decree applications, and different requirements. A business cannot use a Chapter 2 individual investor decree to claim the 4% corporate rate, and an individual investor cannot use a Chapter 3 export services decree to claim capital gains exemptions. They are separate chapters within a single administrative code.

"If I had an Act 20 or Act 22 decree, I need to re-apply under Act 60."

No. Existing decree holders under the pre-2020 laws retain their benefits for the full duration of their original decrees. The transition to Act 60 applies to new applications filed on or after January 1, 2020. If you received a decree under Act 20 or Act 22, it remains in effect under the terms it was originally granted (Act 60-2019, transitional provisions).

"Puerto Rico is tax-free under Act 60."

This is a dangerous oversimplification. Act 60 provides significant tax reductions on specific types of income — primarily export services income (Chapter 3) and passive investment income (Chapter 2). However, ordinary earned income like wages and salaries remains subject to Puerto Rico's standard progressive income tax rates of up to 33%. Property taxes, sales taxes (IVU), and other local levies also apply. And U.S.-sourced income remains subject to U.S. federal taxation even for Puerto Rico residents (IRC Section 933; IRS Publication 570).

"The 4% rate for new applicants after 2025 makes Act 60 not worth it."

While the move from 0% to 4% on passive income is meaningful, perspective matters. The top U.S. federal long-term capital gains rate is 20% (plus the 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax), and many states add their own capital gains taxes. Even at 4%, Puerto Rico's individual investor program remains one of the most competitive in the world for investors with significant capital gains exposure — and the extension through 2055 provides three decades of certainty.

What This Means for You in 2026

Understanding the evolution from Act 20 and Act 22 to Act 60 is more than academic trivia — it directly impacts how you structure your move, your business, and your compliance strategy. Here is what matters depending on your situation.

Individual Investors

If you filed your decree application before December 31, 2025, you are locked in at 0% on passive income for the life of your decree. Your immediate priorities are maintaining strict bona fide residency compliance (183-day presence test, tax home test, closer connection test), meeting the $10,000 annual charitable donation, purchasing qualifying PR real property within two years, and filing your annual DDEC report on time.

If you apply in 2026 or later, the 4% rate applies, but the program is now extended through 2055 — meaning you have decades of planning horizon. The 6-year prior non-residency requirement also applies.

Export Services Businesses

The Chapter 3 export services incentives were not directly impacted by HB 505. The 4% corporate tax rate, 100% dividend exemption, and other business incentives remain unchanged. Your key compliance obligations include maintaining a bona fide office in Puerto Rico, ensuring services are performed from PR for clients outside PR, filing CPA-audited financials, and meeting employment minimums if your revenue exceeds $3 million.

Businesses at or below $3 million in revenue benefit from the reduced 2% rate for their first five years and have no minimum employee requirement.

Bitcoin and Digital Asset Holders

For Bitcoin investors, the distinction between pre-move and post-move appreciation is critical. Under Act 60 Chapter 2, gains on Bitcoin acquired after establishing bona fide residency are eligible for the 0% rate (pre-2026 decrees) or 4% rate (post-2025 decrees). However, appreciation that accrued before your move remains subject to either the 5% toll tax election under Section 2021.01(e) or standard U.S. federal capital gains treatment (IRS Notice 2014-21; Act 60-2019, Sec. 2021.01(e)).

Establishing your cost basis as of the date you become a bona fide resident is essential documentation that must be maintained for IRS audit defense.

IRS Scrutiny: Why the History Matters Now More Than Ever

The IRS has launched a dedicated enforcement campaign specifically targeting Act 60 (and legacy Act 20/22) decree holders. The IRS Large Business & International Division has identified common compliance failures including incorrect income source allocation, failure to meet the bona fide residency test, and erroneous treatment of pre-move capital gains as Puerto Rico-sourced income (IRS, "Introduction to Puerto Rico Acts 20 and 22," LB&I Publication).

Understanding the legislative history is directly relevant to IRS audit defense. If you originally obtained a decree under Act 20 or Act 22 and have since transitioned into the Act 60 framework, you need to know exactly which terms, requirements, and rates apply to your specific decree — because the rules have shifted at multiple points. A decree granted under Act 22 in 2013 carries different compliance terms than one granted under Act 60 Chapter 2 in 2023, even though both provide capital gains benefits. Auditors know this distinction and will test whether you do too.

Audit-Proofing Your Decree

Maintain a comprehensive residency file updated annually with travel logs, boarding passes, utility bills, proof of property ownership, banking records, and community involvement documentation. Know whether your decree falls under the original Act 20/22 terms or the Act 60 framework. Ensure your income sourcing is defensible — especially the distinction between PR-sourced and US-sourced income (IRC Sections 861-865; Treasury Reg. 1.937-2). Work with a qualified Puerto Rico CPA and tax attorney who understands all three legislative frameworks.

The Bottom Line

The evolution from Act 20 and Act 22 to Act 60 — and now to the post-HB 505 regime — reflects Puerto Rico's ongoing effort to balance economic development incentives with fiscal sustainability and public equity. The core value proposition remains strong: a 4% corporate tax rate for qualifying export businesses and deeply reduced rates on investment income for individual residents, all within a U.S. territory that requires no passport and no expatriation.

But the compliance requirements have tightened meaningfully at every legislative step. What started in 2012 as a relatively simple tax exemption decree process has become a multi-layered compliance regime requiring real property purchases, annual charitable donations, CPA-audited financials, enhanced DDEC reporting, and meticulous residency documentation to withstand IRS scrutiny.

The investors and business owners who succeed under Act 60 are those who treat it not as a tax loophole but as a structured incentive program with real requirements that must be met every single year. Understanding the history — from Act 20 to Act 22 to Act 60 to HB 505 — gives you the context to navigate the present and plan for the future with confidence.
