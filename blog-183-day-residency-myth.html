The 183-Day Residency Myth: What the IRS Actually Tests for Act 60 Holders El Mito de los 183 Días: Lo Que el IRS Realmente Evalúa para Titulares de la Ley 60

Share this article: Compartir este articulo:

You just spent 184 days in Puerto Rico this year. You counted every single one. Your Act 60 decree sits framed on your wall. You assumed you were safe.

Then the IRS auditor asks: "Where does your spouse live?" And suddenly, your $400,000 in tax savings is at risk.

The problem? You believed the 183-day myth. The truth is more complex—and far more consequential.

Critical Fact:

Under IRC Section 937(a) and Treasury Regulation §1.937-1, bona fide residency requires passing THREE separate tests. The 183-day presence requirement is only one of them—and often the easiest to satisfy.

The Three-Test Framework: What IRC Section 937 Actually Requires

IRC Section 937(a), enacted by the American Jobs Creation Act of 2004, establishes that an individual qualifies as a bona fide resident of Puerto Rico only if they meet all three of the following tests during the taxable year:

1

Presence Test

Present in Puerto Rico for at least 183 days during the tax year (or meet alternative presence criteria)

2

Tax Home Test

Do NOT have a tax home outside Puerto Rico during any part of the taxable year

3

Closer Connection Test

Do NOT have a closer connection to the United States or any foreign country than to Puerto Rico

All-or-Nothing Standard:

If you fail any one of these three tests, you are NOT a bona fide resident of Puerto Rico for that entire tax year. The IRS does not prorate residency. This is an "all-or-nothing" determination—and it's based on facts and circumstances, not just counting days on a calendar.

Test #1: The Presence Test (The Easy One)

According to IRS Publication 570 and Treasury Regulation §1.937-1(c), you satisfy the presence test by meeting at least one of the following five conditions:

A

Simple 183-Day Rule: You are present in Puerto Rico for at least 183 days during the taxable year.

B

Three-Year Rolling Test: You are present in Puerto Rico for at least 549 days during the three-year period consisting of the current tax year and the two immediately preceding years, with at least 60 days of presence in each of those three years.

C

90-Day U.S. Limit: You are present in the United States for no more than 90 days during the taxable year.

D

Minimal U.S. Earnings Test: You have U.S. earned income of less than $3,000, you are present in Puerto Rico for more days than in the United States, and you have no significant connection to the United States.

E

No Significant Connection: You have no significant connection to the United States during the taxable year (including no permanent home, voter registration, or minor children residing in the U.S.).

Example 1: Tech Entrepreneur Who Passes the Presence Test

Scenario: Marcus, a software entrepreneur, moves to Puerto Rico on March 15, 2025. He spends 201 days in Puerto Rico from March 15 through December 31, 2025. He spends 85 days in the United States during 2025.

Result: Marcus satisfies the presence test under condition (A) because he was present in Puerto Rico for more than 183 days during the taxable year. He also satisfies condition (C) because he was present in the United States for fewer than 90 days.

Bottom line: Most Act 60 decree holders easily satisfy the presence test. It's the next two tests where things get complicated.

Test #2: The Tax Home Test (Where Most People Stumble)

Under IRC Section 937(a)(2) and Treasury Regulation §1.937-1(d), you must not have a tax home outside Puerto Rico during any part of the taxable year.

What is a "Tax Home"?

According to Treasury Regulation §1.937-1(d)(1), your tax home is located at your regular or principal place of business. If you have no regular place of business, your tax home is where you regularly live. This is determined under the principles of IRC Section 911(d)(3).

Example 2: The Remote Worker Who Fails the Tax Home Test

Scenario: Elena is a remote software consultant. She holds an Act 60 decree and spends 210 days in Puerto Rico during 2025. However, 90% of her client meetings take place via Zoom with her primary client headquartered in New York. She bills $380,000 annually, and her client contract designates her "principal place of business" as New York for liability purposes.

IRS Position: Even though Elena spends 210 days in Puerto Rico, the IRS could argue that her tax home is in New York because that's where her principal business activity is economically centered. Her client relationships, revenue source, and contractual arrangements all point to New York as her regular place of business.

Result: Elena fails the tax home test. She is NOT a bona fide resident of Puerto Rico for 2025, even though she satisfied the 183-day presence test. All of her income remains subject to U.S. federal income tax.

How to Pass the Tax Home Test

What Works:

  • • Establish a physical office in Puerto Rico
  • • Conduct client meetings from Puerto Rico
  • • Use a Puerto Rico business address on all contracts
  • • Maintain business bank accounts in Puerto Rico
  • • Hold board meetings and strategic planning sessions in Puerto Rico
  • • Have employees or contractors based in Puerto Rico

Red Flags:

  • • Maintaining an office in the mainland U.S.
  • • Primary clients or business partners located in the U.S.
  • • Business mail sent to a U.S. address
  • • Board meetings held primarily in the U.S.
  • • Business operations managed from the U.S.
  • • U.S.-based employees performing core functions

Test #3: The Closer Connection Test (The IRS's Secret Weapon)

Under IRC Section 937(a)(2) and Treasury Regulation §1.937-1(e), you must not have a closer connection to the United States or a foreign country than to Puerto Rico.

This is a facts-and-circumstances test. IRS Publication 570 lists nine factors the IRS considers when evaluating closer connection, but the list is not exhaustive:

1

Location of your permanent home

2

Location of your family (spouse, minor children, and other close family members)

3

Location of personal belongings (automobiles, furniture, clothing, jewelry, art, collectibles)

4

Location of social, political, cultural, or religious organizations with which you have a current relationship

5

Location where you conduct routine personal banking activities

6

Location where you conduct business activities (other than your tax home)

7

Jurisdiction that issued your driver's license

8

Jurisdiction in which you vote

9

Country of residence you designate on official forms and documents

Example 3: The Family Factor (A $210,000 Mistake)

Scenario: Daniel holds an Act 60 decree. He spends 195 days in Puerto Rico during 2025. His wife and two minor children (ages 8 and 11) remain in their family home in Miami because the children are enrolled in private school there. Daniel flies to Miami every other weekend to see his family. He maintains health insurance, a country club membership, and a mortgage in Florida.

Tax at Stake: Daniel realizes $600,000 in capital gains from selling Bitcoin he acquired after moving to Puerto Rico. Under Act 60, that gain should be 100% tax-free. Under U.S. federal tax, he owes approximately $119,000 in capital gains tax (20% rate) plus $11,400 in Net Investment Income Tax (3.8%), for a total federal liability of $130,400. When Florida has no state income tax but he would owe an additional estimated $80,000 to the IRS if residency is denied, his total exposure is $210,400.

IRS Position: Even though Daniel spent 195 days in Puerto Rico, the IRS argues he has a closer connection to Florida because: (1) his spouse and minor children live in Florida full-time; (2) his permanent home with all his family belongings is in Florida; (3) his social connections (country club, children's school community) are in Florida; and (4) his routine personal activities (weekend family time) occur in Florida.

Result: Daniel fails the closer connection test. The IRS assesses $210,400 in federal tax, plus penalties and interest. His Act 60 decree provides no protection because he was never a bona fide resident of Puerto Rico under IRC Section 937.

How to Pass the Closer Connection Test

Action Checklist:

  • ✓ Move your spouse and minor children to Puerto Rico (or formalize a legal separation)
  • ✓ Purchase or rent a home in Puerto Rico and make it your principal residence
  • ✓ Move valuable personal property (art, collectibles, heirlooms) to Puerto Rico
  • ✓ Obtain a Puerto Rico driver's license and vehicle registration
  • ✓ Register to vote in Puerto Rico
  • ✓ Open bank accounts in Puerto Rico and conduct routine banking there
  • ✓ Join social, civic, or religious organizations in Puerto Rico
  • ✓ Use your Puerto Rico address on all official forms (passport, tax forms, legal documents)
  • ✓ Establish healthcare providers in Puerto Rico
  • ✓ Sever or minimize ties to the U.S. (sell or rent out U.S. home, close U.S. country club memberships, etc.)

The Form 8898 Filing Requirement (Often Overlooked)

Under IRC Section 937(c), if you begin or end bona fide residence in Puerto Rico during any tax year and your worldwide gross income exceeds $75,000, you must file Form 8898, "Statement for Individuals Who Begin or End Bona Fide Residence in a U.S. Possession."

Form 8898 must be filed by the due date (including extensions) of your Form 1040 for the year in which you begin or end bona fide residence.

Penalty for Failure to File Form 8898:

According to the Form 8898 instructions, if you fail to file Form 8898 or include incorrect information, you may be subject to a penalty of $1,000, unless you can show reasonable cause and not willful neglect. This penalty is in addition to any criminal penalties that may apply.

Pro tip: Filing Form 8898 creates an official record of your residency claim with the IRS. Include detailed attachments documenting your compliance with all three tests. This form is your first line of defense in an audit.

What Happens in a Real IRS Residency Audit?

The IRS has launched a campaign specifically targeting Act 60 decree holders. These "Campaign Audits" focus almost exclusively on the bona fide residency determination.

What the IRS Requests in a Campaign Audit:

  • • Complete travel records (passport stamps, airline receipts, hotel records) for the audit year and often 2-3 prior years
  • • Cell phone location data and credit card transaction records showing geographic spending patterns
  • • Lease agreements or property deeds for all homes (Puerto Rico and U.S.)
  • • Proof of where spouse and children reside (school enrollment records, medical records)
  • • Business contracts showing location of principal place of business
  • • Bank statements showing where routine banking occurs
  • • Driver's license, voter registration, and vehicle registration documents
  • • Membership records for clubs, gyms, religious organizations
  • • Utility bills for all properties
  • • Evidence of social media posts showing location over time

The Burden of Proof is on YOU:

In a bona fide residency audit, the taxpayer bears the burden of proving they meet all three tests. The IRS does not need to prove you failed. You must affirmatively demonstrate—with contemporaneous documentation—that you satisfied the presence test, tax home test, and closer connection test for the entire taxable year.

Practical Recommendations for Act 60 Decree Holders

1. Exceed the 183-Day Minimum

Don't aim for 183 days—aim for 220+ days in Puerto Rico. Build a cushion. Travel disruptions, medical emergencies, and family obligations can eat into your day count quickly.

2. Establish a Real Business Presence in Puerto Rico

If you're claiming Act 60 export services benefits, establish a physical office in Puerto Rico with local employees. Conduct board meetings, client calls, and strategic planning from Puerto Rico. Document everything.

3. Move Your Family (Or Formalize the Separation)

If your spouse and children remain in the mainland U.S., you are inviting an IRS challenge on closer connection. Either move the entire family to Puerto Rico or formalize a legal separation to demonstrate independent domiciles.

4. Create a Contemporaneous Record

Keep a daily journal with photos showing your presence in Puerto Rico. Save receipts, airline tickets, and credit card statements. The IRS will not take your word for it—you need documentary proof.

5. File Form 8898 on Time with Detailed Attachments

Don't just check boxes on Form 8898. Attach a detailed narrative explaining how you satisfy each of the three tests, with supporting documentation.

6. Get a Pre-Move Forensic Audit

Before you claim bona fide residency, hire a qualified Puerto Rico CPA to conduct a forensic review of your residency facts. Identify weaknesses before the IRS does. It's far cheaper to fix issues proactively than to fight an audit.

Conclusion: Don't Let the 183-Day Myth Cost You Everything

The 183-day presence requirement is real, but it's only one-third of the equation. Under IRC Section 937(a), you must also pass the tax home test and the closer connection test. These are not checkbox exercises—they are intensive, facts-and-circumstances analyses that require advance planning, documentation, and often professional guidance.

The IRS is actively auditing Act 60 decree holders. The stakes are enormous: if you fail the bona fide residency test, you lose 100% of your Act 60 tax benefits for the entire year. There is no proration, no partial credit, and no second chances.

The Bottom Line:

Counting to 183 is easy. Proving bona fide residency is not. If you're serious about Act 60, treat residency compliance like the six-figure (or seven-figure) tax decision that it is.
