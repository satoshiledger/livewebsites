The 5% Toll Tax on Pre-Move Bitcoin: How to Calculate Your Cost Basis Before Relocating El Impuesto de Peaje del 5% Sobre Bitcoin Pre-Mudanza: Cómo Calcular Su Base de Costo

Share this article:Compartir este articulo:

You've accumulated significant Bitcoin gains while living on the mainland. Now you're planning to relocate to Puerto Rico under Act 60 to benefit from the 0% (pre-2026 decree) or 4% (post-2025 decree) tax rate on future capital gains. But what happens to the appreciation that already occurred before your move?

This is the single most consequential — and most misunderstood — tax question for Bitcoin holders relocating to Puerto Rico. The answer involves Act 60's toll tax provision (Section 2021.01(e)), the federal 10-year lookback rule under Treasury Regulation 1.937-2, the split holding period mechanics for marketable vs. non-marketable property, and the critical documentation of your move-date fair market value for every Bitcoin position you hold.

The Core Rule

Act 60 does not exempt pre-move gains. Only appreciation that accrues after you establish bona fide Puerto Rico residency qualifies for the Act 60 rate. Gains attributable to the period before your move remain subject to U.S. federal capital gains tax — unless you elect the Puerto Rico toll tax at 5%. Getting this wrong is the #1 audit trigger for Bitcoin holders under Act 60 (Holland & Knight, Nov. 2025; Golding & Golding).

The Problem: Pre-Move Appreciation Doesn't Disappear

Bitcoin is treated as property for U.S. tax purposes under IRS Notice 2014-21. When you sell Bitcoin, you recognize a capital gain equal to the difference between the sale price and your cost basis (what you originally paid). Under IRC Section 865, capital gains on personal property are generally sourced to the taxpayer's residence at the time of sale.

Here's where it gets complicated for PR relocators: Treasury Regulation 1.937-2(f) provides a special 10-year lookback rule. For individuals who move to Puerto Rico and sell property they owned before establishing PR residency, the gain is not automatically treated as Puerto Rico-sourced — even if the sale happens while the taxpayer is a bona fide PR resident. This lookback period applies for up to 10 years after the move (Treas. Reg. § 1.937-2(f)(1); Holland & Knight, Nov. 2025).

In practical terms: if you bought Bitcoin in 2020 for $10,000 and it's worth $500,000 on the day you establish PR residency in 2026, that $490,000 of pre-move appreciation does not become Puerto Rico-sourced income simply because you moved. You must bifurcate the gain — pre-move vs. post-move — and deal with each portion under different rules.

Your Three Options for Pre-Move Bitcoin Gains

When you hold Bitcoin acquired before relocating to Puerto Rico, you have three basic approaches to the pre-move appreciation. The optimal choice depends on the size of your unrealized gains, your time horizon, and your risk tolerance.

Option 1: Sell Before You Move (Pay U.S. Federal Capital Gains)

Sell your Bitcoin while you're still a U.S. mainland resident. Pay federal long-term capital gains tax (0%, 15%, or 20% depending on income) plus the 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT) if applicable, plus any state capital gains tax. Then repurchase Bitcoin after establishing PR residency — creating a fresh, post-move cost basis that's fully eligible for Act 60 treatment.

Pros: Clean and simple. No bifurcation complexity. No 10-year lookback issues. Your entire post-repurchase position is post-move.

Cons: You pay up to 23.8% federal (plus state) on the entire gain immediately. Wash sale rules do not apply to Bitcoin (IRS CCA 202124008 confirmed crypto-to-crypto swaps are not eligible for like-kind exchange, but wash sale rules under IRC Section 1091 apply only to securities — not to cryptocurrency held directly). However, there may be economic substance arguments if you sell and immediately repurchase.

Option 2: Hold and Bifurcate (Split the Gain Pre/Post Move)

Keep your Bitcoin through the move. When you eventually sell, split the gain into two portions: pre-move appreciation (U.S.-sourced, taxed at federal capital gains rates) and post-move appreciation (Puerto Rico-sourced, eligible for Act 60 rate of 0% or 4%). This split is governed by the 10-year lookback rule under Treasury Regulation 1.937-2(f).

How the split works for Bitcoin: There is an open question about whether cryptocurrency qualifies as a "marketable security" under Treas. Reg. § 1.937-2(f)(1)(vii)(A). Pillsbury Law's analysis noted that Mark Leeds of Sullivan & Cromwell believes there is a path to concluding that crypto is not among the types of property subject to the split holding period rules, though the IRS position remains unclear (Pillsbury, 2025). If Bitcoin is treated as a marketable security, the split is based on the closing fair market value on the first day of your possession holding period (i.e., your move date). If it's treated as non-marketable property, the split is calculated using a days-of-ownership method.

Cons: Complex record-keeping. Ongoing uncertainty about Bitcoin's classification under Treas. Reg. § 1.937-2. The pre-move portion remains taxed at federal rates whenever you sell (even decades later, unless sold after 10 years of PR residency).

Option 3: Elect the 5% Toll Tax (Act 60 Section 2021.01(e))

Under Act 60 Section 2021.01(e), a decree holder can elect to pay a flat 5% Puerto Rico tax on the unrealized appreciation that existed as of the date they became a bona fide PR resident. This 5% toll tax is paid in lieu of any other Puerto Rico income tax on the pre-move portion, and is not subject to the alternate basic tax. The election effectively resets your cost basis for Puerto Rico tax purposes to the move-date fair market value (Act 60-2019, Section 2021.01(e); Golding & Golding).

Critical distinction: The 5% toll tax is a Puerto Rico tax. It does not replace or eliminate the U.S. federal tax on pre-move gains. However, it interacts with the 10-year lookback rule under Treas. Reg. § 1.937-2. Under that regulation, an individual can elect to treat the portion of gain attributable to their possession holding period as possession-source income by reporting it on their PR tax return. After making this election and paying the 5% toll tax, the post-move portion of the gain is treated as PR-sourced and eligible for the 0%/4% Act 60 rate.

Pros: 5% is dramatically lower than the up to 23.8% federal rate. It cleanly separates pre-move and post-move gain for future dispositions. It simplifies compliance going forward.

Worked Example: The 5% Toll Tax on Bitcoin

Scenario

Alex purchased 10 BTC in January 2021 at $30,000 each (total cost basis: $300,000).

Alex establishes bona fide PR residency on July 1, 2026.

On July 1, 2026, BTC is trading at $120,000 (total FMV: $1,200,000).

Pre-move unrealized gain: $1,200,000 − $300,000 = $900,000.

Alex holds an Act 60 Chapter 2 decree obtained before January 1, 2026 (grandfathered at 0%).

If Alex Elects the 5% Toll Tax:

Puerto Rico toll tax: $900,000 × 5% = $45,000 (paid to Puerto Rico).

Alex's effective cost basis for future PR tax purposes resets to $1,200,000 (the move-date FMV).

If Alex later sells all 10 BTC at $200,000 each ($2,000,000 total), the post-move gain is $2,000,000 − $1,200,000 = $800,000.

Puerto Rico tax on post-move gain: $800,000 × 0% = $0 (grandfathered decree).

Total PR tax paid: $45,000. Total gain realized: $1,700,000. Effective rate on total gain: 2.6%.

If Alex Had Sold on the Mainland (No Relocation):

Federal long-term capital gains: $1,700,000 × 20% = $340,000.

NIIT: $1,700,000 × 3.8% = $64,600.

State tax (e.g., California at 13.3%): $1,700,000 × 13.3% = $226,100.

Total mainland tax: approximately $630,700. Effective rate: 37.1%.

Toll Tax Savings in This Example: approximately $585,700

Even accounting for the $45,000 toll tax payment, Alex saves over half a million dollars compared to selling on the mainland — and that's before any additional post-move appreciation.

The Federal Side: How the Toll Tax Interacts with U.S. Tax

The 5% toll tax is a Puerto Rico tax — it does not eliminate or replace U.S. federal tax liability on pre-move gains. Here is how the interaction works:

Under IRC Section 933, a bona fide resident of Puerto Rico excludes Puerto Rico-sourced income from U.S. federal gross income. The toll tax election under Treas. Reg. § 1.937-2(f)(1)(vi) allows the taxpayer to treat the post-move portion of gain as Puerto Rico-sourced by reporting it on the PR return. But the pre-move portion remains U.S.-sourced. When the Bitcoin is eventually sold, the pre-move appreciation (the gain from original cost basis to move-date FMV) is reportable on the U.S. federal return as U.S.-source capital gain.

In practical terms, this means the pre-move gain may be subject to both the 5% PR toll tax and U.S. federal capital gains tax. Whether the 5% toll tax generates a foreign tax credit against the federal liability depends on the specific facts and professional analysis. This is an area where the guidance is not entirely settled, and the interaction between Puerto Rico's territorial tax system and the federal system creates complexity that requires professional counsel (Creative Planning, Jan. 2026).

After 10 Years: The Slate Gets Wiped

Here's the planning opportunity: under the 10-year lookback rule, gains on property sold after 10 years of bona fide PR residency are no longer subject to the split holding period rules. After a full decade of genuine PR residency, the entire gain — including pre-move appreciation — can be treated as Puerto Rico-sourced. For Bitcoin holders with a long time horizon, this means holding pre-move Bitcoin for 10+ years post-residency can convert the entire gain to PR-sourced income eligible for Act 60 treatment (Cordasco & Company, Oct. 2025; Treas. Reg. § 1.937-2(f)(1)).

The Open Question: Is Bitcoin a "Marketable Security"?

The split holding period rules under Treas. Reg. § 1.937-2(f)(1)(vi) distinguish between marketable securities and other property. For marketable securities, the pre-move vs. post-move gain split is calculated using the fair market value on the first day of the possession holding period (your move date). For non-marketable property, the split uses a days-of-ownership allocation method that can produce different results.

Bitcoin was not contemplated when these regulations were written. The question of whether Bitcoin qualifies as a "marketable security" under the regulation's definition has not been definitively answered by the IRS. Pillsbury Law reported that Mark Leeds of Sullivan & Cromwell identified a viable argument that cryptocurrency is not among the types of property subject to the split holding period rules, though this position carries risk (Pillsbury, 2025). Holland & Knight noted that cryptocurrency is treated as property under IRS Revenue Ruling 2019-24 and sourced under IRC Section 865, but the specific interaction with the 10-year lookback rule's property classification remains an area of interpretive ambiguity (Holland & Knight, Nov. 2025).

For planning purposes, most conservative practitioners treat Bitcoin as subject to the split holding period rules and apply the marketable security methodology (using the move-date FMV) given that Bitcoin trades on established exchanges with readily available pricing. This approach is more favorable to the taxpayer because it allows a clean split at the move-date price rather than a days-of-ownership proration.

Documenting Your Move-Date Fair Market Value: The Most Important Step

The entire toll tax framework hinges on establishing, with verifiable precision, the fair market value of every Bitcoin position you hold on the date you establish bona fide Puerto Rico residency. If you cannot document this value, you cannot bifurcate gains, you cannot elect the toll tax, and the IRS will presume the entire gain is U.S.-sourced (TokenTax, Jun. 2025; Holland & Knight, Nov. 2025).

✓ Create a Snapshot of Every Position

On the date your PR residency becomes effective (the date your tax home changes to Puerto Rico), document the exact quantity of Bitcoin in every wallet and exchange account. Record the FMV as of that date using a reputable pricing source (CoinGecko, CoinMarketCap, or your exchange's recorded prices). Screenshot the balances and prices. Export CSV files from every exchange account showing holdings as of that date.

✓ Tag Every Lot as Pre-Move or Post-Move

Every Bitcoin lot (individual purchase or receipt) must be classified. Lots acquired before your move date are "pre-move" and subject to bifurcation. Lots acquired after your move date are "post-move" and fully eligible for Act 60 treatment. Use specific identification or FIFO methodology consistently and maintain records that allow you to trace each lot to its acquisition date and original cost basis.

✓ Preserve On-Chain Evidence

For Bitcoin held in self-custody wallets, record the wallet addresses and transaction IDs (TXIDs) for all acquisitions. Blockchain data is immutable and can be used to corroborate acquisition dates and amounts. Under the enhanced 2026 DDEC compliance portal, decree holders may be required to declare wallet addresses and transaction histories.

✓ Establish the Residency Date with Precision

Your "possession holding period" begins on the first day your tax home is in Puerto Rico. This is not necessarily the date you arrive or the date you sign a lease — it's the date you satisfy the bona fide residency tests under IRC Section 937. File IRS Form 8898 in the year of your move. Keep your lease agreement, property deed, utility activation records, PR driver's license application, and bank account opening documentation to corroborate the exact date.

Five Fatal Mistakes Bitcoin Holders Make with Pre-Move Gains

1. Treating ALL Gains as Post-Move

This is the mistake that gets people prosecuted. The Gajwani case involved exactly this kind of aggressive position — treating pre-residency appreciation as Puerto Rico-sourced. The IRS has explicitly rejected this approach through CCM 202538025 and the Gajwani prosecution (DOJ, Jun. 2025; Holland & Knight, Nov. 2025).

2. Failing to Document Move-Date FMV

Without a verifiable record of the fair market value of your Bitcoin on the date you establish PR residency, you have no basis for bifurcation. The IRS can and will challenge your gain allocation if you cannot produce contemporaneous documentation.

3. Relying on Aggressive Opinion Letters

Multiple practitioners issued opinion letters advising that pre-move crypto gains could be entirely sourced to Puerto Rico. These opinions have been publicly described by IRS senior representatives as incorrect. Baker McKenzie was subpoenaed for records related to such opinion letters in connection with the Gajwani case. Reliance on counsel is not a defense to criminal prosecution (Tax Notes, Nov. 2025).

4. Not Electing the Toll Tax When It's Clearly Beneficial

For holders with large pre-move gains, paying 5% to Puerto Rico is dramatically cheaper than paying up to 23.8% (federal + NIIT) when the Bitcoin is eventually sold. Failing to make the election proactively means losing the opportunity to cleanly reset your PR cost basis.

5. Mixing Pre-Move and Post-Move Lots

Commingling pre-move Bitcoin with post-move purchases in the same wallet or exchange account without clear lot tracking creates an accounting nightmare. If you can't prove which coins are post-move, the IRS may treat the entire position as pre-move. Maintain separate wallets or use exchange sub-accounts to segregate pre-move and post-move holdings.

Planning Decision Framework

Situation Recommended Approach
Small pre-move gains (under $50K) Consider selling before the move and repurchasing post-residency. The tax hit is manageable and creates a clean post-move cost basis.
Large pre-move gains, short time horizon Elect the 5% toll tax. Pay 5% on pre-move appreciation, reset your PR cost basis, and sell post-move with all future gains at 0%/4%.
Large pre-move gains, 10+ year time horizon Consider holding through the 10-year lookback period. After 10 years of bona fide PR residency, the entire gain becomes PR-sourced. No toll tax needed if you can wait.
Mixed portfolio (some pre-move, some post-move) Elect toll tax on pre-move lots. Keep post-move lots segregated. Document everything at the lot level.
Bitcoin ETFs (IBIT, FBTC) held pre-move ETF shares are securities subject to both the 10-year lookback rule and wash sale rules (unlike direct crypto). The toll tax election mechanics are the same but ETF classification is more clearly "marketable security."

The Bottom Line

The 5% toll tax is one of the most powerful planning tools available to Bitcoin holders relocating to Puerto Rico. For someone sitting on $1 million in pre-move appreciation, the difference between paying 5% ($50,000) and paying 23.8%+ on the mainland ($238,000+) is substantial. Combined with 0% (or 4%) on all post-move appreciation, the total tax savings over a lifetime of Bitcoin investing can easily reach seven figures.

But the toll tax is not automatic. It requires an affirmative election, precise documentation of move-date fair market value, disciplined lot-level tracking of every Bitcoin position, and professional guidance to navigate the interaction between Puerto Rico's toll tax provision and the federal 10-year lookback rule.

In the current enforcement environment — where the IRS has specifically identified pre-move crypto gain sourcing as a top audit priority — the cost of getting the toll tax analysis wrong is not just a larger tax bill. It's the risk of criminal prosecution, as the Gajwani case demonstrated. Invest in documentation. Invest in qualified counsel. The toll tax is a powerful tool, but only for those who use it correctly.
