How Much Does It Cost to Start a Business in Puerto Rico? 2026 Breakdown ¿Cuánto Cuesta Iniciar un Negocio en Puerto Rico? Desglose 2026

Share this article:Compartir este articulo:

Whether you're relocating to Puerto Rico under Act 60, launching an export services company to capture the 4% corporate tax rate, or simply starting a local business on the island, one of the first practical questions is: how much will this actually cost?

The answer depends on your business structure, whether you're pursuing an Act 60 decree, and how much professional support you engage. A bare-bones LLC formation can be accomplished for under $500 in government fees. A fully Act 60-optimized export services corporation with professional tax counsel, CPA engagement, and decree application support can run $15,000 to $25,000 in the first year — still a fraction of the tax savings for most decree holders.

This guide breaks down every cost category — government filing fees, professional services, licensing, and ongoing compliance — so you can build an accurate budget before your first day of business in Puerto Rico.

Government Filing Fees: The Non-Negotiables

Every Puerto Rico business must file formation documents with the PR Department of State (Departamento de Estado). The fees vary by entity type but are straightforward and relatively affordable compared to most U.S. states.

Filing Fee Notes
LLC Certificate of Formation $250 Filed with PR Department of State (14 LPRA § 3952)
Corporation Articles of Incorporation $150 Filed with PR Department of State (Act 164-2009)
Foreign entity authorization $150 For existing mainland LLC/Corp registering in PR
Name reservation (optional) $75 Reserves name for 120 days
Trade name / DBA registration $150 If operating under a name different from the legal entity name
EIN (Federal Employer Identification Number) $0 Free from the IRS; apply online at irs.gov
SURI merchant registration $0 Free via Hacienda's SURI portal; required 30 days before operations (TaxJar; CPA Puerto Rico)
Patente Municipal application $0 No application fee, but ongoing municipal tax of up to 0.5% of gross receipts (Northwest Registered Agent; CPA Puerto Rico)

Bottom Line: Government Fees Only

For a standard Puerto Rico LLC, the total government filing cost is approximately $250 (Certificate of Formation) plus any optional filings. Corporations pay $150 for Articles of Incorporation. These are among the lowest formation costs in any U.S. jurisdiction.

Registered Agent Costs

Every Puerto Rico LLC and corporation must maintain a registered agent with a physical address in Puerto Rico who can accept service of process and legal documents on behalf of the business. You can serve as your own registered agent if you have a Puerto Rico address, or you can hire a commercial registered agent service (NW Registered Agent; IncNow; InCorp).

Registered Agent Fee Ranges (2026)

Self-service (you or a business partner): $0 — but requires a physical PR address and availability during business hours.

Commercial registered agent services: $99 to $259 per year, depending on the provider and service level (InCorp, IncNow).

Law firm or CPA firm as registered agent: $200 to $500 per year, often bundled with other compliance services.

For Act 60 decree holders who need to demonstrate a bona fide office in Puerto Rico, the registered agent address alone is not sufficient. You'll need a dedicated office, coworking membership, or home office with the proper municipal permitting — an additional cost we'll cover below.

Licensing and Permits: The Three Essentials

To legally operate a business in Puerto Rico, you need three primary permits beyond your entity formation (Piloto 151, "Operating a Business in Puerto Rico"; Swyft Filings; Northwest Registered Agent).

1. Registro de Comerciante (Merchant's Registration Certificate)

Cost: Free. Where: SURI portal (suri.hacienda.pr.gov).

This certificate grants you permission to operate a business in Puerto Rico. It must be obtained at least 30 days before commencing operations. Failure to register on time can result in a fine of up to $10,000 (CPA Puerto Rico, "Sales and Use Tax"). You'll need your Certificate of Incorporation/Formation, EIN documentation, and taxpayer identification.

2. Permiso Único (Single Use Permit)

Cost: Varies by municipality and business type; typically $100-$500.

This consolidated permit combines the Use Permit, Fire Prevention Certification, Sanitary License, Environmental Compliance certificate, and applicable special licenses into a single application. You apply through the Office of Permits Management (OGPE) at ogpe.pr.gov. If you operate from a coworking space, you may be able to request a Carta de Consulta to operate under the coworking facility's existing Permiso Único (Piloto 151).

3. Patente Municipal (Municipal License Tax)

Application cost: Free. Ongoing tax: Up to 0.5% of gross receipts for non-financial businesses; up to 1.5% for financial businesses.

Every municipality in Puerto Rico requires this. You apply at the finance office (Oficina de Finanzas) of the municipality where your business is located. The Patente Provisional must be obtained 30 days before commencing operations and renews every six months. The annual declaration must be filed by the fifth day after April 15 each year (CPA Puerto Rico, "Municipal License Tax"; Northwest Registered Agent). Under Act 60 Chapter 3, export services decree holders receive a 50% exemption on municipal license taxes.

Act 60 Decree Application Costs

If you're incorporating in Puerto Rico specifically to take advantage of Act 60 tax incentives, the decree application is an additional — and significant — cost layer. The process involves application fees paid to DDEC, plus professional fees for legal and tax counsel to prepare your application and supporting documentation (Tax Law Ventures, "How to Apply for Act 60," Jul. 2025).

Cost Item Chapter 2 (Individual Investor) Chapter 3 (Export Services)
DDEC application fee $750 $750
Decree acceptance fee $5,000 $5,000
Annual decree compliance fee $5,000/year $5,000/year
Annual charitable donation (Ch. 2) $10,000/year (pre-2026 decree); $15,000/year (post-2025 decree) N/A
Professional fees (attorney + CPA) $5,000 – $15,000 $7,500 – $20,000
Processing time 6 – 12 months 6 – 12 months

Important Note on Professional Fees

The professional fee range is wide because the complexity of your situation matters enormously. A straightforward individual investor application with clean residency documentation and simple capital gains income may be at the lower end. A crypto-heavy individual with pre-move positions, or an export services business with complex sourcing analysis, will require more extensive professional work. Given the current IRS enforcement environment (see our article on the IRS crackdown), underinvesting in professional guidance is a false economy.

Professional Services: What You'll Pay for CPA and Legal Support

Puerto Rico's tax system is distinct from any U.S. state. LLCs in Puerto Rico default to corporate tax treatment (unlike the mainland, where they default to pass-through/disregarded entity status), which means entity election is a critical decision that requires professional guidance. You'll also need a PR-licensed CPA for ongoing bookkeeping, tax preparation, and — if you're an Act 60 Chapter 3 decree holder — CPA-audited financial statements (14 LPRA § 3952; Act 60, Chapter 3; PR Dept. of State Corporation Registry).

PR-Licensed CPA (Ongoing)

Monthly bookkeeping: $300 – $1,000/month depending on transaction volume. Annual tax preparation (Form 482 + DDEC reports): $1,500 – $5,000. CPA-audited financial statements (required for Ch. 3 if revenue exceeds $3M): $5,000 – $15,000+. Entity election analysis and implementation: $1,000 – $3,000 (one-time).

U.S. Tax Attorney or CPA (Federal Filing)

Annual U.S. federal return (Form 1040 with PR income exclusion): $1,000 – $5,000. Income sourcing analysis and documentation: $2,000 – $10,000 (one-time or as needed). IRS audit defense (if needed): $10,000+ depending on scope.

PR Attorney (Entity Formation + Compliance)

LLC operating agreement drafting: $500 – $2,000. Act 60 decree application preparation and submission: $3,000 – $10,000 (included in decree professional fees above). General counsel retainer: $1,000 – $3,000/month for ongoing advisory.

Starting a business is one cost. Keeping it compliant is another. Puerto Rico has several annual requirements that create recurring expenses.

For corporations with revenue exceeding $3 million, the annual report filed with the Department of State must include CPA-audited financial statements — a significant additional cost. LLCs are not required to file an annual report, only to pay the $150 annual fee (PR Dept. of State Corporation Registry).

Costs People Forget: The Hidden Line Items

Beyond the obvious formation and decree costs, several expenses catch new business owners off guard.

PR LLC Default Tax Treatment

Unlike the mainland, Puerto Rico LLCs default to corporate tax treatment (C-Corp). If you want pass-through or disregarded entity status, you must make an affirmative election. Getting this wrong can result in double taxation. Entity election analysis with a PR CPA is essential — and is a cost many people don't anticipate (14 LPRA § 3952; NW Registered Agent).

Sales and Use Tax (IVU) Compliance

If your business sells taxable goods or services, you must collect and remit Puerto Rico's Sales and Use Tax (IVU) through monthly returns filed via SURI. The combined state and municipal IVU rate is 11.5% (10.5% state + 1% municipal). Monthly filing compliance requires either your time or CPA time (CPA Puerto Rico, "Sales and Use Tax").

Workers' Compensation and Disability Insurance

If you have employees in Puerto Rico (required for Act 60 Ch. 3 if revenue exceeds $3M), you must obtain workers' compensation coverage through the State Insurance Fund (Corporación del Fondo del Seguro del Estado) and temporary disability insurance. These are employer costs that are separate from payroll taxes.

Personal Property Tax on Business Assets

Puerto Rico imposes a personal property tax on business assets — equipment, furniture, fixtures, and inventory used by the business. The average rate is approximately 10% of the net book value of such assets. Act 60 Ch. 3 decree holders receive a 75% exemption, but the remaining 25% is still due.

The Bottom Line: Is It Worth the Cost?

For a simple local LLC without Act 60 incentives, Puerto Rico is one of the most affordable places in the U.S. to incorporate — under $500 in government fees and a few thousand dollars in professional support to get started.

For Act 60 decree holders, the costs are higher — potentially $30,000 to $70,000 in the first year when you include decree fees, professional services, and ongoing compliance. But context matters. A Chapter 3 export services business paying a 4% corporate rate instead of 21% federal (plus state taxes) on $500,000 of net income saves roughly $85,000 per year in taxes. A Chapter 2 individual investor with $1 million in annual capital gains saves $200,000+ per year at the 0% rate (or $168,000+ at the 4% post-2025 rate). The formation and compliance costs pay for themselves within the first few months.

The key is budgeting accurately, engaging qualified professionals from the start, and treating compliance as an investment rather than an expense. In the current enforcement environment, the cost of getting it wrong — through IRS audits, decree revocation, or back taxes — vastly exceeds the cost of getting it right.
