Share this article: Compartir este articulo:

If you're considering forming a business in Puerto Rico — whether to take advantage of Act 60 tax incentives, expand your operations, or relocate entirely — you need accurate cost projections. One wrong number could blow your budget before you even open your doors.

This comprehensive guide breaks down the exact costs to incorporate an LLC or corporation in Puerto Rico as of 2026, using official fee schedules from the Puerto Rico Department of State, Department of Treasury (Hacienda), and licensed service providers. Every figure cited below is sourced from government publications or verified market rates.

Bottom line: Expect to budget between $400 and $1,200 for year one (formation only), and $300 to $800 annually for ongoing compliance, depending on whether you hire professionals or DIY.

Official Department of State Filing Fees

The Puerto Rico Department of State (Departamento de Estado) sets all official filing fees. These fees are mandatory and the same whether you file yourself or hire a service.

LLC Certificate of Formation: $250

To form a Limited Liability Company in Puerto Rico, you must file a Certificate of Formation with the Department of State and pay a $250 filing fee. The statutory authority for Puerto Rico LLCs is found in Title 14, Chapter 239 of the Puerto Rico General Corporations Act (14 L.P.R.A. §3951 et seq.), with filing fees governed by 14 L.P.R.A. §3901 and the certificate of formation procedure set out in §3962. This fee is non-refundable and applies to both domestic and foreign LLCs registering in Puerto Rico.

Expedited Filing Options Available

Need your LLC faster? Puerto Rico offers expedited processing: 24-hour service for an additional $100, or 2-hour service for an additional $500 (in addition to the base $250 fee). Verify current expedited fee options directly with the Puerto Rico Department of State before filing.

Corporation Certificate of Incorporation: $150

To form a corporation (Inc., Corp.) in Puerto Rico, you file a Certificate of Incorporation and pay a $150 filing fee to the Department of State. This fee applies to both standard corporations and professional corporations under Act 164-2009 (General Corporations Act), as amended. Filing fees for corporations are also governed by 14 L.P.R.A. §3901.

Additional Name Reservation & DBA Fees

  • Name Reservation (optional): $75 for LLCs, $10 for corporations for 120 days of exclusive use
  • Trade Name / DBA Filing: $150 to register an assumed business name (valid 10 years; renewal $150). Note: After registration, Puerto Rico law requires publication of a notice in a local newspaper — cost varies by publication. Puerto Rico treats trade names similarly to trademarks, which is unusual versus other U.S. states. Source: Northwest Registered Agent — Puerto Rico DBA Guide.
  • Foreign Entity Authorization: $150 (mail) / $250 (online or in person) for out-of-state companies registering to do business in PR — fee varies by filing method. Source: Northwest Registered Agent — Foreign LLC Puerto Rico.

Registered Agent: Required by Law

Every Puerto Rico LLC and corporation must maintain a registered agent with a physical street address in Puerto Rico. The registered agent requirement for corporations is codified in 14 L.P.R.A. §3542 (Chapter 223 of the General Corporations Act); because the LLC Act (§3962) incorporates corporate filing procedures by reference, §3542 is also the provision cited by the Department of State and licensed service providers for LLC registered agent obligations. The registered agent receives official government notices and service of process (lawsuits) on behalf of your business.

Option 1: Act as Your Own Registered Agent — Free (But Risky)

You can serve as your own registered agent if you're a Puerto Rico resident with a physical address on the island. However, this means:

  • Your personal address becomes public record
  • You must be available at that address during business hours (Monday–Friday, 9am–5pm)
  • You could be served with a lawsuit in person at any time

Option 2: Hire a Commercial Registered Agent — $99 to $199/year

Annual Compliance: The Recurring Costs

Formation is just the beginning. To stay in good standing with the Puerto Rico Department of State, you must file annual reports or pay annual fees every year by April 15.

LLCs: $150 Annual Fee (No Report Required)

Under Chapter 239 of the General Corporations Act, all Puerto Rico LLCs must pay a $150 annual fee on or before April 15 each year. LLCs do not have to file a full annual report — just pay the fee through the electronic registry. Source: Puerto Rico Department of State — Corporation Registry.

Corporations: $150 Annual Report + Financial Statement

Corporations must file an Annual Report with the Department of State by April 15, which includes:

  • Names and addresses of officers and directors
  • Registered agent information
  • A balance sheet showing the financial condition of the business
  • The $150 annual filing fee

Audited Financials Requirement — Department of State Annual Report

For the Department of State annual report: if your corporation's annual revenue exceeds $3 million, the balance sheet must be audited by a Puerto Rico-licensed CPA. If revenue is under $3 million, a balance sheet prepared by someone with general accounting knowledge is acceptable. Source: Puerto Rico Department of State — Corporation Registry.

Note: Separate CPA audit requirements may apply for Puerto Rico income tax returns filed with Hacienda, which have their own thresholds. Consult a licensed CPA to confirm obligations for both filings.

Late Filing Penalties

Miss the April 15 deadline? The Department of State imposes escalating penalties. The actual amount assessed depends on circumstances and time elapsed, but the authorized range is:

  • For-profit corporations and LLCs: Penalties range from $500 to $2,000, increasing on a monthly basis starting April 16. Source: Northwest Registered Agent — Puerto Rico Annual Report Guide.
  • LLCs specifically: In addition to the base penalty, LLCs are charged $500 plus 1.5% monthly interest on the unpaid annual fee for each month delinquent. Source: RelocatePuertoRico.com — LLC Compliance; RelocatePuertoRico.com — Act 60 Corporate Filing Requirements.
  • All entities: Missing multiple consecutive filings can result in administrative dissolution by the Secretary of State.

Tax Registration: Free But Required

Federal Employer Identification Number (EIN): Free

Every Puerto Rico business needs a federal EIN from the IRS. You can apply online at IRS.gov for free. Processing is instant if you apply online. No fees, no exceptions.

SURI Registration (Puerto Rico Treasury): Free

All businesses operating in Puerto Rico must register with the Department of Treasury through the SURI (Sistema Unificado de Rentas Internas) portal at suri.hacienda.pr.gov. Registration is free, though certain business activities may trigger additional licensing or tax obligations.

Merchant's Registry Certificate: Free (But Has Compliance Deadlines)

If you're selling goods or services in Puerto Rico, you must obtain a Merchant's Registry Certificate (Certificado de Registro de Comerciante) through SURI. Key compliance requirements:

  • Registration is free
  • Must be requested within 30 days of commencing business activity — failure to register on time can trigger penalties
  • The certificate must be renewed every 2 years (also free)
  • The certificate must be displayed at your place of business

Sales Tax Registration (IVU)

Businesses with physical presence must register for IVU. Remote sellers must register if they have sales over $100,000 OR 200+ transactions annually in Puerto Rico (thresholds established effective 2021 — verify with SURI or a licensed CPA for any legislative updates). Registration is free through SURI, but you'll collect and remit 11.5% sales tax (10.5% commonwealth + 1% municipal).

Professional Service Costs (Optional but Recommended)

While you can file everything yourself, most businesses hire professionals to avoid costly mistakes. Here's what to expect:

Formation Services: $300 to $800

Online formation companies and local attorneys typically charge:

  • Budget online services: $300–$500 (includes state filing fees, registered agent for 1 year, basic operating agreement template)
  • Mid-tier services: $500–$800 (adds EIN filing, customized operating agreement, compliance alerts)
  • Attorney-assisted formation: $800–$1,500 (includes legal review, customized documents, Act 60 pre-qualification assessment)

Bookkeeping & Tax Preparation: $200 to $500/month

Once you're operational, ongoing accounting services typically cost:

  • Basic bookkeeping (no employees): $200–$350/month
  • Full-service bookkeeping + payroll: $400–$600/month
  • Annual tax return preparation: $800–$2,000 (depends on complexity, Act 60 status)

Act 60 Decree Application: $3,000 to $10,000+

If you're forming a business to qualify for Act 60 tax incentives, professional guidance is essential. Application fees to the Office of Industrial Tax Exemption (OITE) plus legal/CPA services typically run $3,000 to $10,000 depending on the complexity of your business model.

Bitcoin & Cryptocurrency Holders — Additional Planning Required

If you hold Bitcoin or other digital assets and are considering Act 60, the business formation itself is only part of the picture. Act 60 Chapter 2 (Individual Investors) can provide 0% Puerto Rico tax on post-residency capital gains for decree holders who applied by December 31, 2026, while pre-move appreciation remains subject to U.S. federal tax. Establishing your cost basis as of your move date, structuring your entity correctly, and complying with both IRS and DDEC requirements requires specialized Bitcoin accounting. Budget for this expertise separately from standard formation fees.

Hidden Costs to Watch For

Beyond the obvious formation and annual fees, budget for these often-overlooked expenses:

Municipal License

If you have a physical location in Puerto Rico, you need a municipal business license (Patente Municipal). Fees vary by municipality and business type — some municipalities and industries have higher rates. Budget approximately $50 to $300+ annually as a starting estimate and verify the exact amount with your specific municipality before filing.

Business Insurance

Business insurance costs vary widely based on your industry, revenue, and coverage limits. Puerto Rico insurers do not publish standard rate schedules publicly. Contact local providers such as MAPFRE Puerto Rico, AIG Puerto Rico, or Popular Insurance for a quote specific to your business type and risk profile.

Payroll Compliance

Hiring employees? Register with PR Department of Labor, obtain unemployment insurance, workers' comp, and disability coverage. Budget $200–$400/month for payroll processing.

Bank Account Fees

Puerto Rico's major business banks — Banco Popular, FirstBank, and Oriental Bank — offer business checking accounts with varying monthly fees and minimum balance requirements. Contact each bank directly for current fee schedules, as these change regularly.
