Act 60 Annual Compliance: What You Need to File Each Year to Keep Your Decree Cumplimiento Anual de Ley 60: Qué Necesita Presentar Cada Año para Mantener Su Decreto

Share this article:Compartir este articulo:

Your Act 60 decree is not a one-time benefit. It's a binding contract with the Government of Puerto Rico — and like any contract, it comes with ongoing obligations. Fail to meet those obligations, and you don't just risk a fine. You risk losing the decree entirely, which means retroactive exposure to standard Puerto Rico tax rates of up to 33% on individual income or 37.5% on corporate income.

In 2025, the DDEC audited nearly 1,800 decrees and formed a new Compliance Audit Committee with authority to impose fines up to $10,000 and recommend decree revocations. Starting in 2026, the agency is rolling out automatic notifications and penalties for late filings, beginning with a $1,000 penalty that can escalate to revocation for persistent noncompliance (News Is My Business, Oct. 2025). This article covers every annual filing obligation — for both Chapter 2 (Individual Investor) and Chapter 3 (Export Services) decree holders — so you can stay compliant and protect your tax benefits.

The Key Date to Remember

The DDEC Exempt Annual Report (EAR) for Act 60 decree holders is due by November 15 each year (or the 15th day of the 11th month following the close of your tax year). All filings go through the DDEC Portal of Incentives at incentives.ddec.pr.gov (DDEC, 2025).

The Complete Annual Compliance Calendar

Act 60 decree holders face multiple overlapping filing obligations — to the DDEC, to Puerto Rico's Department of Treasury (Hacienda), and in many cases to the IRS. Here is the full calendar of deadlines for a calendar-year taxpayer:

The DDEC Exempt Annual Report (EAR): The Core Filing

The Exempt Annual Report is the single most important compliance document for Act 60 decree holders. It is filed through the DDEC's Portal of Incentives (incentives.ddec.pr.gov) and serves as your annual proof to the Puerto Rico government that you are meeting all decree conditions. The 2025 EAR (for tax year 2024) was due November 15, 2025, and the DDEC emphasized it would be strictly enforcing compliance with filing deadlines (DDEC, 2025).

Chapter 2 (Individual Investor) EAR Requirements

The Chapter 2 annual report requires the following documentation:

Residency verification: Proof of bona fide PR residency, including lease or property deed, utility bills, bank statements, Puerto Rico driver's license, voter registration, and any other documentation demonstrating the closer connection to PR.

Income reporting: Breakdown of income by source — PR-sourced (eligible for Act 60 treatment) vs. U.S.-sourced (subject to federal tax). For crypto holders, this includes wallet addresses, transaction histories, and pre-move vs. post-move categorization of holdings.

Charitable donation receipts: Documentation of the $10,000 annual donation ($15,000 for post-2025 decrees under HB 505), including receipts from CECFL-approved organizations and Section 1101.01-certified nonprofits.

Real property ownership: Proof that you own residential real property in Puerto Rico as your primary residence (required within 2 years of decree issuance).

Filing fee: $5,000, paid online through the Portal of Incentives at the time of filing (Act 60 Investor, 2025; McConnell Valdés, 2025).

Chapter 3 (Export Services) EAR Requirements

The Chapter 3 annual report requires additional business-specific documentation:

CPA-audited financial statements: Required for decree holders with revenue exceeding $3 million annually. This is a full audit conducted by a Puerto Rico-licensed CPA, not a review or compilation. Businesses below $3 million may submit reviewed or compiled financial statements, depending on their decree terms.

Employment verification: If annual revenue exceeds $3 million, proof of at least one full-time PR-resident employee. The employee can be the business owner, but must directly participate in the exempt business activities. Payroll records, tax withholding filings, and employment contracts are required (McConnell Valdés, 2025; Sabalier Law).

Income source documentation: Evidence that services were performed from Puerto Rico for clients outside Puerto Rico. This includes contracts, invoices, billing records, and travel logs demonstrating where services were physically performed.

Bona fide office verification: Proof that the business maintains a real office or establishment in Puerto Rico — not a virtual mailbox or coworking day pass.

Filing fee: $5,000, paid through the Portal of Incentives (DDEC, 2025).

The $10,000 Charitable Donation Requirement (Chapter 2)

One of the most distinctive compliance obligations for Act 60 Chapter 2 decree holders is the mandatory annual charitable donation. The requirement begins in the second year of the decree and must be completed by December 31 of each tax year (Puerto Rico Advantage; Relocate PR).

Pre-2026 Decree Holders: $10,000 Annual Donation

First $5,000: Must go to a nonprofit on the CECFL (Comisión Especial Conjunta de Fondos Legislativos para Impacto Comunitario) approved list. These organizations focus on alleviating child poverty in Puerto Rico. The list is published annually and available at cecflpr.org.

Second $5,000: Can go to any Puerto Rico nonprofit certified under Section 1101.01(a)(2)(A)(iii) of the Puerto Rico Internal Revenue Code — the PR equivalent of a 501(c)(3). DDEC has confirmed that the full $10,000 may be donated to a single CECFL-approved organization if desired (Puerto Rico Advantage).

Post-2025 Decree Holders (HB 505): $15,000 Annual Donation

Under HB 505 (passed in 2025), new decree holders who apply after January 1, 2026 are subject to a $15,000 annual charitable donation requirement. At least 50% ($7,500) must go to CECFL-designated organizations focused on child poverty. The remaining $7,500 may go to any Section 1101.01-certified nonprofit (HB 505, 2025).

Donation Rules to Know

The charity cannot be owned or controlled by the decree holder, their spouse or partner, descendants, or ascendants.

The organization must operate in Puerto Rico.

You may donate to multiple organizations as long as the total meets the minimum.

Retain official donation receipts — you must submit them with your EAR filing.

Complete donations by early November if possible to avoid year-end processing delays at the nonprofit level (WoodsLux, Oct. 2025).

Puerto Rico and Federal Tax Returns

Beyond the DDEC annual report, decree holders must file income tax returns with both Puerto Rico and the IRS. These filings are separate from the EAR but equally important for maintaining compliance.

Puerto Rico Income Tax Return

Individuals: File Form 482 (Planilla de Contribución sobre Ingresos) by April 15. Report worldwide income, then deduct exempt Act 60 gains and income in Schedule B3. Attach a copy of your decree.

Corporations (Ch. 3): File Form 480.20 by the 15th day of the 4th month after the close of the fiscal year. Corporations operating under Act 60 must clearly identify exempt and non-exempt income.

Extensions: Puerto Rico grants automatic extensions to October 15 for individuals and the 15th day of the 10th month for corporations, but the extension is to file — not to pay. Estimated taxes must still be paid by the original due date.

U.S. Federal Income Tax Return

Form 1040: You are still a U.S. citizen and must file a federal return. Report worldwide income, then exclude Puerto Rico-sourced income under IRC Section 933. Only U.S.-sourced and foreign-sourced income is taxable at federal rates.

Form 8898: File in the year you first establish (or end) bona fide PR residency. This alerts the IRS to your change of tax status.

Key distinction: Act 60 exempt income that qualifies as PR-sourced goes on your PR return (Form 482) and is excluded from your federal return. U.S.-sourced income (including pre-move gains and income from services performed on the mainland) must appear on both returns, with appropriate credits to avoid double taxation.

Residency Documentation: Your First Line of Defense

Residency compliance is the area where the IRS has focused the greatest enforcement attention. The GAO's December 2025 report identified 179 taxpayers who failed residency requirements in a single audit by Puerto Rico officials, and the IRS has stated that many resident investor decree holders may not be meeting the bona fide residency test (GAO-26-107225, Dec. 2025). Here is what you should be documenting continuously throughout the year:

183-Day Presence Test

Keep every boarding pass, flight itinerary, and travel receipt. The IRS has subpoenaed airline records, passport stamps, and credit card geolocation data in Act 60 audits. Build a spreadsheet tracking every day spent in Puerto Rico, every day spent on the mainland, and every day spent outside the U.S. Your CPA should verify you meet the 183-day threshold before year-end — ideally with a cushion of 20+ additional days to account for emergencies or disputes (Tax Law Ventures, Jul. 2025; GAO-26-107225).

Tax Home Test

Your principal place of business must be in Puerto Rico. This means your main office, your primary revenue-generating activities, and the location where you spend the majority of your working time must all be in PR. If you operate a Ch. 3 export services business, maintain a physical office in PR — not just a virtual address. Keep lease agreements, office photos, and utility bills current.

Closer Connection Test

You must have stronger personal and economic ties to Puerto Rico than to any mainland state or foreign country. Key documentation includes: PR driver's license, PR vehicle registration, PR voter registration, PR bank accounts (as primary accounts), PR medical and dental providers, PR social clubs or religious organizations, PR address on all financial and legal documents, and closure or transfer of mainland memberships and affiliations. The IRS examines the totality of circumstances — no single factor is dispositive, but maintaining mainland connections (such as keeping a mainland home, using mainland doctors, or voting in mainland elections) creates audit risk (IRC §937; Treas. Reg. §1.937-1).

New for 2026: Enhanced DDEC Compliance Requirements

The DDEC's Office of Incentives, under Director Ernesto J. Zayas-García, announced significant enhancements to the compliance framework in late 2025. These changes apply to EAR filings starting in 2026 and represent a meaningful increase in documentation requirements (News Is My Business, Oct. 2025):

Certified CPA Letters

The DDEC now requires a certified letter from a Puerto Rico-licensed CPA attesting to income source classifications. This is not a financial statement audit — it's a CPA certification specifically confirming the decree holder's income has been properly categorized as PR-sourced vs. U.S.-sourced vs. foreign-sourced. This requirement applies to both Chapter 2 and Chapter 3 decree holders.

Cryptocurrency Holdings Disclosure

Decree holders with cryptocurrency positions must now provide wallet addresses, transaction histories, and a categorization of holdings into pre-move vs. post-move lots. This is a significant new requirement targeting the specific enforcement concern raised by the GAO and IRS — that crypto holders are improperly treating pre-move appreciation as PR-sourced income.

Data Sharing with the IRS

The DDEC's Office of Incentives now coordinates directly with the IRS and Puerto Rico's Hacienda to share compliance data on an ongoing basis. This follows the GAO's first recommendation that the IRS establish procedures to regularly obtain Hacienda data on all resident investor recipients. In practical terms, this means information you file with the DDEC may be accessible to IRS auditors (GAO-26-107225, Recommendation 1; News Is My Business, Oct. 2025).

Automatic Penalties for Late Filing

Beginning in 2026, the DDEC is implementing automatic notifications and fines for late EAR filings. The initial penalty is $1,000 per violation, with escalation to fines up to $10,000 and potential decree revocation for persistent noncompliance. This is a departure from previous years where enforcement was less systematic (News Is My Business, Oct. 2025).

Chapter 3 Export Services: Additional Annual Obligations

Chapter 3 decree holders face several additional compliance requirements beyond the basic EAR filing. These are the obligations that frequently trip up business owners:

Reasonable Salary Documentation

If you are the owner of a Ch. 3 entity, you must pay yourself a reasonable salary that is subject to standard Puerto Rico income tax rates. The IRS and DDEC's Office of Industrial Tax Exemption (OITE) actively scrutinize whether owner salaries are artificially low — a strategy designed to maximize the amount of income taxed at the 4% corporate rate rather than the higher individual rate. Your CPA should document the methodology used to determine your salary, including comparable market data, the nature of services you perform, and the time you spend working in the business. Keep detailed records to defend your salary in an audit.

Employment Requirement ($3M+ Revenue)

Businesses with actual or projected annual revenue exceeding $3 million must employ at least one full-time PR-resident employee who directly participates in the exempt business activities. The employee can be the business owner. Maintain W-2s, payroll tax filings, employee contracts, and proof of PR residency for all employees counted toward this requirement (McConnell Valdés; Sabalier Law).

Service Location Tracking

Income is only eligible for the 4% rate if the services are performed from Puerto Rico for clients outside Puerto Rico. If you travel to a client's mainland office and perform services there, that income is U.S.-sourced and subject to federal tax. Keep travel logs, contracts specifying service delivery location, and billing records that correlate hours worked with your physical location.

CPA-Audited Financial Statements ($3M+ Revenue)

Businesses with revenue above $3 million must submit CPA-audited financial statements with their annual report. This is a full audit under Generally Accepted Auditing Standards, conducted by a CPA licensed in Puerto Rico. The cost typically ranges from $5,000 to $15,000 or more depending on the complexity of the business. Plan for this expense in your annual compliance budget and engage your CPA early in the fiscal year to allow adequate time for the audit (Rachel Farris CPA; McConnell Valdés).

These costs are significant — but they should be weighed against the tax savings your decree provides. A Chapter 2 decree holder with $500,000 in annual PR-sourced capital gains saves roughly $119,000 per year in federal tax (compared to the 23.8% mainland rate). A Chapter 3 decree holder with $1 million in eligible export income saves roughly $170,000 per year compared to the 21% federal corporate rate. Annual compliance costs of $20,000–$40,000 represent a small fraction of the savings — but only if you remain in compliance.

What Happens If You Don't Comply

DDEC Penalties

Starting 2026: automatic $1,000 fine for late EAR filings, escalating to fines up to $10,000 per violation. For persistent noncompliance, the DDEC Compliance Audit Committee can recommend decree revocation. In 2025, the DDEC audited nearly 1,800 decrees — the most extensive compliance effort in years (News Is My Business, Oct. 2025).

Decree Revocation

If your decree is revoked, all income that was previously treated as exempt reverts to standard Puerto Rico tax rates. This means you could face retroactive tax liability at PR's standard rate of up to 33% on individual income or up to 37.5% on corporate income — plus penalties and interest — for all years where you claimed the Act 60 rate improperly.

IRS Re-Assessment

If the IRS determines you did not meet bona fide residency requirements, all income you claimed as PR-sourced is reclassified as U.S.-sourced. You owe federal capital gains tax (up to 23.8%), NIIT, and potentially state tax — plus accuracy-related penalties of 20% and interest. In the most egregious cases, the IRS has pursued criminal prosecution, as demonstrated in the Gajwani case (DOJ, Jun. 2025).

The Annual Compliance Checklist

Use this checklist to ensure you're meeting every obligation throughout the year:

☐

January–March: Organize prior year records. Engage PR CPA for tax preparation. Verify 183-day count for prior year. Compile income source documentation.

☐

April 15: File PR Form 482 (or 480.20 for corporations). File U.S. Form 1040. Pay LLC annual fee ($150). File Patente Municipal declaration. Request extensions if needed — but pay estimated taxes by this date.

☐

May–September: Track travel days meticulously. Begin CPA audit engagement (Ch. 3 if revenue > $3M). Maintain updated income source records. Document service delivery locations for Ch. 3.

☐

October–November: Complete charitable donations (aim for early November at the latest). Gather all EAR documentation. Obtain CPA certification letter for income source classifications. Prepare crypto holdings disclosure if applicable.

☐

November 15: File the DDEC Exempt Annual Report (EAR) with $5,000 payment through incentives.ddec.pr.gov. Submit all supporting documentation.

☐

December 31: Final deadline for charitable donation completion. Verify final 183-day count. Ensure all residency documentation is current. Archive all records for at least 7 years.

☐

Ongoing: Keep a real-time travel log. Save every flight itinerary and boarding pass. Maintain PR bank account activity. Update PR driver's license and vehicle registration as needed. Document all client contracts with service delivery locations. Retain payroll records for employment verification.

The Bottom Line

Act 60 compliance is not optional, and in 2026 it is no longer loosely enforced. The DDEC's expanded audit efforts, the new Compliance Audit Committee, automatic late-filing penalties, enhanced crypto disclosure requirements, and direct data sharing with the IRS all signal a fundamentally different enforcement landscape than the one that existed when Acts 20 and 22 were first enacted in 2012.

The decree holders who thrive under this new regime are those who treat compliance as a year-round discipline — not an annual scramble. Engage a qualified PR CPA and a U.S. tax professional who understand both sides of the filing equation. Budget for compliance costs as a known expense. Document everything in real time. And remember: the cost of compliance ($20,000–$40,000 per year) is a fraction of the tax savings your decree provides — but only if you keep it.
