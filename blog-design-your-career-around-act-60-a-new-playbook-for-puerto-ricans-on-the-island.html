Design Your Career Around Act 60: A New Playbook for Puerto Ricans on the Island Diseña Tu Carrera Alrededor de la Ley 60: Un Nuevo Enfoque para los Puertorriqueños en la Isla

Share this article: Compartir este articulo:

For decades, the story has been the same: Puerto Rico produces talented people, and talented people leave. They go to New York, to Miami, to Orlando. They go because the island's economy doesn't have enough room for them — and because nobody showed them a different path.

But here's what most people on the island don't know: the law that mainlanders use to reduce their taxes to 4% was also written for you. Act 60's Export Services chapter — Chapter 3 — doesn't require you to be a millionaire relocating from California. It requires you to run a service business, operate from Puerto Rico, and serve clients outside the island. That's it.

The mindset shift is this: instead of asking "what jobs exist in Puerto Rico?", start asking "what skills can I build that the world will pay for — and that Act 60 will reward?"

This article is for Puerto Ricans who are already on the island, or who want to stay. It's about designing your career intentionally — building skills and businesses that plug directly into a legal framework that was designed, in part, to benefit the Puerto Rican economy and the people who live in it.

The Structural Problem Act 60 Was Designed to Solve

Puerto Rico has one of the most educated workforces in the Caribbean. The island awards roughly 50,000 undergraduate and graduate degrees per year. Yet for generations, the economy couldn't absorb them. Youth unemployment, while improving — reaching 12.5% in 2024 according to World Bank data — has historically averaged nearly 25% since 1991. The result has been an exodus that has cost Puerto Rico more than 600,000 people over the past two decades. Source: Federal Reserve Bank of New York — Migration in Puerto Rico: Is There a Brain Drain?

Act 60 Export Services was written, in part, as an economic development answer to exactly this problem. The law's stated purpose is to develop Puerto Rico as an international export service center — to create an economic reason for high-skill work to be performed here, rather than somewhere else.

What the Law Actually Says

Under Act 60-2019, Chapter 3, any entity with a bona fide office in Puerto Rico that provides eligible services to clients outside Puerto Rico pays a corporate income tax rate of 4% — and as low as 2% for the first five years if annual revenue is under $3 million. Dividends paid to shareholders are exempt from Puerto Rico income tax entirely. Source: Act 60-2019, Chapter 3 (Sections 2031.01 et seq.); McV Law — Act 60 Practice Guide.

To qualify, you don't need a decree from Silicon Valley. You need a real office in Puerto Rico, real services performed in Puerto Rico, and real clients outside the island. For a Puerto Rican professional building a client base in the United States, that description fits millions of potential careers.

The key insight that most people miss: the skills that qualify for Act 60 Export Services are exactly the skills the modern global economy is paying a premium for. Software development. Digital marketing. Financial consulting. Graphic design. Data analytics. AI and machine learning. These are not exotic specialties — they are learnable, buildable careers that a young professional in San Juan can pursue just as effectively as one in Austin or London.

The Career Design Framework: Think in Three Layers

Designing your career around Act 60 doesn't mean chasing the tax incentive — it means building something real that also happens to qualify. Here's how to think about it.

1

Layer 1: Choose a Skill That Travels

The foundation — what you're actually good at

The first question isn't "what qualifies under Act 60?" — it's "what skill can I develop that the global market will pay for, regardless of where I'm located?" Act 60 rewards skills that travel across borders. Your location is Puerto Rico. Your market is the world.

Every skill on this list is explicitly eligible under Act 60 Chapter 3 Export Services. Source: Act 60-2019, Section 2031.01; ACT60_QUICK_REFERENCE, Eligible Activities.

2

Layer 2: Structure It as a Business, Not a Job

The entity — how you capture the tax benefit

Act 60 Export Services benefits flow to a business entity, not to an individual employee. This means the transition from "freelancer" or "employee" to "LLC owner" is not just a legal technicality — it's what unlocks the 4% rate. You form a Puerto Rico LLC, apply for the Act 60 decree through DDEC's Single Business Portal, and operate your service business from a real Puerto Rico office (which can be a home office or coworking space, provided it's legitimate and documented).

For businesses with revenue under $3 million — which describes the vast majority of independent professionals and small agencies — the corporate income tax rate starts at 2% for the first five years, then moves to 4%. Dividend distributions to shareholders from that exempt income are 0%. You also benefit from 75%–100% property tax exemptions and 50%–100% municipal tax exemptions depending on your business volume. Source: Act 60-2019, Chapter 3, Sections 2031.01 et seq.; BONN LLC Act 60 Guide.

3

Layer 3: Build Your Client Base Outside Puerto Rico

The market — where the money comes from

The one non-negotiable requirement of Act 60 Export Services is that your clients must be located outside Puerto Rico. Your services cannot have a direct economic nexus with the Puerto Rico market. This is the rule, and it's also the opportunity: you're not competing for a piece of Puerto Rico's economy. You're competing for a piece of the entire world's economy — and doing it from here.

The U.S. mainland is the most obvious market. A Puerto Rican software developer, marketing consultant, or financial analyst serving U.S. clients is not doing anything exotic — they're just doing it strategically. The bilingual, bicultural advantage that Puerto Ricans already possess is a genuine competitive edge in the U.S. market, particularly in industries where Spanish-language capability commands a premium. Source: Act 60-2019, Section 2031.01 — export services must serve clients outside Puerto Rico with no nexus to local Puerto Rican economic activities.

Careers That Qualify: What the Law Actually Rewards

The following careers are explicitly listed as eligible export service activities under Act 60-2019, Chapter 3, Section 2031.01. Each one is a real, learnable, high-demand profession that can be built from Puerto Rico and sold to the world.

Technology

  • Software development — web, mobile, enterprise applications for U.S. or global clients
  • SaaS products — build and sell software-as-a-service to businesses outside PR
  • Cybersecurity consulting — increasingly in demand across every industry
  • Data analytics & AI/ML — one of the fastest-growing skill categories globally
  • App and game development — mobile, desktop, gaming studios
  • Blockchain development — smart contracts, DeFi infrastructure, Web3

Creative Industries

  • Graphic design & branding — serving U.S. brands and agencies remotely
  • Digital content creation — video production, animation, media for external markets
  • Music production & publishing — producing, licensing, distributing globally
  • Advertising & creative agencies — campaigns for clients headquartered outside PR
  • Educational content & e-learning — courses, training, curriculum for external students
  • Publishing — books, media, digital publications distributed globally

Business & Professional Services

  • Management consulting — strategy, operations, organizational development for U.S. companies
  • Accounting & tax preparation — serving mainland U.S. individuals and businesses
  • Financial advisory — investment management, fund administration for offshore clients
  • Digital marketing & SEO — serving brands and agencies across the United States
  • PR & communications — media relations, public affairs for external organizations
  • Insurance services — broking, underwriting support for external markets

Engineering, Science & Health

  • Engineering & architecture — blueprints, designs, consulting for projects outside PR
  • R&D services — research and development work for external clients or corporations
  • Scientific & laboratory services — testing, analysis, technical consulting
  • Telemedicine — medical consultations for patients physically located outside PR
  • Legal support services — research, document review, paralegal work for mainland law firms
  • Risk management consulting — compliance, audit, enterprise risk for external clients

The One Rule That Cannot Be Broken

Every service on this list qualifies only if your clients are located outside Puerto Rico and your work has no direct nexus to economic activity in the Puerto Rico market. You can serve U.S. companies, European clients, Latin American businesses — but not Puerto Rico-based clients. If you serve both, only the revenue from external clients counts toward your exempt income. Source: Act 60-2019, Section 2031.01.

The Bigger Picture: This Is What the Law Was Built For

Act 60 gets covered almost exclusively as a tool for wealthy mainlanders — a tax strategy for crypto investors and hedge fund managers who want to reduce their capital gains. And yes, that's a legitimate use of the law. But it's not the whole story.

Chapter 3 — Export Services — was written with a different population in mind. It was designed to give Puerto Rico a tax-competitive reason to become a global service export center. That means Puerto Ricans running businesses here, employing people here, paying taxes here, and keeping wealth here. At 4% (or 2% to start), the island's corporate rate is competitive with any jurisdiction on earth for the type of knowledge work the global economy is demanding.

The Question Worth Asking

If you are a young professional in Puerto Rico, or a student about to graduate, ask yourself: What skills am I building, and who will pay for them?

If the honest answer is "the local Puerto Rico economy," you are competing for a piece of a small pie, and the data shows what that looks like over time.

But if you build skills that the U.S. and global economy will pay for — and you structure your business correctly under Act 60 — you are competing for a piece of the largest economy in the world, from one of the most beautiful places on it, at one of the lowest corporate tax rates anywhere.

The brain drain that has cost Puerto Rico hundreds of thousands of people over the past two decades was not inevitable. It was a product of a structural mismatch between the skills the island produces and the economic opportunities available on the island. Act 60 Export Services changes that equation — not completely, not painlessly, but meaningfully.

The shift is simple: stop thinking of the Puerto Rico economy as your market, and start thinking of it as your home base. Your market is the world. Your home is here.

How to Start: A Practical Roadmap

1

Build the Skill First

Don't form the LLC before you have the skill and the clients. Build your expertise, prove you can deliver value to clients outside Puerto Rico, and build a track record. The decree application is far easier when you have real revenue to point to.

2

Form a Puerto Rico LLC

File a Certificate of Formation with the Puerto Rico Department of State ($250), register with SURI (free), and establish a legitimate bona fide office in Puerto Rico. This doesn't require a commercial lease — a documented home office can qualify, but it must be real and consistently used for business. Source: Act 60-2019, Chapter 3; 14 L.P.R.A. §3951 et seq.

3

Apply for the Act 60 Chapter 3 Decree

Submit your application through DDEC's Single Business Portal at incentivos.ddec.pr.gov. The application requires a business plan, description of eligible services, and client information. Professional CPA and attorney assistance is strongly recommended — the decree is a binding 15-year contract with the government. Source: DDEC Single Business Portal; Act 60-2019, Chapter 3.

4

Maintain Compliance Every Year

File your DDEC annual report, maintain CPA-audited financial statements if required, and document that your services are being performed in Puerto Rico for clients outside Puerto Rico. The decree is a contract — honor it. If your revenue exceeds $3 million, you'll also need at least one full-time Puerto Rico resident employee. Source: Act 60-2019, Chapter 3; DDEC Annual Reporting Requirements.

5

Explore Chapter 10 If You're Under 35

If you're between 16 and 35 years old, Act 60 Chapter 10 (Young Entrepreneurs) offers 0% income tax for three years on the first $500,000 of net income annually, plus property and municipal tax exemptions. It cannot be combined with Chapter 3, but it's worth evaluating at the start of your career. Source: Act 60-2019, Section 2100.01.
